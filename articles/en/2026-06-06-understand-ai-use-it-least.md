# The People Who Understand AI Best Use It Least

> 发布日期:2026-06-06 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-06-最懂AI的人反而最舍得不用它.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-06-understand-ai-use-it-least.md)

---

Last week I spent more than two hours talking with an AI whistleblower — an investigative journalist who was among the first to get inside OpenAI and write the real story of its boardroom war, and, as it happens, an MIT alum who knows the technology cold.

The thing that floored me had nothing to do with how much he knows. It was this: he barely uses AI.

I made him say it twice. We've all absorbed the same reflex by now — *use AI as fast as you can, or get left behind.* So when someone who understands the technology this well tells you he mostly stays away from it, your first instinct is to assume he's being a contrarian. He isn't. He's just answering a different question than the rest of us.

Most of us ask: *what can I do with AI?* He asks a second question first: *what should I not hand to AI?*

## His security doesn't come from the tool

He's not anti-technology. He stays away from these tools because he is unusually clear about where his real value sits.

His job is not to repackage information that's already online into something tidier. His job is to walk into the real world, find real people, earn their trust, and hear stories that were never written down anywhere. AI — however powerful — can only work with what has already been recorded. But the things that actually matter, like the truth of an internal power struggle or the ambition hiding behind the capital, don't post themselves to the internet. Someone has to go dig them out.

That's where his sense of safety comes from. He knows exactly which of his abilities no model can replace. And once you've located that for yourself, the panic — *I have to use AI for everything or I'm done* — quietly loses its grip.

## A simple line for what to hand over and what to keep

Here's the most useful frame I've collected on this, and it's almost embarrassingly simple. Sort your work into two piles.

The first pile is **utility tasks** — things you want *done*, not things you want to be good at. Formatting a document, drafting boilerplate, summarizing a report you'll never look at again. Use AI all you want here. The whole point is to spend *less* of your life on these.

The second pile is **meaning tasks** — the things you actually want to become good at, the work you'd want to spend your time on even if no one paid you. Here, be very careful. The temptation is to use AI to skip the hard part. Don't. The struggle is not in the way of the skill; the struggle *is* the skill. Automate it away and you don't just save time — you quietly delete the thing that would have made you better.

The mistake isn't using AI. The mistake is using it on the wrong pile.

## The part of the bill you don't see

There's a cost to outsourcing that doesn't show up on the receipt. Psychologists call it *cognitive offloading*: the moment we expect an external tool to hold something for us, we encode less of it ourselves. Studies have found that when children expect to have outside help available, they remember less — and then perform worse on their own once the help is taken away.

Read that slowly, because it's the whole argument in one sentence. When you hand the remembering to the machine, you get efficiency, and you also lose a little of your own ability to remember. When you hand the figuring-out to the machine, you get an answer, and you also lose the workout that would have grown the muscle.

What AI lets you skip is sometimes just minutes. Sometimes it's your own growth. The receipt only ever shows you the minutes.

## So what's actually scarce

If the skill of "using AI" gets cheaper and easier every month — and it does — then it's a thin advantage to build a life on. So what's the thick one?

When I pushed the journalist on what the next generation will actually need, he gave me two things. The first was **adaptability**. Not the passive kind — not "learn to endure whatever happens." Real adaptability is closer to what systems thinkers mean by resilience: a system that never deviates from its set point is actually *fragile*; the resilient one is the one that can take a hit, wobble, and recover. Adaptability isn't standing perfectly still in the storm. It's learning to surf it.

The second was **learning how to learn** — and this one compounds. The more you learn, the more efficiently your brain wires up the next thing; skill begets skill, the way interest begets interest. It's a Matthew effect: the gap between people who keep learning and people who stopped doesn't stay constant, it widens. AI doesn't flatten that gap. It amplifies it — in *both* directions. Use it to learn faster and you pull ahead. Use it to avoid learning and you fall behind faster than you would have on your own.

Underneath both abilities is one quiet sentence he kept coming back to: *I don't know this yet, but I can learn it.* That sentence is the deepest security a person can carry. It doesn't depend on which tool is hot this year. It survives every tool going obsolete.

## The point was never to stay away from AI

He closed with a story I keep thinking about. A school in Hong Kong didn't hand its students a list of approved apps. Instead it had them debate — together — which technologies would actually support their learning and which might quietly harm it. The kids weren't passive recipients of whatever tool got pushed at them. They participated in the judgment: *what kind of learning environment do we want, and what relationship do we want with these tools?* In the end they chose, on their own, to back away from some of the obvious products and try healthier options.

That's the real work, and it isn't only about kids. The goal isn't to decide *for* a person which tools to use. It's to build the person's ability to decide — to know when to reach for the tool and when to keep yourself in the loop, to know that AI can help you learn but cannot learn *for* you.

So here's where I've landed. The first-class skill of this era is not how well you use AI. Everyone will be able to do that soon enough. The scarce thing is the judgment underneath it — knowing what to hand over and what to keep, and being honest with yourself about the difference.

The people who understand AI best aren't the ones who use it the most. They're the ones who've figured out, deliberately, where *not* to. Use AI to become stronger. Just don't use it to skip the work that would have made you strong.
