# AI Can Distill Your Skills. It Can't Distill Your System.

> 发布日期:2026-05-27
>
> 中文版:[AI 能蒸馏你的技能，蒸馏不了你的系统](../zh/2026-05-27-AI能蒸馏你的技能蒸馏不了你的系统.md)


*When output becomes free, the only moat left is the one you grew yourself — and most people are quietly dismantling theirs.*

## A Man With Intelligence But No System

On September 1, 1953, a neurosurgeon named William Beecher Scoville cut into the brain of a 27-year-old motor-winder named Henry Molaison and removed most of the hippocampus on both sides of his head.[1] Molaison had suffered intractable epilepsy for a decade. Scoville called the procedure "frankly experimental."[1] The seizures eased. Something else broke.

Molaison's IQ stayed intact. His reasoning stayed intact. He could hold a conversation, solve a puzzle, recall his own childhood. But he could no longer form new long-term memories. "If he met someone who then left the room, within minutes he had no recollection of the person or their meeting."[1] Every morning, for the next fifty-five years, was page one.

For the rest of his life — until his death in 2008 — over a hundred researchers studied him under the initials "H.M."[1] What they found rearranged the science of memory. But strip away the neuroscience and look at the human fact: here was a man with full intelligence and no way to grow it. Experience washed over him and left nothing behind. He could think. He could not accumulate.

He was, in the most literal sense, a man with intelligence but no system to grow it.

Hold that image, because it is the exact shape of a trap that AI sets — and that most people are walking into without noticing. When producing output becomes nearly free, the temptation is to produce more of it and skip the slow part: the part where experience hardens into judgment. Skip that part long enough and you keep your intelligence and lose your system. You stay, in your own way, on page one.

## Storage Is Not Transduction

The obvious objection arrives early, so let's deal with it early. "Fine," you say, "but H.M. lost his memories. I'm not losing anything — I'm storing more than ever. Every chat log, every note, every prompt is saved. Isn't that a memory system?"

No. And the reason is the most important distinction in this entire essay.

H.M. could retrieve old memories perfectly well. Ask him about the 1930s and he'd answer. What he lost was not storage and not retrieval. Brenda Milner's decades of study established that the hippocampus is not a storehouse at all — it is a *transducer*, the organ that converts short-term experience into long-term structure.[2] H.M.'s filing cabinet was intact. The machine that files new things into it was gone.

Storage is not transduction. A hard drive full of everything you have ever thought is a filing cabinet. It is not the thing that takes today's hard problem, wrestles it, and leaves you slightly different tomorrow. That integration — experience reshaping the structure that meets the next experience — is what H.M. lost, and it is precisely what no external database gives you back.

A filing cabinet is not a hippocampus.

This matters because the most common way people use AI is as a bigger, faster filing cabinet — retrieve, assemble, output — and they mistake the volume of what passes through for growth. It isn't growth. The pages turn. Nothing is filed.

## The Two Intelligences

In 1963, the psychologist Raymond Cattell split "intelligence" into two things that had been confused for one.[3]

The first he called **fluid intelligence** — Gf. It is raw reasoning: solving a novel puzzle, spotting a pattern you've never seen, holding several things in mind at once. It depends very little on what you already know. And it peaks early — somewhere in the early-to-mid twenties — then declines, gently at first, faster after sixty.[3]

The second he called **crystallized intelligence** — Gc. It is everything you've accumulated: knowledge, vocabulary, judgment, the thousand learned patterns that let an expert glance at a problem and know which corner to grab. Unlike Gf, it does not peak and fall. "Crystallized intelligence remains stable or even increases through middle adulthood."[3] Vocabulary keeps climbing into your seventies. Judgment compounds.

Here is the part that closes the loop with H.M., and it is not a metaphor — it is anatomy. Fluid intelligence runs largely on the prefrontal cortex. Crystallized intelligence is "a function of brain regions that involve the storage and usage of long-term memories, such as the hippocampus."[3]

The intelligence that grows with age lives in the organ H.M. lost. He kept his fluid reasoning and lost the seat of the crystallized kind. He could solve the puzzle in front of him forever — and never get wiser.

Keep both words. The rest of the argument is about which one AI touches.

## What AI Actually Distills

Think of a task you used to grind through — a first draft, a summary, a block of boilerplate, a research scan. Last year it cost you an afternoon. Now it costs you ninety seconds and a prompt.

What just happened? AI distilled a skill. It took a *copyable* capability — a Gf-like act of reasoning, drafting, pattern-matching — and made it cheap. This is real, and it is genuinely useful. AI is extraordinarily good at exactly the kind of work Gf names: novel-looking problems with learnable structure.

But notice what did *not* happen. You did not get wiser about the thing. You got the output without the wrestling that, last year, left a deposit in your Gc. The afternoon you used to spend was not pure waste — it was the transduction step, the slow filing of experience into judgment. Buy it back with a prompt and you keep the output and lose the deposit.

Skills are rentable. The system that grows them is not.

This is why the people pulling ahead don't treat AI as a vending machine. Garry Tan, who runs Y Combinator, frames the sharp move as compounding rather than single-shot: do a task manually once, have AI package it into a reusable skill, then let it snowball.[4] The first group asks AI to *do the thing*. The second group asks AI to *build the system that does the thing, and to teach them while it builds*. One rents. The other accumulates.

The verb in the title is exact. AI can distill your skills — boil them down, bottle them, hand them to anyone. It cannot distill your system, because your system is not a skill. It's the hippocampus you spent years growing.

## The Word of 2025

You are not imagining the unease. In 2025 the culture finally named it.

Merriam-Webster made "slop" its Word of the Year, defining it as "digital content of low quality that is produced usually in quantity by means of artificial intelligence."[5] The Economist and the American Dialect Society chose the same word. Macquarie Dictionary went further and crowned "AI slop" specifically — "low-quality content created by generative AI, often containing errors, and not requested by the user" — winning its people's-choice vote too.[5]

A word becomes Word of the Year when a society suddenly needs it. "Slop" arrived because the gap between AI-*assisted* and AI-*hollowed* finally became visible enough to name. The volume of output went vertical; the average quality of judgment behind it did not.

And most of us are closer to the slop end than we'd like to admit. By one common estimate, the large majority of AI users stay locked in a mechanical loop: prompt, accept, publish. Prompt, accept, publish. No step in that loop is a transduction step. Nothing gets filed. It is page one, repeated at scale, with better production values.

The word arrived because the gap between assisted and hollowed finally became visible.

## r Greater Than g

The reigning belief about AI is that it levels the playing field — that a tool this powerful, handed to everyone, must close gaps. It is the great equalizer.

The evidence runs the other way.

Thomas Piketty spent six hundred pages of *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* on a single inequality: **r > g**. The return on capital, historically, outpaces the growth of wages and the economy.[6] So whoever owns compounding assets pulls away from whoever earns a wage, not because they're smarter, but because the math of ownership beats the math of labor. Capital outruns work. The gap widens on its own.

AI does the same thing to the gap between people who own a system and people who rent skills. Hand a powerful tool to someone with a deep Gc — taste, judgment, an audience, the instinct for which corner to grab — and they get a multiplier on an already-large base. Hand the same tool to someone running the prompt-accept-publish loop, and they get faster slop. Same tool. Opposite trajectories. The tool didn't equalize; it amplified whatever was already there.

Capital outruns wages; systems outrun skills. AI just turned up the multiplier.

This is the uncomfortable part. If you have been using AI substitutively — to skip the wrestling rather than to accelerate your own growth — the tool has not been helping you catch up. It has been helping the people ahead of you pull further away, while handing you the feeling of productivity as consolation.

## Three Things It Cannot Distill

So what survives? What can't be boiled down, bottled, and handed to anyone with a prompt?

Three things. Call them moats.

The first is **taste** — judgment under abundance. When producing options is free, the scarce act is choosing. Steve Jobs wore the same black turtleneck every day, hundreds of identical ones made by Issey Miyake, precisely so he would never spend judgment on what to wear and could spend all of it on what to ship.[7] Taste is not knowing more options; it is knowing which to kill. AI floods you with options. It cannot tell you which one is *yours*. That discrimination was grown, decision by decision, exactly the way Gc is grown — and it lives nowhere a model can reach.

The second is **distribution** — the audience and trust you built over time. A model can write you a thousand posts. It cannot make ten thousand people care what you, specifically, think. Trust is a deposit account with a very slow interest rate and no shortcut. It compounds only if you keep depositing.

The third is **high agency** — the drive to act in the first place. AI did not generate your motivation, and it cannot sustain it. It will answer any question you bring; it will not decide that the question is worth asking. The blank page is still yours to walk up to. Agency is the one input no tool supplies, and the one without which the other two never get built.

Taste, distribution, agency — each one is a deposit account, not a skill you can download.

## But AI Remembers Everything I Think

Here is the strongest objection, and it deserves to be stated at full strength, not as a strawman.

"You're behind the times. The newest tools *do* have memory. They remember my projects, my preferences, my past conversations, my entire corpus. That is a system — an external, ever-growing, perfectly-recalled hippocampus that never sleeps and never forgets. Isn't *that* the compounding system you say AI can't be?"

It's the best objection in the room. And the answer is the distinction from §2, plus one more thing.

First: perfect recall is still storage, not transduction. A model that has read everything you've written can *retrieve* it brilliantly. But retrieval is the function H.M. kept. What grows a person is integration — yesterday's struggle reshaping the structure that meets tomorrow's problem — and that happens in *you*, or it doesn't happen. An external memory that recalls your past thoughts perfectly, while you yourself stop doing the integrating, is H.M.'s filing cabinet with a faster index. The cabinet got better. The transducer is still gone.

Second, and stranger: a real memory system has to *forget on purpose*. In 1983, Francis Crick and Graeme Mitchison proposed in *Nature* that the function of dreaming is to *un*-learn — that "we dream in order to forget," running a reverse-learning pass to weaken spurious connections so the useful ones stand out.[8] Forgetting is not a failure of the system; it is the system working. A model tuned to accumulate everything, indiscriminately, is not a superior memory. It's a hoard. Without the pruning step, more is just more.

Crick's insight: a system has to forget on purpose. AI that only accumulates is not a memory — it's a hoard.

The concession is real: external memory tools *are* useful, and used well they are the best pruning instrument ever built — they can hold the noise so your own Gc fills only with signal. That is additive use, and it's the good kind. But the tool holding your memories is not the same as you growing. The cabinet is not the hippocampus, no matter how good the index gets.

## The Second Half of the Chessboard

Put one grain of wheat on the first square of a chessboard, two on the second, four on the third, and keep doubling. By the sixty-fourth square the board holds 2⁶⁴ − 1 grains — **18,446,744,073,709,551,615** of them, roughly 1.8 × 10¹⁹, more than a thousand years of global wheat harvests.[9] The whole second half of the board dwarfs the whole first half: the sixty-fourth square alone carries more than two billion times everything on the first thirty-two squares combined.[9]

That is what compounding looks like, and it is why the cost of substitutive AI use is so easy to underestimate. Compounding is back-loaded. The gap looks small for a long time and then, all at once, it doesn't.

Warren Buffett is the cleanest illustration. His investment returns compounded at roughly 20% a year for decades — impressive, but not magical. The magical part is *when* the wealth showed up: he earned about 99% of his net worth after the age of fifty, and didn't become a billionaire until fifty-six.[10] "The biggest thing about making money is time," he said. "You don't have to be particularly smart, you just have to be patient."[10] The 20% was steady the whole way. The fortune was almost entirely in the back half of the board.

On an exponential curve, the day you fall behind is not the day you notice — it's decades earlier.

The limit of the argument deserves naming, plainly. None of this is a controlled study. It is one observer's claim, built on a chain of analogies — a man's brain in 1953, a psychologist's 1963 distinction, a chessboard, an investor. Analogies illuminate; they don't prove. What would refute the thesis? If, five years from now, the people who used AI most substitutively — pure prompt-accept-publish, no system-building — turn out to have grown their taste, judgment, and distribution just as much as the people who used it to accelerate their own work, then the distinction I'm drawing is false, and you should drop it. I don't think that's what we'll find. But that is the falsifier, and it's fair to hold me to it.

## Additive, Not Substitutive

Go back to page one.

H.M. didn't lose his intelligence. He lost the machine that turns experience into growth, and so every day arrived as new and left as nothing. The fear that AI will make us stupid is the wrong fear. AI won't take your fluid intelligence. The risk is subtler and worse: that you'll keep your intelligence and quietly stop accumulating, output rising while your system flatlines — H.M. with great production values.

But notice that this was never a story about whether to use AI. The dividing line was never "uses AI" versus "doesn't." It is **additive** versus **substitutive**.

Additive: you use AI to grow your own system faster. New surfaces to explore, faster feedback loops, more experiments per week, the noise pruned away so your Gc fills only with signal. The wrestling stays yours; the tool just gets you to more of it. This is Crick's dreaming brain, doing the forgetting on purpose so the learning lands.

Substitutive: you use AI to skip the wrestling. You borrow its judgment instead of building your own, accept its output instead of integrating the experience, and mistake the turning pages for growth. This is Scoville's scalpel — except you're holding it, and the hippocampus it's removing is your own.

Same tool. The whole difference is in the verb you ask it to perform: *grow me*, or *replace me*.

Used one way, AI is the best pruning tool you've ever owned. Used the other way, it's the surgeon — and you're H.M.

Page one. Or not.

## In Short

In 1953 a surgeon removed Henry Molaison's hippocampus. He kept his intelligence and lost the ability to grow it — every morning was page one. That is the trap AI sets when it makes output free: you can keep getting smarter-seeming output while quietly losing the system that makes *you* better.

There are two kinds of intelligence. Fluid (reasoning, peaks young, copyable) and crystallized (accumulated judgment, grows for decades). AI is great at the first and cannot build the second for you — and the second, it turns out, lives in the very part of the brain Molaison lost.

Storing everything in AI is not a system. Storage is not the same as growth; a filing cabinet is not a hippocampus. A real system even forgets on purpose, the way Crick said we dream in order to forget.

AI did not level the field. Like capital outrunning wages, it amplified the gap between people who own a compounding system and people who rent depreciating skills. And compounding is back-loaded — Buffett earned 99% of his wealth after fifty — so the day you fall behind is decades before you feel it.

Three things AI can't distill: taste, distribution, agency. Each is a deposit account, not a download.

The line was never "use AI or don't." It's additive versus substitutive. Before you accept the next thing AI hands you, ask one question: does taking this grow my own judgment, or does it just borrow someone else's? Do it the first way and AI is the best tool you've ever had. Do it the second way long enough, and you're H.M. — intelligent, productive, and stuck on page one.
