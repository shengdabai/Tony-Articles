# The Things That Get Expensive Are the Things That Are You

> 发布日期 · Published: 2026-05-30 · [中文](../zh/2026-05-30-越来越贵的东西恰恰就是你.md) | [English](../en/2026-05-30-the-things-that-get-expensive.md)

---

A friend who had lived in the US for years came back to China last month and said the thing every returnee says: "It's so *convenient* here." Order food, hail a courier, call a plumber — tap your phone, and within half an hour the world rearranges itself around you. And it barely costs anything.

His instinct was to read this as a verdict: China wins, America is overrated, who's the developed country now?

But there's a colder way to do the math. Don't stand where the consumer stands. Stand where the delivery rider stands.

In China, a rider earns roughly 5 to 7 yuan per order, and a good phone costs about 7,000 yuan — so he has to run a thousand orders to afford it. In the US, a rider clears 7 to 10 dollars per order in fees and tips, and the phone costs 1,000 dollars — a hundred-odd orders and it's his. If your job is "deliver food until you can buy a phone," America is the easier place to do it.

Your convenience doesn't only depend on how much *you* earn. It depends on how cheap *other people's time* is.

And here's the part that should stop you: this isn't a culture problem, or a policy problem, or a who-works-harder problem. American services are expensive *by destiny*, and China is heading the same way. Every society that grows rich follows the same arc — **things get cheaper, people get more expensive.** So much so that your lived experience becomes: the richer everyone around me gets, the *less* convenient my life feels.

## The chart that split the modern world in two

The economist Mark Perry made a famous graph he updates every year — people call it the "Chart of the Century." It tracks the price of American goods and services from 2000 to 2025, and one glance tells you modern life has been cleaved down the middle.

On one side, things collapse in price. Televisions, adjusted for quality, fell by something like 98%. Software, toys, phone service — all way down. Clothing barely moved. New cars rose 24.7%, far below the roughly 90% of overall inflation.

On the other side, services soar. Hospital services up 271%. College tuition up 194%. Textbooks up 180%. Childcare up 152%.

Material abundance and crushing burden, at the same time, in the same economy. It looks like a contradiction. It isn't. It's a very hard law of modern economics, and it has a name: **Baumol's cost disease.**

## Why the cellist can never get faster

William Baumol and William Bowen named it in 1965, and their first clue came from the performing arts. Take the standard example: a Beethoven string quartet.

When it premiered in 1828, it took four professional musicians forty minutes to play. Fast-forward two centuries to 2026, and the same piece *still* takes four people forty minutes. You cannot mechanize it. There is no productivity gain available. Four people, forty minutes, forever.

Meanwhile, the world outside the concert hall got staggeringly more productive. A single factory worker today, armed with machines and software and a global supply chain, produces vastly more than his grandfather did. He creates more wealth, so his wages rise — and the goods he makes get cheaper. Everybody wins.

So here's the paradox: what about the four musicians? Their productivity hasn't budged. By the logic of the factory, they shouldn't get a raise. But if you don't raise their pay, they quit and become something else. So to keep them on the stage, you have to pay them more anyway — for producing exactly what they produced in 1828.

Baumol split the economy in two. The **progressive sector** — manufacturing, software, logistics — where productivity climbs, unit costs fall, wages rise. And the **stagnant sector** — art, medicine, education, haircuts — where productivity can barely move, but wages have to rise along with everyone else's, or the people leave.

The result is inescapable: the relative cost of the stagnant sector — which is mostly *wages* — rises without limit. That's why a doctor's hour, a teacher's hour, a nurse's hour keeps getting more expensive while your TV keeps getting cheaper.

## What scarcity moved to

The first thing this does to a society is change how people think about repair. We used to mend torn clothes and fix broken appliances. Now we throw them out and buy new — and that isn't waste, it's thrift. A factory stamps out a new car door for almost nothing; a body-shop worker hammering your old one flat by hand costs a fortune. So the insurance company writes you a check and scraps the car.

The second thing is darker: healthcare and education become budget black holes. The US spent about 5.3 trillion dollars on health in 2024 — 18% of GDP. And don't assume China is cheap: its total health spending hit 9.09 trillion yuan, 6.7% of GDP, and climbing fast. There's rent-seeking in there, and inefficiency, and the simple fact that richer people care more about their health. But the deepest cause is the cost disease. A doctor's time examining you, a teacher's care reading your essay — you cannot make those 100 times more productive with a machine.

So here is what I want you to sit with. **Modernity did not abolish scarcity. It moved scarcity from things to people.**

The poor of the past couldn't afford a decent coat but could easily find someone to run an errand. The poor of today can buy a sharp-looking jacket, a smartphone, a giant high-definition TV — and cannot afford a good doctor, a good school, a good lawyer, good care for their parents. Your *sense* of being rich, to a large degree, comes down to how much of other people's time you can summon.

## Can AI cure the disease?

The obvious hope: if a robot can do the service, you've finally made the stagnant sector scalable. Things are cheap because you can leverage them — design once, sell a million times. People are expensive because they can't be copied. So copy the people. Cure the disease.

To a point, yes. A huge share of what we pay humans for is really just *information* — a cooking procedure, a contract draft, a customer-service answer, a first-pass medical diagnosis. Machines can do these well, sometimes better than we can.

But some services need a human in the room, and no amount of intelligence changes that. The AI's diagnosis can be flawless — but who weighs the trade-offs with the frightened patient, who tells them it'll be okay, who *signs their name* and carries the risk of being wrong? AI will explain the same problem patiently a hundred times — but you need a human teacher to look a kid in the eye and say, "Stop pretending. You just didn't study." One caregiver tending one elder is care. One caregiver tending a hundred elders through machines is *animal husbandry*.

This is the twist that matters: the cost disease doesn't make human time obsolete. **It makes real human time more precious than ever.** As AI strips away everything copyable, compressible, automatable, what's left standing is exactly the part that was always you — responsibility, trust, judgment, presence, taste, the weighing of one wrong against another.

The things that get expensive are the things that are *you*.

## So what do you do with this?

A warning first: high cost is not high profit. Healthcare is expensive, but doctors aren't getting rich off the disease; education is expensive, but nobody got wealthy by becoming a professor. The cost disease says stagnant-sector wages get *dragged up* by the progressive sector — it never promised anyone outsized profit. What it gives you is a floor: as long as the service is genuinely needed, your income won't collapse. That's defense, not offense. But on top of that floor, you can build.

The best move is to attach yourself to something copyable, to find leverage that lets your skill multiply. A designer shouldn't only sell design-hours — get the design into production. A teacher shouldn't only give a lecture — turn the course, the question bank, the feedback loop into a product. A doctor shouldn't only see patients — turn the protocol, the follow-up, the patient education into an organization. **Separate judgment from execution: that separation is your lever.**

The second move is to become the one who signs. Let AI generate the diagnosis, the contract, the blueprint — then you glance at it, you stamp it, you take the responsibility. Make a few small decisions that fit reality, and that signature is worth a fortune.

The third is to build the platform underneath. Before AI, most human service-time wasn't spent serving — it went to forms, client-hunting, compliance, records, billing. Automate that friction away and you've earned your cut.

And if you insist on one-to-one, go the luxury route. You're no longer selling time and skill — you're selling identity, trust, an effect above the industry's ceiling. Your service might be 20% better than the AI's. Someone will gladly pay 200% more.

## A society that refused to ride

There were times in Chinese history that refused to use people as beasts of burden. The Song dynasty records that scholar-officials like Wang Anshi, Sima Guang, and Cheng Yi rode donkeys and horses into old age and flatly refused the sedan chair — refused to be *carried* by other men. Even in the mountains, on roads too dangerous to walk, urged to take the chair, they'd lean on a staff and walk instead. This wasn't a pose. Wang Anshi's reason was four characters: *未尝敢以人代畜* — "I have never dared to use a man in place of a beast."

It was only later, in the Southern Song, that the rain-slick roads of the Jiangnan finally won the officials their chairs.

Wang Anshi and his peers would look at the cost disease — at the relentless, unstoppable rise in the price of a human hour — and they would not call it a sickness.

They'd call it a society finally learning that people should be expensive.

And in the age of AI, that is the most hopeful sentence I know. The machine takes the copyable parts. What it can't take — what it makes *dearer* with every passing year — is the part of the work that was always, irreducibly, you.
