# The Last Mile Makes AI Real

> 发布日期:2026-07-10 · [中文](../zh/2026-07-10-最后一公里让 AI 变成真的.md) | [English](../en/2026-07-10-last-mile-makes-ai-real.md)

---

Today’s notes kept pulling me away from the usual AI question.

The usual question is: which model is strongest?

It is a natural question. Benchmarks are easy to compare. Demos are easy to share. A new model release gives everyone a clean object to admire, argue about, and rank.

But the more interesting signal is appearing somewhere less glamorous: in deployment.

Microsoft recently announced a $2.5 billion investment in a new Frontier Company, with 6,000 industry and engineering experts embedded with customers to co-design and improve AI systems at scale.[^1] AWS announced a $1 billion Forward Deployed Engineering organization to embed thousands of engineers with customers building agentic AI systems.[^2] OpenAI launched the OpenAI Deployment Company and agreed to acquire Tomoro, bringing about 150 forward deployed engineers and deployment specialists into that effort.[^3]

These are not small side projects.

They are a message from the market: the frontier is no longer only at the model lab. The frontier has moved into the messy place where a model touches old databases, human incentives, compliance rules, broken workflows, nervous managers, unclear goals, and customers who do not speak in clean prompts.

That place has a name.

The last mile.

And the last mile is where AI becomes real.

## A Demo Is Not a System

AI demos are seductive because they remove context.

A demo usually starts with a clean input, a clear task, a cooperative environment, and a person who already knows what success should look like. The model writes a reply. The agent calls a tool. The voice assistant answers a question. The spreadsheet updates. Everyone nods.

That is useful, but it is not the same thing as a working system.

A working system has to survive the rest of the world.

It has to know which data it is allowed to see. It has to recover when the user says the wrong thing. It has to handle an exception without exposing private information. It has to fit into software that was built years before anyone expected an AI agent to use it. It has to explain enough of itself that a team can trust it, debug it, and improve it.

Most important, it has to connect to a real human purpose.

This is where the fantasy of “just give everyone a powerful model” breaks down. A powerful model is not a finished capability. It is closer to a powerful engine sitting on the floor. Someone still has to build the vehicle, choose the road, define the dashboard, write the rules of use, and decide what kind of accident is unacceptable.

That work is not secondary.

That work is the product.

## The New Scarcity Is Translation

Palantir has used forward deployed software engineers for years. Its public description is simple: these engineers embed with customers and configure software to solve hard problems for one customer, rather than building one generic capability for many customers.[^4]

That distinction matters.

The person in the last mile is not merely “implementing requirements.” In many real settings, the requirements are the problem. The customer may know the pain but not the structure. The operator may know the workaround but not the abstraction. The manager may know the metric but not the hidden behavior behind it. The engineer may know the API but not the business consequence of a wrong answer.

So the scarce work becomes translation.

Not translation between Chinese and English.

Translation between worlds.

Between a messy operation and a clean workflow.

Between a human complaint and a machine-readable specification.

Between “this takes too long” and “this decision depends on three fields that live in different systems.”

Between “we need AI” and “we need a safer way to decide which cases deserve human attention first.”

This kind of translation cannot be solved by a prompt alone, because the prompt is downstream of understanding. If the real problem has not been found, a better prompt only makes the wrong answer arrive faster.

That is why the last mile belongs to people who can move between layers. They can sit with the user, inspect the workflow, talk to the engineer, notice the incentive, respect the constraint, and then turn all of that into a system that can be tested.

This is not a soft skill replacing hard skill.

It is hard skill finally touching reality.

## AI Does Not Remove People. It Moves the Human Job

There is a lazy way to talk about AI adoption: machines replace people.

Sometimes they do replace tasks. Sometimes they reduce headcount. It would be dishonest to pretend otherwise.

But the more useful frame is this: AI moves the human job.

When generation was expensive, the human job was often to produce the first version. Write the draft. Prepare the report. Search the database. Translate the document. Make the slide. Summarize the call.

When generation becomes cheap, the human job moves upward and outward.

Upward, toward judgment: What is worth making? What is correct enough? What cannot be allowed to fail? What evidence would change our mind?

Outward, toward context: Who will use this? What are they afraid of? Which part of the workflow is really about people and accountability? Where is the trust boundary? Which legacy habit will break the beautiful design?

This is why today’s deployment signal connects to another note about work. McKinsey reported that employee use of AI at work rose from 30 percent in 2023 to 76 percent in 2025.[^5] The tools moved quickly into ordinary work. But the same discussion also points to something that has not changed as quickly: people still want meaningful work, growth, recognition, trust, and competent leadership.

That should not surprise us.

Tools change the surface of work. They do not erase the human need to know why the work matters, how to get better, and whether the system is fair.

In fact, the more AI enters the workflow, the more these human questions become operational questions. If nobody knows who owns a decision, the agent will amplify confusion. If nobody knows what good looks like, the dashboard will decorate uncertainty. If nobody trusts the process, automation will feel like disappearance, even when it is technically correct.

So the person who makes AI land is not just a coder.

That person is a designer of trust.

## The Same Pattern Appears in Learning

This is not only an enterprise story.

The same last-mile problem appears in education and personal growth.

OpenAI’s Study Mode is interesting not because it can answer questions. ChatGPT could already answer questions. The interesting part is that the mode is designed to guide with Socratic questions, hints, and self-reflection prompts instead of simply giving answers outright.[^6]

That is a last-mile design choice.

The easy version of AI learning is answer delivery. Ask a question, get the answer, feel relieved, move on.

The stronger version is friction design. The tool slows you down at the right moment. It asks what you already know. It asks where you got stuck. It gives a hint instead of a shortcut. It checks whether you can explain the idea back.

The model may contain knowledge, but learning still has to happen inside a person.

That gap is the last mile.

No tutor, human or artificial, can cross it for you. A good tutor can make the path visible. A bad tutor can make you feel productive while stealing the struggle that would have made you stronger.

This is one reason I dislike the phrase “AI saves time” when it is used too casually.

Saved time for what?

If AI saves you twenty minutes and you spend those twenty minutes avoiding the part where you think, practice, decide, or ship, nothing was saved. The cost was only hidden.

But if AI removes low-value friction so you can spend more time on the real last mile, then the tool is doing its job.

Use AI to get to the hard part faster.

Do not use AI to pretend the hard part disappeared.

## Ordinary People Need Deployment Ability Too

It is easy to hear “forward deployed engineering” and think this belongs only to large technology companies.

I do not think so.

Most people now need a small version of deployment ability.

A writer needs it when turning a vague idea into a piece that a real reader can follow.

A teacher needs it when turning abundant information into a learning experience that changes what a student can do.

A founder needs it when turning a demo into something a customer will pay for, use again, and trust with a real workflow.

A freelancer needs it when turning a tool stack into a repeatable service instead of a pile of clever screenshots.

A learner needs it when turning notes, videos, and AI explanations into a skill that survives without the tab open.

The pattern is the same:

- find the real problem;
- define what success means;
- connect tools to context;
- test against reality;
- keep the feedback loop alive.

This is deployment.

Not in the narrow software sense.

In the human sense.

It is the ability to make an idea land in the world without losing its shape.

## The Zeroth Class Works in the Last Mile

I keep coming back to the idea of the zeroth class.

The zeroth class is not made of people who use AI the most. It is made of people who use AI in a way that makes them more capable.

They are not satisfied with receiving output. They ask where the output must land. They do not worship the model. They build the bridge between the model and the situation. They do not confuse fluency with completion. They ask what would make the result useful, trusted, repeatable, and alive.

This is less glamorous than chasing the newest model.

It is also more durable.

Models will keep improving. Interfaces will become easier. More people will gain access to powerful tools. The visible advantage of being early will fade.

But the last mile will not disappear.

Reality does not become clean just because intelligence became cheap.

People still have unclear goals. Organizations still have hidden incentives. Students still need to struggle. Customers still need trust. Systems still fail in ways the demo did not show. Someone still has to decide what should happen when the machine is uncertain.

That someone should be stronger because of AI, not weaker.

So maybe the real question is not “Will AI replace me?”

A better question is:

Where is the last mile in my work?

Where does the beautiful answer become a real change?

Where does the model stop and responsibility begin?

If you can find that place, do not run away from it.

Move toward it.

That is where the leverage is moving.

That is where your judgment becomes visible.

That is where AI stops being a performance and starts becoming a capability.

The model can generate.

The agent can loop.

The demo can impress.

But the last mile still asks for a person who can make things real.
