# Friction Is Food

> 发布日期:2026-06-26 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-26-阻力是营养.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-26-friction-is-food.md)

---

The easiest mistake in the AI age is to think the enemy is difficulty.

We look at a task, see the friction, and immediately ask how to remove it. Can the model summarize this? Can it write the first draft? Can it make the slides? Can it generate the image? Can it automate the whole loop while I sleep?

Often, yes. And often, that is good.

But today's notes kept pushing me toward a more uncomfortable question:

What if some difficulty is not the thing blocking growth, but the thing feeding it?

Not all friction is noble. Nobody becomes wiser by renaming files by hand, fighting broken formats, copying data between forms, or doing repetitive cleanup that a machine can do better. That kind of friction is waste. Remove it.

But there is another kind of friction: the moment when you have to choose, explain, test, compare, revise, wait, or hold an unfinished thought long enough for it to become yours.

That friction is not waste.

That friction is food.

## The Strange Danger of Perfect Convenience

One old experiment keeps returning to public imagination whenever people talk about abundance. In 1968, researcher John B. Calhoun placed four pairs of mice into what later became known as Universe 25: an enclosed environment with food, water, nesting places, and protection from predators.[^1]

The colony grew quickly. In Calhoun's published account, the population reached about 620 mice by day 315 and peaked around 2,200 by day 560. After that, social behavior collapsed. The last birth occurred around day 600, and the colony moved toward extinction.[^2]

This experiment is often overused as a crude warning about population density. That is too simple, and we should be careful about turning mouse behavior into a direct prophecy for human society. Humans are not mice. Cities are not cages. Culture matters. Institutions matter. Meaning matters.

Still, the image is hard to forget: a world where the basic material problems were solved, yet the creatures inside did not become more alive. They became less capable of forming the social patterns that sustained life.

That is the part worth carrying into the AI age.

AI promises a kind of local abundance. More drafts. More answers. More images. More code. More summaries. More plans. More surface-level production than one person could have created alone.

The risk is not that abundance itself is bad.

The risk is that abundance can make the wrong things disappear.

If AI removes pointless labor, it gives us room to climb.

If AI removes every meaningful challenge, it quietly removes the training ground.

## Flow Needs a Slope

The psychology of flow gives us a better way to say this.

Flow is not created by comfort. It appears when a task is demanding enough to require full attention, but not so demanding that it crushes you. The classic model connects flow to a balance between challenge and skill: too easy becomes boredom; too hard becomes anxiety; the sweet spot is demanding but possible.[^3]

That is also a good design principle for AI.

A bad AI tool makes everything feel flat. It removes the need to think, decide, or struggle. It gives you output before you have formed judgment. It makes the first version look finished, and then quietly trains you to stop at the first version.

A good AI tool creates a slope.

It takes away the rocks that only cut your feet, but it leaves the climb.

It helps you reach the place where the work is just a little too hard for your current self. Not impossible. Not humiliating. Not performative suffering. Just hard enough that you must become more precise, more honest, and more awake.

That is why "use AI to become stronger" cannot only mean "use AI to produce more."

It must mean: use AI to place yourself closer to the edge of your current ability.

## The Difference Between a Crutch and a Whetstone

The same tool can be a crutch or a whetstone.

A crutch takes weight away because you cannot stand.

A whetstone creates resistance so the blade becomes sharper.

When AI writes a paragraph because you do not care about that paragraph, it may be a useful crutch. Fine. Life has enough chores.

But when AI writes the paragraph because you are afraid to find out what you actually think, it becomes dangerous. The result may look better than your own first attempt, but it has not sharpened you. It has hidden the place where sharpening should have happened.

The same is true for learning.

If AI explains a concept after you have tried to predict the answer, failed, and compared your reasoning with the explanation, it becomes a tutor.

If AI gives the answer before you have made any mental move, it becomes a sedative.

The difference is not the tool.

The difference is where the human effort sits in the loop.

## Play Is Not Laziness

There is another piece from neuroscience that helps here.

Jaak Panksepp's affective neuroscience proposed several primary emotional systems in mammals, including SEEKING and PLAY.[^4] I am less interested in turning that into a rigid pop-science formula than in the practical reminder: exploration is not a luxury. Play is one of the ways a living system tests the world.

Real play contains friction.

A game with no rules is not a game. A puzzle with no constraint is not a puzzle. A sport with no resistance is not a sport. A conversation where nobody risks being changed is not really a conversation.

This matters because many AI products are designed around a shallow fantasy of ease: no blank page, no hard search, no awkward draft, no slow practice, no need to sit with uncertainty.

But if we remove all uncertainty, we also remove the doorway through which curiosity enters.

The creative mind does not only need resources. It needs something to push against.

## Do Not Romanticize Pain

There is a trap on the other side too.

Once you begin defending difficulty, it is easy to become sentimental about pain. That is not the point.

Some hardship is just bad design.

Confusing software is not a teacher. Bureaucratic forms are not character building. Repetitive labor does not become meaningful because it is tiring. Poverty, exhaustion, and anxiety do not automatically make people creative. Very often, they make people smaller.

So the rule cannot be "keep difficulty."

The rule has to be more precise:

Remove friction that drains attention without creating judgment.

Preserve friction that creates skill, taste, memory, courage, or responsibility.

That distinction is the work.

AI should help us remove dead friction faster than ever. It should also help us design living friction more deliberately than ever.

A writing system can remove formatting work while preserving the need to choose a real claim.

A learning system can remove search cost while preserving recall, prediction, and self-testing.

A coding system can generate boilerplate while preserving debugging, review, and ownership.

A personal knowledge system can retrieve notes while preserving the human act of connecting them.

This is not anti-automation.

It is better automation.

## The Fifteen-Minute Human Layer

One practical answer is small enough to sound almost silly:

Keep a daily human layer.

Fifteen minutes of writing before asking for a draft.

Fifteen minutes of sketching before generating an image.

Fifteen minutes of predicting before reading the answer.

Fifteen minutes of manual review before publishing.

Fifteen minutes of trying to explain the idea in your own words before letting AI polish it.

The number is not magic. The principle matters.

Before the machine enters, make one human move.

Not because the human move will be better. Often it will be worse.

But it creates a trace of your own attention. It gives the model something to respond to besides generic probability. It lets you compare, reject, revise, and learn. It keeps the loop alive.

The future will probably not belong to people who proudly refuse AI.

But it also will not belong to people who let AI remove every moment of strain.

It will belong to people who know which strain to keep.

## The Abundance Test

Here is the test I want to use:

After AI helps me, am I more capable of doing the next version?

If the answer is yes, the tool fed me.

If the answer is no, the tool merely carried me.

Sometimes being carried is fine. We all need that. Not every task deserves to become training.

But if every meaningful task is carried for us, something inside begins to lose its grip.

The danger of abundance is not comfort itself. Comfort can be humane. Safety can create room for exploration. Tools can free attention for better work.

The danger is a life where all the meaningful resistance has been sanded away, and then we wonder why we feel strangely weak in a world full of help.

Stewart Brand's famous line from the Whole Earth Catalog was: "We are as gods and might as well get good at it."[^5] The sentence has always sounded half triumphant, half warning.

AI makes that warning more concrete.

If we are going to hold more power, we must get better at choosing what not to erase.

Do not keep pointless pain.

Do not worship difficulty.

But do not let convenience starve you.

Some friction is food.

Eat enough to stay alive.
