# A Tool Does Not Exist Until Someone Can Explain It

> 发布日期:2026-06-17 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-17-讲不明白的工具等于不存在.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-17-a-tool-does-not-exist-until-someone-can-explain-it.md)

---

Today's notes kept circling around a strange new bottleneck.

It is becoming easier to build things, but not easier to make people understand why those things matter.

That sounds like a marketing problem. I do not think it is only that. It is a deeper shift in what counts as value. In a world where AI can help one person generate code, drafts, images, interfaces, landing pages, demos, summaries, and analysis at a speed that used to require a small team, the question "Can you make it?" is losing some of its old power.

The harder question is becoming: can anyone see it?

Not merely see that it exists. See what it does. See why it matters. See whether it can be trusted. See where it fits into an already crowded life. See the difference between this thing and the ten similar things that appeared this week.

A demo is not adoption. A repository is not a product. A prompt is not a workflow. A feature is not a habit.

Until someone can explain a tool clearly enough that another person can imagine using it, the tool has not fully entered the world.

It is technically present, but socially absent.

## Building Is Getting Cheaper

The data is not subtle.

GitHub's 2025 Octoverse says more than 180 million developers were building on the platform, with more than 630 million total projects and about 230 new repositories created every minute.[^1] It also reports 4.3 million AI projects. Stack Overflow's 2025 survey shows that 84% of respondents were using or planning to use AI tools in their development process.[^2]

That does not mean everyone is suddenly a great builder. It means the cost of beginning has fallen.

This is a huge deal. A person who used to only have an idea can now make a rough prototype. A writer can build a simple internal tool. A teacher can turn repeated explanations into a practice system. A small team can test product shapes before hiring a large engineering staff. A curious person can learn by touching the material instead of only reading about it.

I like this direction. It fits one of my deepest beliefs: AI should make people stronger, not lazier.

But lower production cost creates a new problem. When more people can make more things, the scarce resource moves.

In the old internet, having a working product was already a strong signal. In the AI era, a working product may only be the starting line. The harder problem becomes interpretation: what is this, who is it for, why should I care, and what should I do next?

This is why so many technically impressive projects feel invisible. The maker thinks the value is obvious because they lived through the problem. The reader sees a dense README, a feature list, a screenshot, maybe a video, and still cannot form the simple sentence: "This helps me with X."

If that sentence does not appear in the reader's mind, the product may as well be hidden.

## Explanation Is Not Decoration

Many builders treat explanation as packaging.

First we build the real thing. Then we add a nicer name, a clearer homepage, a launch post, a video, a few social posts. Explanation becomes the ribbon around the product.

I think that mental model is wrong.

Explanation is not decoration. Explanation is part of the product's interface with reality.

A good explanation reduces the cost of adoption. It tells the user what problem to attach the tool to. It gives the user a first move. It says what the tool is not. It warns against the wrong use. It shows a before and after. It helps the user borrow the maker's mental model without needing to repeat all the maker's trial and error.

In that sense, explanation is a bridge.

The code may be brilliant, but the user cannot cross brilliance. The user crosses a story, a use case, a metaphor, a demo, a comparison, a concrete moment of recognition.

This is not a new human weakness created by social media. Human beings have always used stories to organize attention and cooperation. A 2017 Nature Communications study on the Agta hunter-gatherers found that storytelling was linked with cooperation and that skilled storytellers were preferred social partners.[^3] The lesson is not that every product needs a dramatic myth. The lesson is simpler: humans use narrative to decide what to coordinate around.

When a tool is hard to explain, it is hard to coordinate around.

That matters even more when tools are multiplying.

## The New Scarcity Is Trustable Translation

The phrase I kept thinking about today was "someone who can speak human."

Not someone who dumbs things down. Not someone who turns every serious thing into a catchy slogan. Not someone who hides complexity with confidence.

Someone who can translate without betraying.

That skill is much rarer than it looks. To explain a technical tool well, you need to understand the tool, understand the user, understand the context, and understand the emotional resistance that appears when people face something new. You need to know what can be omitted without lying, what must be named even if it is inconvenient, and which example will make the invisible part visible.

AI can help with this, but only after a human has done the hard noticing.

If you ask AI to write marketing copy for a thing you do not understand, it will usually produce smooth fog. If you bring it real user questions, failed explanations, rough demos, objections, screenshots, comments, and your own honest judgment, it can help you refine the bridge.

That is the difference between amplification and substitution.

The same Stack Overflow survey that showed high AI-tool usage also showed friction: many developers reported accuracy concerns and workflow frustration.[^2] McKinsey's 2025 State of AI report similarly found that many organizations are using generative AI, but only a much smaller share are following the practices associated with scaling impact, and most are not yet seeing tangible enterprise-wide earnings impact.[^4]

In plain language: access is not adoption.

Buying tools is easy. Opening accounts is easy. Running experiments is easy. Making a new capability understandable, trusted, integrated, and useful inside actual work is much harder.

That is where the translator becomes valuable.

## The Counterargument: Is This Just Marketing?

There is a fair objection.

If we say explanation is becoming more valuable, does that mean the future belongs to people who talk better than they build? Are we just rewarding hype, personality, and distribution?

Sometimes, yes. That danger is real.

The internet already has too many people who can make weak things sound strong. AI will make that worse. It will generate cleaner launches for worse products, sharper claims for shallower thinking, and more confident descriptions of things that do not deserve confidence.

So the answer is not "storytelling beats building."

The better answer is: building and explanation must become one loop.

If you explain before you build, you may discover the product is vague. If you show a rough demo, you may discover the user cares about a different part. If you write a simple use case, you may discover your feature list has no center. If you answer real objections, you may discover the product needs a different architecture. If you cannot explain the tool without hiding its limits, the tool itself may not be ready.

Good explanation is not a mask. It is a test.

Can the idea survive contact with an ordinary person's confusion? Can the tool survive a first-time user's wrong assumption? Can the value survive being compressed into two minutes without becoming false? Can the maker say what the tool is for without saying it is for everyone?

If not, the problem is not only communication.

The product may still be blurry.

## What This Means for a Person

This is not only for startups or developers.

It matters for anyone trying to grow in the AI age.

If you learn something and cannot explain it, the learning is still fragile. If you build a workflow and cannot teach it, the workflow is still too private. If you have taste but cannot show your criteria, other people cannot trust your taste. If you have experience but cannot turn it into language, the experience stays trapped inside one life.

That is why writing publicly, giving feedback publicly, making rough demos, and sharing learning notes are not just content tactics.

They are ways of making your thinking visible enough to be improved.

The first version may be rough. It should be rough. Roughness is not the enemy. The enemy is polished emptiness. A rough explanation with real contact can be improved. A smooth explanation with no contact only becomes more deceptive as it gets cleaner.

This is also why I like the idea of "learn by doing" more in the AI era than before. Before AI, doing often required too much setup. Now the setup is lighter. You can build the small version, show the small version, explain the small version, watch where people misunderstand it, and improve both the tool and the explanation.

That loop is powerful:

Build a little.

Explain a little.

Watch reality answer.

Revise the thing.

Revise the story.

Repeat.

Over time, the tool becomes clearer because the explanation keeps testing it. The explanation becomes clearer because the tool keeps grounding it.

## The Real Work Is Making Use Possible

I keep returning to a simple sentence:

A tool does not exist until someone can explain it.

This is not literally true, of course. The files may exist. The code may run. The model may respond. The prototype may sit on a server. The GitHub repository may have stars.

But human value is not created at the moment something can run.

Human value is created when a capability becomes usable.

And usability is not only an interface problem. It is a meaning problem.

What is this? Why now? Why this way? What does it replace? What does it not replace? What does a good first attempt look like? What should I be careful about? What kind of person or team becomes stronger if they use this well?

In a slower world, these questions could wait. The product would have time to find language around itself.

In the AI era, the world is too crowded for that.

If you build something useful but cannot explain it, someone else may explain a worse version better. If you learn something valuable but cannot translate it, it may never leave your notebook. If you use AI to make more things without improving your ability to make them understandable, you will only add to the pile.

The opportunity is not to become louder.

The opportunity is to become clearer.

The people I trust most in the next few years will not be the people who merely produce the most. They will be the people who can turn new capability into shared understanding. They will make tools easier to adopt, ideas easier to test, workflows easier to repeat, and learning easier to act on.

They will not only build.

They will help things truly arrive.
