# Put AI Where the Work Already Happens

> 发布日期:2026-07-17 · [中文](../zh/2026-07-17-把%20AI%20放到工作本来发生的地方.md) | [English](../en/2026-07-17-put-ai-where-work-happens.md)

---

The most overrated question in AI right now is: how smart is the model?

It is not a useless question. Intelligence matters. Better reasoning, better memory, better tool use, better coding, better multimodal understanding all change what becomes possible. But for most people and most organizations, the sharper question is different:

Where does the AI live?

Not in the technical sense of cloud or local, browser or desktop, chat window or API. I mean: where does it live in the human loop? Does it appear when the work is actually happening, or does it wait in a separate place, hoping someone remembers to visit? Does it receive the messy context before the decision is made, or only a cleaned-up prompt after the real discussion is already over? Does it sit where responsibility, hesitation, disagreement, handoff, and feedback already happen?

A very smart tool outside the loop often becomes decoration. A merely useful tool at the right moment can become infrastructure.

This is why many AI products feel impressive in demos but disappear from real life. The demo starts with attention. Real work starts with interruption. The demo has a prepared prompt. Real work has half-formed intent, missing files, old habits, politics, tired people, unclear ownership, and the awkward fact that nobody wants to learn another system unless the pain is already high.

The future will not belong only to the smartest AI. It will belong to the AI that gets placed closest to the moment where people already act.

The old lesson of electricity is still useful here. Early factories did not become dramatically more productive merely because a new power source arrived. In many places, electric motors were first added to existing layouts. The old factory logic remained: belts, shafts, multi-floor arrangements, and workflows shaped by the location of a central engine. The big gains came later, when firms redesigned plants around the flexibility of electric power: individual motors, different layouts, smoother material flow, new labor practices, and new forms of discretion on the floor.[^1]

That is not a story about electricity. It is a story about placement.

The motor did not change the factory until the factory changed around the motor.

AI is now going through the same misunderstanding. Many teams are trying to “add AI” to existing work the way an early factory added an electric motor to a shaft-driven layout. The meeting is still separate from the notes. The notes are still separate from the task system. The task system is still separate from the person who can approve the change. The customer complaint still becomes a summary, then a ticket, then a discussion, then a requirement, then a handoff, then a delay. Somewhere along the way someone says, “Let’s use AI.”

But the AI has arrived too late. It is no longer inside the living loop. It is cleaning up the corpse of context.

Economists have a name for part of this delay: general-purpose technologies require complementary investments. A serious new technology often demands new processes, new skills, new business models, new data habits, and new ways of measuring work. Those investments are often intangible, so the official productivity numbers can look disappointing at first. Later, once the hidden complements start working together, the gains become visible.[^2]

For an individual, the same thing is true at a smaller scale. Buying access to a better model is the visible part. The invisible part is harder: deciding which repeated problem deserves a workflow, where the input comes from, how privacy is protected, what counts as a good output, when the human must review, how mistakes return to the system, and where the next action happens.

If those complements do not exist, AI becomes a clever guest. It visits, performs, leaves, and changes very little.

If those complements do exist, AI becomes part of the room.

This is where habit matters. Human behavior is not mainly changed by lectures. It is changed by moments. The Fogg Behavior Model says a behavior happens when motivation, ability, and a prompt meet at the same time.[^3] You may disagree with the model in details, but the basic intuition is hard to ignore: if the prompt appears far away from the moment of need, the desired behavior often never happens.

This explains why many “AI workflows” fail. They ask the user to remember the tool after the user has already entered another habit. Finish the meeting, then open the AI app. Watch the video, then leave the video page to make notes. Read the customer message, then copy it somewhere else for analysis. Have the disagreement, then later write a perfect prompt summarizing what everyone meant.

That is not a workflow. That is homework.

The better design is almost always more humble: put the prompt where the person already is.

If people already discuss work in a team chat, the agent should be visible there. If people already create context in meetings, the transcript should become usable context by default, with boundaries and consent. If people already review documents in a certain folder, the assistant should understand that folder's structure. If people already make decisions through comments, approvals, or checklists, the AI should attach itself to those moments instead of asking everyone to move into a new temple called “the AI platform.”

The point is not to worship existing habits. Some habits are bad. Some workflows deserve to die. But you cannot redesign reality by pretending people are blank slates. A good tool first respects the path that attention already takes. Then, step by step, it changes the path.

The history of a famous sticky note product shows the same pattern in miniature. A weak adhesive was first a strange technical fact: it stuck lightly and could be removed. Interesting, but not yet a product. It became valuable when someone found a live human problem for it: a bookmark that needed to stay in place without damaging the page. Later, the note became a communication object because people started using it inside the office's existing flow of documents, desks, messages, and reminders.[^4]

The invention was not only the adhesive. The invention was the fit.

Many AI builders still overvalue the adhesive. They show the model can summarize, draft, code, classify, search, and reason. Good. But where does that ability stick? Which page, which folder, which conversation, which decision, which morning routine, which sales handoff, which learning loop, which review point?

If you cannot answer that, you do not yet have a product. You have capability looking for a place to land.

This distinction matters even more for organizations than for individuals. A personal AI assistant can still create value if one motivated person remembers to use it. An organization cannot rely on collective memory. It runs on defaults.

If the default is “everyone privately asks their own AI,” the organization may get faster individuals and a more fragmented company. Context hides in private chats. People learn different habits. The same question gets answered ten different ways. Mistakes do not become shared lessons. Good prompts do not become team assets. The AI increases personal throughput but leaves organizational learning weak.

If the default is “AI participates where work is already coordinated,” the effect is different. A shared agent in a common workspace is not only a tool. It is a training environment. People see how others ask, correct, reject, delegate, verify, and recover. The remaining 20 percent that the machine cannot finish becomes visible human judgment rather than private cleanup. The organization's taste becomes observable.

That visibility is uncomfortable. It should be. Real adoption usually exposes the work we were hiding from ourselves.

This is also why “make a skill for your upstream or downstream partner” is such a powerful idea. Most organizational delay lives between people, not inside people. A designer waiting for a clearer request, a marketer waiting for a visual draft, an operator waiting for a report, a manager waiting for a decision memo: these are dependency costs. If AI only makes each specialist faster inside their own box, the handoff remains expensive. But if one team packages part of its judgment into a reusable skill for another team, the boundary changes. The downstream person can act earlier. The upstream person becomes less of a bottleneck. The organization slowly moves from functional silos toward complete loops.

That is AI making people stronger, not lazier.

Lazy AI says: “Do this for me.”

Strong AI says: “Move the next capability closer to the place where I actually need it, so I can take responsibility for a larger loop.”

There is a danger here. “Put AI where the work happens” can easily become an excuse for surveillance, over-automation, and managerial fantasy. Record everything. Summarize everyone. Score every conversation. Turn every human hesitation into a metric. That is not intelligence. That is anxiety with software.

Placement without boundaries becomes intrusion.

The right question is not “How can AI see everything?” The right question is: “What context does the system need in order to help, what should remain private, who is responsible for the next action, and how can people contest or correct the output?” If those questions are skipped, embedding AI into the workflow will not create trust. It will create quiet resistance.

So the practical test is simple.

Take one AI use case you care about. Do not start by choosing the model. Draw the loop.

Where does the need first appear? Where is the context born? Where does the person hesitate? Where does the handoff happen? Where does quality get judged? Where does feedback return? Where does the next action already happen today?

Then place AI at one of those points.

Not everywhere. One point.

If the AI appears too early, it may generate noise before the problem is real. If it appears too late, it may only beautify decisions already made. If it appears in a separate place, it may be forgotten. But if it appears at the moment of friction, with the right permission, the right memory, and the right human responsibility, it can turn a repeated weakness into a stronger loop.

That is the work.

Not chasing the newest model.

Not collecting tools.

Not building a shrine to automation.

Just moving intelligence closer to reality, one loop at a time.

The next great AI interface may not look like a new app at all. It may look like a meeting that finally remembers, a note that turns into action, a folder that understands why it exists, a team chat that trains the team, a checklist that learns from failure, a personal system that asks the hard question before you escape into easy output.

AI should not wait for us in a separate room.

It should meet us where the work is already alive.
