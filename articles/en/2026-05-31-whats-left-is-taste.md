# When AI Can Replicate Everything, What's Left Is Taste

> 发布日期 · Published: 2026-05-31 · [中文](../zh/2026-05-31-当AI能复制一切剩下的只有品味.md) | [English](../en/2026-05-31-whats-left-is-taste.md)

---

Steve Jobs's closet held about a hundred identical black turtlenecks.[^1] He had asked the designer Issey Miyake to make them, and he wore the same thing every day for the rest of his life. People filed this under "eccentric genius." It was the opposite of eccentric. It was a man refusing to spend a single neuron on a question that didn't matter, so that all of his judgment could flow toward the one question that did: what should this product be?

The closet is the argument made physical. In a world where you can have anything, the rare move is to decide — on purpose — what you will not have.

That move is about to become the only one that matters.

---

## Cheap things start wars

We tell a comforting story about abundance: make something plentiful, and quality rises. History says the opposite. When a medium gets cheap, it doesn't get good. It gets violent.

In the early 1900s, William Randolph Hearst launched a Chicago paper and priced it at one cent — half what the others charged.[^2] The price war that followed went nowhere, because rivals simply matched him. So the fight left the page and moved to the street. Hearst hired a man named Max Annenberg to assemble a circulation gang armed with blackjacks, brass knuckles, and guns, whose job was to make sure newsstands sold his paper and hid everyone else's.[^3] In 1907 his crew hijacked a rival's delivery truck and dumped the whole load into the Chicago River.[^4] By 1910 the two biggest papers were each spending about a million dollars a year on this — call it thirty-odd million in today's money.[^5] By one tally, at least twenty-seven men and newsboys were killed in the peak years alone.[^6]

Notice the shape of it. When content itself became cheap — the penny press — the contest didn't disappear. It moved to the next scarce thing: distribution. Whoever controlled the newsstand won.

We are living through the same move, one layer up. AI has made content nearly free. Anyone can generate a competent essay, a passable design, a working function, in seconds. So the contest is leaving "can you produce it" — that's settled, everyone can — and moving to the next scarce thing. Not distribution this time. Judgment. The weapon changes. The war doesn't end.

## The scarce thing is inside your head

Here's the part most people get wrong. They assume the new scarce resource is "out there" somewhere — a better tool, a secret prompt, more compute. It isn't. It's the most physically limited resource you own: your own attention.

This is measurable, not metaphorical. A 2022 study in *Scientific Reports* split forty young adults into a mentally fatigued group and a rested one.[^7] The tired brains showed smaller P300 waves — meaning they allocated fewer resources to actually processing what they saw — and larger feedback-error signals, meaning they learned worse from their own mistakes. Drained people don't just feel slower. They literally evaluate the world with less of their brain online, and they grow more risk-averse, reaching for whatever relieves the pressure now.

Scarcity does this in every domain. Decades of research on poverty shows that material want consumes cognitive bandwidth directly — attention, working memory, the machinery of complex decisions — leaving less for everything else.[^8] A study of eighty-six low-income women found mental bandwidth itself measurably lower under material stress.[^9] The lesson generalizes brutally: scarcity of money, of time, of attention all draw down the same finite account. And judgment is drawn from that same account.

So when you spend your day reacting — fielding every notification, generating ten things instead of choosing one — you aren't being productive. You're overdrawing the exact reserve that good decisions require. The person who guards that budget is not being precious. They're playing a structurally different game, on a board the busy person can't even see.

## Not a faster brush — a different dimension

We have stood at this exact inflection point before, and we know how it resolves.

Photography arrived in 1839.[^10] Within a few years a machine could capture a face more accurately than a trained painter could in a week. Every artist faced a brutal question: what now? The ones who tried to out-realism the camera lost — that race was over the moment the shutter clicked. But a handful asked a better question: *what can a painter do that a camera cannot?* The answer was Impressionism. Monet and his circle stopped chasing accuracy and started chasing what the lens couldn't hold — shifting light, the felt texture of a moment, the subjective truth of being there.[^11] They didn't paint faster. They changed what painting was for. (Their first exhibition, in 1874, was held in the studio of a portrait photographer — the machine and its answer sharing a room.)

AI is the camera, and it just walked into every knowledge worker's studio. The losing move is the obvious one: try to out-produce it. Write more, ship more, generate more — faster, faster. You will lose, the same way the realist painters lost, because raw production is precisely what the machine does better than you ever will. The winning move is the harder question. What can you do that the model cannot? And the honest answer keeps landing on the same word: know what should exist, and — harder — what should not.

## The fork in the road

I won't pretend the default path bends toward growth. It bends the other way.

For most people, AI will quietly make them more productive and less capable at the same time. Every judgment you outsource is a muscle you stop using, and muscles you stop using waste away. Lean on the tool to decide, and your own capacity to decide erodes — slowly, comfortably, invisibly. That's the real risk. Not that AI replaces you, but that you volunteer to become an appendage of it: a person who can prompt but can no longer judge, who ships constantly and chooses nothing.

Jobs's closet is the other fork. Subtract on purpose. Spend nothing on what doesn't matter, so you can spend everything on what does. Used that way, AI becomes the most powerful subtraction tool ever built — it can absorb the rote work that used to drain your budget, freeing the whole account for the one thing it can't touch.

Which is taste. Not taste as decoration — taste as discernment. Jony Ive once said he'd known people who studied and studied design and still had terrible taste,[^12] which is the whole point: taste is not intelligence, not effort, not information. It is the cultivated, hard-won sense of what is worth bringing into the world. And it has always been the scarce thing. Back in 1971, the economist Herbert Simon saw it coming with eerie precision: "a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention."[^13] Information was about to become infinite, and the bottleneck would move to the one resource that doesn't scale — the attention, and the judgment, of a single human mind.

AI didn't create that scarcity. It just made it impossible to ignore.

So here is the one reframe worth carrying out of this essay. Treat every interaction with AI as a trade, and ask the trade out loud: *what am I handing over, and what am I doing with what I get back?* If you offload the busywork and reinvest the freed attention into deeper judgment — sharper taste, harder questions, things the model cannot reach — you are compounding an edge it can never copy. If you offload the busywork and let the freed attention dissolve into more busywork, you are renting a tool while your own edge quietly erodes.

The closet was never about the shirts. It was about where a finite mind chooses to spend itself. That choice was always the moat. AI just drained the water around it, so now everyone can finally see who built one.
