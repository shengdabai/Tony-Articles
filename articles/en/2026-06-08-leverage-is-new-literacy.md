# Leverage Is the New Literacy

> 发布日期:2026-06-08 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-08-看懂杠杆是新的识字能力.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-08-leverage-is-new-literacy.md)

---

Most people are still trying to become more valuable by becoming more diligent.

That sounds reasonable. Diligence is visible. You can count the hours, feel the fatigue, and tell yourself that the pain means progress. If two people produce different results, the ordinary explanation is simple: one worked harder, or longer, or suffered more.

But this explanation breaks down in the world we already live in.

A small team can build software used by millions. A single essay can travel for years. A course, a tool, a checklist, a script, a design system, a prompt library, or a repeatable service process can keep creating value after the original creator has stopped touching it. The old question was, "How much labor did you put in?" The better question is now, "What part of your work keeps working when you are not there?"

This is leverage.

Not financial leverage in the narrow sense. Not gambling with borrowed money. I mean the broader human meaning: the ability to make one unit of clear thinking, one unit of creation, or one unit of trust produce more than one unit of output.

The new literacy is not merely knowing how to use AI. It is knowing where leverage comes from.

## The Old Factory Already Told Us

The story begins before AI, before software, even before modern management.

In the first chapter of *The Wealth of Nations*, the classic pin factory example shows ten workers producing far more together than they could separately. The important detail is not the exact number. The important pattern is that output changed because work was reorganized. One person drew wire. Another straightened it. Another cut it. Another pointed it. The same human bodies, placed inside a better system, produced a different order of result.

That was an early form of leverage: division of labor.

Then came machine leverage. A machine could repeat a motion without getting tired. Then infrastructure leverage: a road, a water system, a payment rail, a logistics network, once built, could serve more people than the builders could ever meet. Then organizational leverage: a company could coordinate thousands of people through roles, processes, incentives, and trust.

Each step changed the relationship between effort and result.

But most of those older forms of leverage were permissioned. To use labor leverage, you needed people willing to follow you. To use capital leverage, you needed money or someone else's money. To use infrastructure leverage, you needed access to large systems. Even talent was often trapped by gatekeepers: a factory owner, a publisher, a distributor, a manager, a license, a credential.

The internet changed the doorway. Code and media made a new kind of leverage available to ordinary individuals. You could write once and distribute almost everywhere. You could build a small piece of software and let it run while you slept. You could publish a video, a note, a newsletter, or an open-source project without asking a newspaper, school, company, or investor for permission.

The gate did not disappear. Attention is still scarce. Trust is still hard. Distribution is still uneven. But the starting line moved.

This is why "work harder" is no longer a complete life strategy. Hard work matters, but only after you know what it is attached to. If your effort is attached to an unleveraged activity, it dies when the hour ends. If your effort is attached to a leveraged system, the hour may keep producing.

## Ideas Are Strange Goods

Physical goods are rivalrous. If I eat an apple, you cannot eat the same apple. If I sit in a chair, you cannot sit in the exact same chair at the same time.

Ideas behave differently.

An idea, a formula, a recipe, a software pattern, a story structure, a teaching method, a workflow, or a brand promise can be used by many people without being consumed in the same way. Economists call this non-rivalry. That one concept explains a lot of modern wealth.

The value of a product is not only in its atoms. It is also in the pattern that arranges the atoms, the process that makes the result repeatable, the interface that reduces confusion, the reputation that lowers decision cost, and the standard that lets strangers cooperate.

A restaurant is not just food. It is a grammar of service, supply, taste, layout, training, and expectation. Software is not just code. It is a stored decision process. A strong piece of writing is not just text. It is a portable way of seeing. A useful AI workflow is not just a prompt. It is a repeated path from messy input to usable output.

This is the practical meaning of leverage: turn something that used to be trapped inside one moment into something reusable.

The mistake is to think leverage is only for companies, investors, or engineers. It is not. A teacher who turns a recurring explanation into a reusable model is using leverage. A consultant who turns repeated client confusion into a diagnostic framework is using leverage. A creator who turns scattered observations into an archive and then into essays is using leverage. A parent who turns one household conflict into a clearer family rule is using leverage at a human scale.

Leverage begins whenever experience becomes structure.

## AI Makes Leverage Personal

AI matters because it lowers the cost of turning experience into structure.

Before AI, many people had useful patterns in their heads but no easy way to package them. They knew how to explain a concept, review a document, check a workflow, compare options, or make a judgment, but turning that know-how into a tool required writing, coding, designing, documenting, or hiring. The fixed cost was too high, so the knowledge stayed private and local.

AI reduces that fixed cost.

You can describe a recurring workflow and ask AI to turn it into a checklist. You can take a messy set of notes and ask for a taxonomy. You can turn a repeated explanation into a mini-course outline. You can ask for a script, a template, a decision tree, a test case list, or a draft interface. You can build a rough version, use it, see where it fails, and improve it.

This is not magic. It is not full automation. It is not "press a button and become excellent." But it is a major shift in who can begin.

The old world rewarded people who already had access to leverage. The new world increasingly rewards people who can design leverage from their own work.

That skill is different from prompt tricks. A prompt trick asks, "What sentence gets the model to produce a better answer?" Leverage design asks, "Which part of my life or work repeats often enough, matters enough, and has clear enough failure modes that it deserves to become a system?"

This is a higher-level question.

Many people use AI to save time. That is fine, but it is the shallow layer. A stronger use is to create reusable assets: a better personal knowledge base, a durable writing pipeline, a customer research process, a learning loop, a small internal tool, a repeatable analysis method, a library of examples, a set of evaluation criteria.

Saving one hour is useful. Building a system that changes the value of future hours is leverage.

## Leverage Amplifies Judgment

There is a dangerous side to all this.

Leverage does not make you wise. It makes your existing judgment travel farther.

A bad idea with no leverage is a small problem. A bad idea with leverage becomes a system. A shallow opinion becomes a viral slogan. A lazy workflow becomes an automated factory of mistakes. A confused metric becomes a dashboard people trust. A private misunderstanding becomes a public product.

This is why the new literacy must include restraint.

Not every repeated action deserves automation. Some friction is valuable because it forces attention. Some work should stay manual because judgment is still forming. Some knowledge should not be packaged because it depends too much on context. Some data should not be fed into tools because privacy matters more than convenience. Some drafts should not be published because distribution is not the same as contribution.

Leverage asks for taste.

The more powerful your tools become, the more important your selection becomes. What should be multiplied? What should remain small? What should be slowed down? What should be made explicit? What should be kept private? What should be deleted?

These are not technical questions only. They are character questions, product questions, learning questions, and sometimes moral questions.

AI can help you produce more. But a person who produces more of the wrong thing has not become stronger. They have only become louder.

The goal is not to become a content machine, a prompt machine, or an automation machine. The goal is to become a person whose best thinking can survive outside the moment of thinking.

## The Real Personal Operating System

If you want to use leverage well, start smaller than ambition.

Look at your week. Where does the same problem return? Where do you explain the same thing again? Where do you make the same decision with scattered information? Where do you lose attention because the path is unclear? Where do you rely on memory when you should rely on a system?

Then ask a simple question: what is the reusable shape here?

Maybe the answer is a checklist. Maybe it is a saved search. Maybe it is a set of examples. Maybe it is a template. Maybe it is a script. Maybe it is a public article. Maybe it is a private rule. Maybe it is not a tool at all, but a better habit.

This is how a self-evolving system begins. Not with a grand declaration, but with one small conversion: experience into structure, structure into feedback, feedback into improvement.

Over time, these conversions compound.

Your notes become articles. Your articles become frameworks. Your frameworks become products or teaching tools. Your repeated mistakes become tests. Your tests become standards. Your standards become taste. Your taste changes what you notice next.

That is the point. Leverage is not only about money. It is about escaping the prison of doing everything from zero every time.

The unleveraged person starts each day with bare hands. The leveraged person starts with accumulated structure.

## Use AI to Become More Capable

There is a lazy version of AI use: let the machine do the work so you do not have to think.

There is a stronger version: use the machine to expose how you think, where your process breaks, what you repeat, what you can improve, and what deserves to be turned into a reusable asset.

The first version makes you dependent. The second makes you more capable.

This is the line I care about.

The future will not belong simply to people who know the newest tool. Tools will change too fast. The future will belong to people who can notice leverage, design leverage, and govern leverage. People who can see a repeated pain and turn it into a system. People who can use AI not as a substitute for agency, but as a way to make agency executable.

In the old world, the question was: how many hours can you work?

In the new world, the question is: which part of you can keep working after the hour is gone?

That is why leverage is the new literacy.

Not because everyone must become rich. Not because every person must become a startup. But because every person who wants to keep growing needs to understand the basic move of modern life:

Do not merely spend your effort.

Convert it into something that can return.
