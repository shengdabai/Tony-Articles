# The Lag Is the Revolution

> 发布日期:2026-07-04 · [中文](../zh/2026-07-04-真正的革命常常以滞后出现.md) | [English](../en/2026-07-04-the-lag-is-the-revolution.md)

---

Today's notes circled around a sharp discomfort:

AI is everywhere, but the world has not yet become as transformed as the noise promised.

You can see the tools, demos, faster writing, faster coding, automatic summaries, AI features on every surface, shifting job descriptions, bending workflows, moving budgets, and investors rushing toward the new center of gravity.

But if you ask the harder question - where is the civilizational productivity jump? - the answer is much less obvious.

This gap creates two lazy reactions: hype says everything will change next year; dismissal says it was only an expensive toy.

I do not trust either one.

The more useful view is this: when a general-purpose technology arrives, the first visible wave is usually not the real revolution. The first wave is substitution. Old tasks get a new tool. Old processes get a faster engine. Old reports get prettier. Old emails get written in half the time.

That is not nothing. But it is not yet transformation.

The real revolution arrives later, and often looks like a lag.

## A New Engine in an Old Room

There is a famous line from 1987: "You can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics."[^1]

It survived because it named something many people could feel. Computers were appearing in offices, homes, schools, factories, and banks. Yet the broad productivity numbers did not immediately behave like a revolution had arrived.

The same pattern appears in the older analogy of electricity. If a factory kept the same layout, workflow, and assumptions about how workers and machines should move, the new motor often became a cleaner version of the old constraint.

The deeper gain came when the factory itself was redesigned around the new possibility.[^2]

That distinction matters for AI.

Most AI adoption today is still "a new engine in an old room." We put AI into the inbox, the document, the chat window, the search box, the coding editor, the customer support script. This can save time. Sometimes it saves a lot of time. Experiments have found large gains in specific tasks: one study of writing tasks found that access to ChatGPT reduced average completion time and improved rated quality; another study of customer support agents found a meaningful increase in issues resolved per hour, with especially large gains for newer or less-skilled workers.[^3]

Those results are real.

But micro gains do not automatically become macro transformation.

An individual can become faster while the organization stays the same. A team can generate more text while decisions remain slow. A company can deploy ten AI tools while its real bottleneck is permission, unclear ownership, bad data, weak evaluation, or a workflow nobody has had the courage to redesign.

The machine got faster.

The room is still wrong.

## The Measurement Problem Is Not an Excuse

It is tempting to say: maybe the value is already here and the statistics are blind.

Sometimes that is true. Traditional economic measures are better at counting output than counting relief, optionality, better search, faster learning, or avoided frustration. A person who uses AI to understand a legal document, prepare for a difficult conversation, repair a spreadsheet, or learn a new tool may gain real life value that will not appear cleanly in GDP.

Economists studying general-purpose technologies have a name for part of this pattern: the Productivity J-Curve.[^4] The idea is not mysterious. New technologies often require complementary investments that are hard to measure: training, process redesign, new datasets, new management habits, new interfaces, new norms, new ways of judging quality. In the early years, organizations spend heavily on these hidden complements, while measured productivity may look disappointing. Later, when those complements begin to work together, the visible gains can arrive.

That is the optimistic story.

But we should be careful. "The value is invisible" can easily become a lazy excuse for projects that are simply bad.

Not every delay is a J-Curve.

Sometimes the product is useless. Sometimes the pilot is theater. Sometimes the workflow did not need AI. Sometimes the tool creates more review work than it removes. Sometimes the organization buys intelligence and then traps it inside approval chains, messy data, vague ownership, and no feedback loop.

The existence of historical lag does not prove that every current AI investment will pay off.

Lag is not permission to stop thinking.

It is pressure to ask a sharper question:

What invisible complement are we actually building?

## Cost Cutting Is the Smallest Imagination

The weakest AI strategy is simple automation: take a task, reduce the human minutes, keep everything else the same.

This is useful in narrow places. If a repetitive support response can be drafted safely, draft it. If a document can be classified automatically, classify it. If a test can be generated, generate it. Dead friction should die.

But if AI remains only a cost-cutting machine, its ceiling is lower than the hype suggests.

A task can become cheaper without the system becoming smarter.

The more interesting question is not "How many minutes can this save?"

The more interesting question is "What new loop becomes possible because the cost of thought, search, drafting, simulation, and feedback has collapsed?"

That is a different kind of imagination.

For a writer, the point is not only faster drafts. It is a tighter loop between notes, questions, sources, counterarguments, revision, translation, and publishing.

For a learner, the point is not only easier explanations. It is a loop where confusion becomes visible, mistakes become practice, and understanding is rebuilt instead of merely received.

For a small business, the point is not only automatic content. It is a loop where customer questions, delivery failures, product decisions, documentation, and follow-up become a learning system.

For a developer, the point is not only more code. It is a loop where specification, tests, implementation, review, deployment, and user evidence become closer together.

AI becomes serious when it stops being a clever assistant inside the old workflow and starts changing the shape of the workflow.

That is where the lag lives.

Because changing shape is slower than adding a button.

## The Counterargument: What If the Skeptics Are Right?

There is a fair objection here.

What if AI really is being overestimated? What if the easy tasks are already captured, the hard tasks remain stubborn, and the macro effect is smaller than the cultural obsession around it?

That objection deserves respect.

Some economists have argued that if AI mainly creates task-level cost savings, the aggregate effect over the next decade may be real but modest, especially when the easy-to-measure tasks are not the same as the hard, context-dependent work that matters most.[^5] The OECD has also framed the outcome as highly dependent on adoption speed, sector breadth, and whether AI is integrated into wider digital systems rather than isolated pockets.[^6]

This is not anti-AI. It is adult supervision.

It reminds us that technology is not magic dust. A model does not automatically become better medicine, better education, better governance, better products, or better lives. Between capability and value there is a long bridge: institutions, incentives, workflows, trust, data, taste, law, accountability, and actual human need.

That bridge is where many revolutions fail.

It is also where real builders work.

So yes, the skeptics may be right about many AI projects. Many will be wasteful. Many will be forgotten. Many will look embarrassing in hindsight. Some will be automation cosplay with a subscription attached.

But that does not make the whole wave meaningless.

It means the difference between toy and transformation will not be decided by model demos alone.

It will be decided by who rebuilds the bridge.

## Personal AI Has the Same Problem

This is not only a company problem.

Individuals make the same mistake at a smaller scale.

We add AI to an old life and expect a new life to appear.

We ask it to summarize our reading, but keep the same shallow relationship with ideas. We ask it to write our drafts, but keep the same unclear point of view. We ask it to plan our day, but keep the same inability to choose what matters. We ask it to automate work, but keep the same bad definition of what "done" means.

Then we wonder why everything feels faster but not deeper.

The answer is uncomfortable:

We installed the motor, but we did not redesign the factory.

For a person, the "factory" is not a building. It is the set of loops that turn attention into change.

How do you capture what matters, choose what to ignore, test an idea against reality, turn a mistake into a rule, and know whether AI made you stronger or merely helped you avoid the part that would have made you stronger?

These are structural questions, not productivity hacks.

The person who uses AI only to move faster inside old loops may feel busy and modern. The person who uses AI to rebuild the loops will look slower at first.

They will spend time naming the process, cleaning the inputs, deciding the test, making review visible, and defining the next use.

That looks like a lag.

It is also where compounding begins.

## Build the Complement

The most important question for this stage of AI is not "Which model is best?"

That question matters, but it is not the deepest one. Models will keep changing. Prices will change. Interfaces will change. Capabilities will move.

The deeper question is:

What complement are you building that becomes more valuable as intelligence gets cheaper?

A clear workflow is a complement. So is a personal knowledge system that feeds real decisions, a habit of testing output before trusting it, a customer evidence loop, a taste for good questions, and a body of practice that lets you judge the answer.

These things do not look as exciting as a new model release. They rarely produce the feeling of instant magic.

But they are the things that let magic become work.

The lag is not empty time. It is the period when serious people build the missing complements while everyone else argues about whether the revolution has arrived.

So when AI feels both everywhere and not yet fully real, I do not think the right response is blind faith.

I also do not think the right response is cynicism.

The right response is construction.

Take one old workflow. Do not sprinkle AI on top of it. Rebuild the loop around the new capability. Decide what the human must still judge, what the machine can draft or monitor, how mistakes return to the system, and what evidence proves the loop is better.

Then run it long enough for the hidden work to become visible.

That is not glamorous.

But most real revolutions are not born in the demo.

They are born in the redesign after the demo stops being impressive.
