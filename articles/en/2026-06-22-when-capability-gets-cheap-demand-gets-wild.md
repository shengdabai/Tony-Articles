# When Capability Gets Cheap, Demand Gets Wild

> 发布日期:2026-06-22 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-22-能力越便宜需求越野.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-22-when-capability-gets-cheap-demand-gets-wild.md)

---

One sentence stayed with me today:

When capability gets cheaper, demand does not politely stay where it was.

It wakes up.

This is the part many people still miss about AI. They look at a model writing code, summarizing documents, making slides, drafting emails, and they ask the most obvious question: if the machine can do this work faster, will there be less work for humans?

It is a reasonable fear. It is also too small.

The more interesting question is not whether AI saves time on the old task. It is what happens to the world once that task becomes cheap enough to be used ten times more often, by ten times more people, in places where it previously made no economic sense.

That is where the real story begins.

## Efficiency Does Not Always Mean Less

In 1865, the economist William Stanley Jevons wrote about a strange pattern in Britain. Steam engines were becoming more efficient, so each unit of work required less coal. A simple mind might expect coal consumption to fall.

The opposite happened.

Because coal-powered work became cheaper, more industries found new uses for it. Efficiency lowered the effective price of energy, and lower price released more demand. Yale's Energy History project summarizes Jevons's argument clearly: improved efficiency in consumption would not reduce energy demand; it could spur increased use instead.[^1]

That is the Jevons paradox.

The useful part is not coal. The useful part is the pattern.

When an input becomes cheaper, people do not merely do the same amount of work at lower cost. They redesign their expectations around the new cost. They try things they would not have tried. They raise standards. They create new categories. They stop treating the old bottleneck as sacred.

AI is doing this to cognition.

Drafting used to be expensive. Now it is cheap.

Code used to be expensive. Now more of it is cheap.

Research summaries used to be expensive. Now first-pass synthesis is cheap.

Translation, ideation, prototyping, basic analysis, test generation, customer-message variants, internal documentation: many things that used to require "Is this worth the time?" now start with "Let's see three versions."

That sentence sounds harmless.

It is not.

"Let's see three versions" is the moment demand wakes up.

## The Calendar Version of Jevons

Most people experience this not as macroeconomics, but as a calendar problem.

AI helps you finish the first draft faster. Then the standard rises. The article needs better examples, a stronger ending, a cleaner argument, a sharper title, a bilingual version, maybe a visual, maybe a distribution package.

AI helps a product person make a prototype faster. Then the team wants five variants, a customer interview script, a landing page, a pricing test, a competitor matrix, and a demo by Friday.

AI helps an engineer generate code faster. Then the scarce part becomes writing the spec, judging edge cases, reviewing security implications, designing rollback, preserving tests, and deciding whether the feature should exist at all.

The old task shrinks.

The surrounding ambition expands.

This is why "AI will save you time" is such an incomplete sentence. Save time for what?

If the saved time is spent avoiding harder thinking, AI makes you softer. If the saved time is reinvested into deeper judgment, better verification, richer taste, and more courageous experiments, AI makes you stronger.

The tool is not the strategy. The reinvestment is the strategy.

## Jobs Are Bundles, Not Single Tasks

The lazy version of the AI employment debate treats every job as one task. If AI can do the task, the job disappears. If AI cannot do the task, the job survives.

Reality is messier.

A job is a bundle of tasks, responsibilities, relationships, context, trust, and consequences. AI can unbundle some tasks. It can make some parts nearly free. But once the cheap part expands, the expensive parts often become more visible.

This is why forecasts are so contested. The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2025 estimates that by 2030, 170 million new roles may be created and 92 million displaced, for a net increase of 78 million jobs.[^2] That is not a promise that everyone will be fine. It is a signal that the labor market is being rearranged, not simply deleted.

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics still projects employment for software developers, quality assurance analysts, and testers to grow 15 percent from 2024 to 2034, much faster than the average for all occupations, with about 129,200 openings each year on average.[^3]

Again, this is not comfort food.

It does not mean every junior developer has an easy path. It does not mean every old workflow survives. It does not mean the transition will be fair, gentle, or evenly distributed.

It means the simple sentence "AI writes code, therefore software jobs vanish" is not enough.

When software gets cheaper, more people want software in more places. The question shifts from "Can someone write this?" to "Who knows what should be written, why it matters, where it can fail, and who is responsible when it does?"

That is a very different labor market.

## The New Scarcity Is Not Busyness

Keynes famously imagined in 1930 that technological progress might eventually make a fifteen-hour workweek enough to satisfy our needs.[^4] In one sense, he saw something true: productivity really did explode.

But human desire did not freeze.

We did not keep the same standard of life and take all the rest as leisure. We invented new expectations, new professions, new anxieties, new luxuries, new forms of comparison, and new definitions of "normal."

This is not necessarily noble. Sometimes it is ridiculous. A better tool can turn into a bigger treadmill.

AI can absolutely do that.

It can make a manager ask for more reports instead of better decisions. It can make a creator publish more noise instead of better thinking. It can make a founder ship more features instead of finding a real customer. It can make a student outsource effort instead of building capacity.

So the point is not "AI creates more jobs, relax."

The point is sharper:

AI lowers the cost of many abilities, and every lower cost forces a moral choice.

Do we use cheap cognition to multiply shallow output, or to attempt work that used to be beyond us?

Do we use cheap drafting to avoid having a point, or to refine a point until it is finally worth sharing?

Do we use cheap coding to flood the world with half-owned tools, or to build systems with clearer boundaries, better tests, and more honest accountability?

Cheap capability does not automatically produce a stronger person.

It only removes one excuse.

## What Becomes More Valuable

If capability gets cheap and demand gets wild, the most valuable people are not those who merely "use AI."

That phrase is already too weak.

The valuable person can do five things.

First, they can choose a real problem. When execution is expensive, weak ideas die early because no one can afford them. When execution is cheap, weak ideas survive long enough to waste everyone else's attention. Problem selection becomes a premium skill.

Second, they can define taste. AI can generate options, but it cannot tell you what kind of work you are willing to sign your name to. Taste is not decoration. Taste is the compressed memory of values, standards, and lived consequences.

Third, they can build verification into the work. More output means more surface area for error. The serious user does not simply ask AI to "be careful." They create tests, checklists, review loops, sources, logs, and stopping rules.

Fourth, they can carry responsibility. The more automated the task, the more important it becomes to know who owns the result. In high-stakes domains, the human value often moves toward judgment, explanation, and accountability.

Fifth, they can turn personal method into a system. A person who only has a clever trick will be copied. A person who has a repeatable loop can improve. The real upgrade is not a prompt. It is a way of working that gets better after every mistake.

This is why I do not like the fantasy of AI as a servant that makes life effortless.

That story is too small for human beings.

The better story is AI as leverage for self-evolution. It lets one person try more, test more, learn faster, cross more boundaries, and build systems that used to require a team.

But leverage has a direction.

If you point it at laziness, you scale laziness.

If you point it at practice, you scale practice.

## A Better Question

So instead of asking, "Will AI take my job?" I think a better question is:

What demand becomes possible when my current bottleneck gets cheaper?

If writing gets cheaper, can I think in public more consistently?

If coding gets cheaper, can I build tools around my own real problems?

If research gets cheaper, can I connect fields that used to live far apart?

If translation gets cheaper, can I let ideas travel across languages without losing their soul?

If prototyping gets cheaper, can I stop worshiping ideas and start testing them against reality?

This is not blind optimism. Some work will disappear. Some people will be hurt. Some institutions will use AI to extract more from people instead of helping them grow. Pretending otherwise is dishonest.

But it is equally dishonest to stare only at disappearing tasks and ignore the expanding frontier.

The future does not belong to people who are merely busy. It does not belong to people who produce the most artifacts. It does not even belong to people who know the most tools.

It belongs to people who can turn cheap capability into meaningful demand.

Not more output for its own sake.

More serious questions.

More personal agency.

More reality checks.

More responsibility.

More chances to become someone who could not have existed under the old cost structure.

That, to me, is the useful optimism of AI.

Not that it will make us work less.

That is possible, but not guaranteed.

The more important possibility is this:

AI may make our old limits cheaper to cross.

What we become after crossing them is still on us.
