# The Budget Teaches the Builder

> 发布日期:2026-07-16 · [中文](../zh/2026-07-16-预算会把人教成不同的人.md) | [English](../en/2026-07-16-the-budget-teaches-the-builder.md)

---

Today's notes circled a sentence that sounds like a cost-management problem at first:

do not be poor in the model, and do not be stingy with tokens.

It is easy to hear that as a simple argument for spending more money on AI. Buy the stronger model. Raise the quota. Stop treating every long prompt like a personal failure. That reading is not wrong, but it is too small.

The deeper point is this:

a budget teaches behavior.

If you give a person a tiny AI budget, you are not merely reducing cost. You are teaching them to ask smaller questions, skip exploration, avoid second drafts, accept the first answer, and treat verification as a luxury.

If you give a person abundant AI access with no standards, you teach a different bad habit: produce more, check less, confuse motion with progress, and let the machine's fluency replace your own judgment.

The real question is not whether tokens should be cheap or expensive.

The real question is: what kind of person does your AI budget train?

## Cheap Intelligence Changes Frugality

We still carry an old instinct from the software era: conserve the expensive resource.

That was sensible when computation was rare, storage was expensive, bandwidth was slow, and every request felt like something that should be optimized away. But AI is pushing us into a strange new economy. The Stanford AI Index reported that the inference cost for a system performing at roughly the level of GPT-3.5 fell more than 280-fold between November 2022 and October 2024. It also notes that hardware costs have been declining around 30 percent per year while energy efficiency has improved around 40 percent per year.[^1]

This does not mean intelligence is free. It is not. The International Energy Agency estimates that data centers used about 415 TWh of electricity in 2024, around 1.5 percent of global electricity consumption, and projects about 945 TWh by 2030 in its base case.[^2]

So there are two truths at once.

At the industrial layer, AI has weight. It consumes electricity, chips, land, cooling, capital, and permission.

At the user layer, each extra round of thinking is becoming dramatically cheaper than it used to be.

That combination changes the meaning of frugality.

The old frugality was: use less machine time.

The new frugality is: waste cheap machine time so you do not waste scarce human life on the wrong thing.

This is not an invitation to generate slop. It is the opposite. It is an invitation to spend tokens on the parts of work that make your own standards sharper: alternative plans, counterarguments, edge cases, source checks, dry runs, error logs, comparisons, practice problems, and small experiments that reality can answer.

The wasteful person asks once and publishes.

The careful person asks ten times so the final human decision becomes cleaner.

From the outside, both people may look like they are "using AI." Inside, they are being trained in opposite directions.

## A Budget Is a Curriculum

A curriculum is not only what a teacher says. It is what the environment repeatedly rewards.

AI budgets work the same way.

If a company says, "Use AI," but every employee has a tight quota, shared account, approval process, or quiet fear of being blamed for usage, the real lesson is not "use AI." The real lesson is "use AI only when you can justify it in advance."

But the whole point of learning a new tool is that you often cannot justify the useful use case in advance. You discover it by trying. You ask a messy question. You compare three approaches. You let the model criticize your plan. You throw away the first answer. You run a small simulation. You ask it to explain what evidence would change its answer. You build a disposable prototype.

That looks inefficient only if you measure the wrong thing.

If the metric is tokens consumed per final output, experimentation looks wasteful.

If the metric is learning per week, experimentation may be the work.

This is especially true for people who are not yet strong. A well-known NBER study by Erik Brynjolfsson, Danielle Li, and Lindsey Raymond looked at 5,179 customer support agents after the introduction of a generative AI assistant. The tool raised productivity by 14 percent on average, but the improvement was much larger for novice and lower-skilled workers: 34 percent.[^3]

That result is often summarized as "AI helps juniors more." But the more interesting reading is that AI can act like a temporary scaffold. It gives less experienced workers access to patterns they have not yet internalized. If they only copy the answer, the scaffold becomes a crutch. If they study the pattern, test it against reality, and slowly absorb the judgment, the scaffold becomes training.

The difference is not the model.

The difference is the learning design around the model.

And the budget is part of that design.

## Starving the Model Starves the Practice

Many people use AI in a way that almost guarantees no growth.

They ask one compact question because they do not want to spend tokens. The model gives one polished answer. They skim it, feel temporarily smarter, and move on.

That is not practice.

Practice needs repetitions.

A writer needs bad drafts. A programmer needs failed tests. A teacher needs different explanations for different learners. A founder needs awkward demos. A researcher needs discarded hypotheses. A designer needs ugly versions. A person rebuilding their life needs false starts that are cheap enough to survive.

AI can make all of those repetitions cheaper.

But only if you let it.

If your AI budget is so tight that every round feels precious, the tool turns into an oracle. You approach it with ceremony. You ask for "the answer." You become careful in the wrong way. You polish the prompt before you have explored the problem. You avoid asking the embarrassing beginner questions. You do not run the second check. You do not ask for the opposite case. You do not make the model show its uncertainty.

You save tokens and lose practice.

That is a bad trade.

The point is not to burn compute for status. A person can consume millions of tokens and still learn nothing if they never stop to inspect the work. Token volume is not wisdom. More gym visits do not automatically produce strength if you never lift correctly.

But the opposite failure is now more common among cautious people: they underuse the cheap training partner because they still treat every interaction like an expense report.

In the AI era, a serious learner needs a practice budget.

Not only a software subscription.

A practice budget.

A budget for asking naive questions, making prototypes, comparing explanations, checking facts, generating drills, building tiny tools, and letting the machine attack your assumptions before reality does.

## The Human Part Must Get More Expensive

There is a trap here. Once we say "spend more tokens," people may hear "think less."

That is exactly backwards.

The more machine thinking you can afford, the more expensive your human standard must become.

Cheap tokens should not lower the bar. They should raise it.

If the model can produce ten drafts, your job is not to accept the most fluent one. Your job is to become better at seeing why nine of them should die.

If the model can write code quickly, your job is not to stop understanding the system. Your job is to demand tests, logs, boundaries, rollback paths, and a clearer mental model.

If the model can summarize a field, your job is not to pretend you have studied it. Your job is to find the hard source, the exception, the disagreement, the part that still hurts your head.

If the model can produce a business plan, your job is not to admire the plan. Your job is to put one small piece in front of the world and watch whether the world answers back.

This is why an abundant AI budget without a judgment budget is dangerous. It makes everything feel done before anything has been earned.

The zeroth class does not use AI to replace the human part.

It uses AI to make the human part more demanding.

It spends tokens to buy more contact with reality, not less.

## What a Good AI Budget Should Teach

A good AI budget should teach a person four habits.

First, ask bigger questions. Do not use AI only to make the old task faster. Use it to ask what task should exist at all.

Second, iterate before you identify with the output. The first answer is raw material. The second answer is a mirror. The third answer starts to reveal the shape of your own standard.

Third, verify as a default, not as a special event. Spend tokens on source checks, adversarial review, and failure cases before the cost moves into the real world.

Fourth, leave residue. After the session, something should remain inside your system: a checklist, a reusable prompt, a better note, a small script, a clearer decision rule, a sharper question, or a piece of judgment you can perform without help next time.

If nothing remains except a beautiful output, the budget was consumption.

If something remains that makes the next round stronger, the budget was training.

This distinction matters for individuals, but it matters even more for organizations. A team that rations AI like office stationery will produce timid usage. A team that removes limits without accountability will produce chaos. A team that pairs generous access with explicit standards can grow a new kind of capability: people who are not merely faster, but better at shaping work.

The budget teaches the builder.

So design the lesson.

## Do Not Save the Wrong Thing

I want to keep today's lesson simple.

Do not save the wrong thing.

Save attention.

Save integrity.

Save the time you would have spent walking confidently in the wrong direction.

Save the future cost of a decision nobody checked.

Save the human courage to look at a weak first version and improve it instead of hiding behind polish.

Do not save the few extra tokens that would have helped you see the problem more clearly.

Of course, be responsible. AI has physical costs. Organizations need budgets. People need limits. There is no virtue in turning compute into noise.

But there is also no virtue in starving the very loop that could make you stronger.

A small mind with a large model can still produce shallow work.

A serious mind with enough machine practice can become frighteningly capable.

That is the line.

The future will not belong to people who merely spend the most tokens.

It will belong to people whose spending becomes training, whose training becomes judgment, and whose judgment becomes a system that keeps getting better.
