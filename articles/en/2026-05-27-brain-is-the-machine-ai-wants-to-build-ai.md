# Your Brain Is the Machine AI Is Trying to Build

> 发布日期：2026-05-27
>
> 中文版 / Chinese version：[你的大脑，正是 AI 拼命想造的那台机器](../zh/2026-05-27-你的大脑正是AI拼命想造的那台机器.md)

---


Think about the last time something clicked for you. Not the last time you asked a chatbot a question and got a clean answer back — the last time you sat there, two unrelated things in your head, and they snapped together on their own. That small jolt. The "oh."

That jolt is not a figure of speech. It is your hippocampus doing physical work: taking a fragment from today and pressing it into a network you built yesterday, firing a connection that wasn't there a second ago. You felt a piece of machinery turn over.

Here is the part that should unsettle you a little. That machine — the one that gave you the "oh" — is exactly what the entire AI industry is now spending billions of dollars trying to build. You were issued one at the factory. And the habit of asking AI for everything is quietly leaving it to rust.

## The man who lost the converter

On September 1, 1953, a surgeon named William Beecher Scoville opened the skull of a 27-year-old epileptic named Henry Molaison and removed most of his hippocampus from both sides of his brain.[1] The seizures eased. But something else happened that no one had predicted. From that day on, Molaison could not form a single new long-term memory.

He lived another fifty-five years. Every nurse who cared for him was a stranger every morning. A book he was reading stayed forever on the first page, because the page he had just read evaporated as his eyes moved down. He could hold a thought for about thirty seconds; then it was gone.[2]

What Molaison taught a generation of scientists was counterintuitive. The hippocampus is not where memories are stored. His old memories — childhood, his own name, how the world worked before 1953 — were all intact. What he lost was the converter: the one piece of equipment that takes "what happened today" and presses it into "what I can call up years from now." The warehouse was fine. The machine that fills the warehouse was gone.

## But his hands kept learning

Then came the strange part. Brenda Milner gave Molaison a mirror-drawing task — trace a star while looking only at its reflection, which scrambles every instinct your hand has. The first day he was clumsy, like anyone. She had him do it again the next day, and the next. His error count dropped. His hand got faster, smoother, better.[3]

And every single day, he swore he had never seen the task before in his life.

His hand had learned something his conscious mind had no access to. Which means memory is not one thing. It is several systems running on different machinery in different parts of the brain. Some of what you know lives in a place that "being told" can reach. Some of it only grows in a place that "being told" cannot touch — it has to be built, by you, through doing.

Hold onto that distinction. The whole argument turns on it.

## The most expensive race in tech is to rebuild you

Fast-forward to 2026. On May 20, at a Beijing AI summit, Deng Yafeng — a Shanda Group vice president running a team called EverMind — told the room that the gap between the big models is closing, and that the real moat from here on is memory and the data layer.[4] His team had spent a handful of months building a long-term-memory system for AI agents, submitted two papers to ACL 2026,[5] and, with Chen Tianqiao, put up an $80,000 prize for a global memory competition.[6]

They are not alone. The open-source Hermes Agent from Nous Research — around 170,000 stars on GitHub, MIT-licensed[7] — already ships the whole loop in working code: it persists memory in a local database with full-text search and LLM-written summaries, generates reusable skills from its own experience, and even has an early self-improvement pipeline.[8] An agent that remembers, learns skills, and rewrites itself.

Read those feature lists again and you will notice what they describe. A converter for turning recent experience into durable, retrievable knowledge. A system that connects across past sessions. A loop that gets better the more it runs. They are reverse-engineering Molaison's hippocampus, in software.

One honest caveat. This is a functional analogy, not a mechanical one. EverMemOS stores records and retrieves them; your hippocampus physically rewires synapses. They are not the same machine. But that difference is the whole point — and it cuts in your favor. Being told a fact and growing the circuitry that holds it are two different events. The AI uses external storage precisely because it has no synapses to rewire. You do.

## "But the calculator didn't make us dumber"

Here is the strongest objection, and it deserves a straight answer, not a dodge. We outsourced arithmetic to the calculator. We outsourced facts to the search engine. Decades went by and humans did not turn into idiots. So why should outsourcing the "connecting" to AI be any different?

Because there is a line between two kinds of outsourcing, and everything depends on which side you are on.

A calculator outsources a deterministic operation — one with a single correct output that needs no internal model of yours to be useful. You offload the multiplication; nothing above the multiplication is harmed, because nothing above it depended on you doing the multiplication by hand. Arithmetic does not compound inside you.

Judgment compounds. Connection compounds. Taste compounds. These are not operations with a clean answer you can look up; they are capacities that exist only as a network you grew, and they get more valuable the denser that network becomes. Outsource arithmetic and you lose nothing built on top of it. Outsource connection and you lose everything built on top of it — because there is nothing on top of it anymore. Nothing was built.

That is the difference. The calculator never touched the machine. Asking AI to do your thinking aims straight at it.

## Why the connections have to grow in you

Why does connection have to be grown rather than delivered? Look at what the brain actually does with it.

There is real evidence that learning transfers across domains. Train someone's ear on musical pitch and their sensitivity to pitch in *language* improves too — both ride the same statistical-learning machinery underneath, so sharpening the cue in one domain sharpens it in any signal that carries it.[9] The connection between two far-apart things is not metaphor; under the right conditions it is one network being reused.

Be honest about the limit, though. Most of the cross-domain "insights" people brag about are noise — you can string a story between any two facts if you try hard enough. So the real mechanism is humbler and more useful than "music makes you better at language." It is this: the denser your internal network, the higher the odds that two nodes which genuinely belong together will find each other. You cannot manufacture the signal. You can only build a network wide enough that the signal, when it comes, has somewhere to land. A sparse network connects nothing, because there is nothing to connect to.

That density is not something AI can hand you. It is the residue of every connection you made yourself.

## Use AI to interview yourself, not to answer for you

So here is the use that builds the machine instead of bypassing it.

Ask AI to write your goals for the year and you get a tidy, sensible list — read more, exercise, save. By February it is gone, because nothing in your head changed when you read it. You were told. Now turn it around: ask AI to interview *you*. Have it push on what you actually think, ask the follow-up question you would have skipped, drag the half-formed fragment out of you and hold it up. You connect the last mile. That connection leaves a mark, because you made it.

This is the division of labor that actually fits the two machines. AI does the outside work — retrieval, breadth, the relentless next question. Your brain does the inside work — integration, the snap, the durable trace. The converter spins faster. The network grows denser. You are not handing the thinking away; you are recruiting a tireless interviewer to force more of it out of you.

## Two uses, not two classes of people

None of this sorts people into the enlightened and the lazy. It is not about who you are. It is about which of two things you are doing on any given afternoon. The same person who let AI write the throwaway email this morning can, this afternoon, use AI to interrogate a decision they have been avoiding. One use spends the machine down. The other feeds it.

The argument does not depend on any of the 2026 headlines, either. The summit will date, the star count will climb, the papers will be old news by next year. What EverMind and Hermes Agent are chasing in code is just the thing you were shipped with — a converter that turns the day into something you can stand on later, a network that connects across everything you have ever learned.

It is still running. The only question is whether you are still using it.

## Further Reading

- Scoville & Milner (1957) — the founding case report of H.M.; the clearest primary source on how the hippocampus dissociates short-term from long-term memory.
- Squire, L. R. (2009). The legacy of patient H.M. for neuroscience. *Neuron*, 61(1), 6–9. PMC2649674 — a careful corrective on what the 1953 lesion did and did not prove; useful for not overclaiming the hippocampus's role.
- EverMemOS (arXiv:2601.02163) — read the three-stage pipeline (episodic trace → semantic consolidation → reconstructive recall) next to any description of how the brain consolidates memory, and the borrowed vocabulary is hard to miss.

## In Short

In 1953 a surgeon removed most of Henry Molaison's hippocampus, and he could never form a new long-term memory again — proving the hippocampus is not the warehouse but the converter that turns today into something you can call up years later. In 2026 the AI industry is spending billions to rebuild that converter in software: EverMind's memory systems, the $80,000 memory competition, the Hermes Agent that remembers and self-improves. But being told a fact and growing the circuitry that holds it are two different events. Outsourcing arithmetic to a calculator costs you nothing, because nothing compounds on top of arithmetic; outsourcing your thinking to AI costs you everything, because judgment and connection only exist as a network you grew yourself. So use AI to interview you, not to answer for you — let it drag your fragments out, and you connect the last mile. Same person, two uses: one spends the machine down, the other feeds it. It is still running. The only question is whether you are still using it.
