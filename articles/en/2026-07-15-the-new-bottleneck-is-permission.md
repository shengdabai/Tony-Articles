# The New Bottleneck Is Permission

> 发布日期:2026-07-15 · [中文](../zh/2026-07-15-新的瓶颈是许可.md) | [English](../en/2026-07-15-the-new-bottleneck-is-permission.md)

---

Today's notes pointed at a less comfortable AI story.

For the last few years, the public conversation has been trained to look upward: bigger models, longer context, better reasoning, more agents, cheaper tokens, faster chips. That was not wrong. Intelligence had to become abundant before the next constraint could reveal itself.

But now the constraint is revealing itself.

The bottleneck is no longer only whether the model can think.

The bottleneck is whether the world will allow that thinking to run.

AI needs electricity. It needs land. It needs cooling. It needs transformers, transmission lines, interconnection queues, environmental permits, local hearings, water plans, noise limits, ratepayer protection, construction workers, and neighbors who do not feel that an abstract future is being built on top of their concrete present.

That is why I think the next AI scarcity is not intelligence.

It is permission.

Not permission in the narrow sense of "someone signs a form." Permission in the deeper sense: the right to enter reality without pretending reality is just a slower computer.

## Intelligence Has Become a Load

Software used to feel weightless.

A new app could be launched from a laptop. A cloud service could scale behind a clean interface. A model demo could make it seem as if intelligence simply appeared from the screen, like language without mass.

But large-scale AI has a body now.

In December 2024, Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory released a report prepared for the U.S. Department of Energy. It estimated that U.S. data centers consumed 176 TWh of electricity in 2023, about 4.4 percent of total U.S. electricity use. The same report projected that data center electricity use could reach 325 to 580 TWh by 2028, or roughly 6.7 to 12 percent of U.S. electricity use, depending on growth scenarios.[^1]

The International Energy Agency's 2025 *Energy and AI* report puts the global picture in the same direction. It estimates that data centers used about 415 TWh of electricity in 2024 and projects around 945 TWh by 2030 in its base case, just under 3 percent of global electricity consumption.[^2]

These numbers change the category of the conversation.

This is not only a software story anymore.

It is an industrial story.

It is also a civic story.

When a data center wants hundreds of megawatts, it is no longer asking only developers and investors for support. It is asking a grid to absorb a new kind of demand. It is asking a community to accept noise, land use, water pressure, and possible utility-bill effects. It is asking public systems to move faster without breaking the trust that made those systems legitimate in the first place.

The old AI question was: can we make the machine smarter?

The new question is: can we make the machine a responsible guest in the physical world?

## Permission Is Not the Enemy of Progress

It is tempting for technologists to treat permission as friction.

Permits are slow. Community meetings are messy. Regulation sounds like delay. Local resistance can look irrational from far away. If you are staring at model scaling curves, the physical world can feel like a bureaucracy standing between genius and destiny.

But that view is too convenient.

Permission is not automatically anti-progress. Often, permission is the world asking a fair question:

who pays the cost of your progress?

On July 14, 2026, New York issued Executive Order No. 62, creating a temporary moratorium on qualifying large data centers while the state develops standards for data center development and a benefits blueprint for localities. The governor's office described the pause as applying to state environmental permits while the state builds a framework around ratepayers, the environment, the energy grid, and communities.[^3]

You can agree or disagree with that policy. The policy debate is not my point.

The signal is simpler: AI infrastructure has become visible enough that local systems are starting to answer back.

This is the moment when a technology stops being a private optimization and becomes a public negotiation.

That negotiation may be clumsy. It may overcorrect. It may be captured by politics, lobbying, fear, or misunderstanding. But the underlying question is real. If a facility consumes large amounts of power, changes local infrastructure planning, and affects people who do not directly profit from the model, then "move fast" is not a complete argument.

Progress that cannot explain its cost is not yet mature progress.

It is only demand.

## The Blueprint Is Not the Capacity

There is a useful distinction here.

A plan is not capacity.

A press release is not capacity.

A capital-expenditure announcement is not capacity.

A pile of GPUs is not capacity if the building cannot be powered, cooled, permitted, connected, and accepted.

This is where today's AI infrastructure story becomes a broader lesson about engineering. Real capability appears only when many boring layers line up. The exciting layer depends on the quiet layers below it.

For a model, those layers may be data, evaluation, deployment, monitoring, cost control, and user trust.

For a data center, those layers may be substations, transformers, land rights, water systems, construction timelines, utility planning, and community benefits.

For a person building a life with AI, those layers may be file organization, clear goals, privacy boundaries, repeatable workflows, source checking, and the permission to let a tool touch parts of your work without breaking your responsibility.

In all three cases, the visible intelligence is not the whole system.

The system is the permission architecture around the intelligence.

This is why the phrase "AI infrastructure" can be misleading. We often use it to mean chips, clouds, and model-serving stacks. But infrastructure also includes the social and procedural surfaces that make a thing allowed, trusted, and repeatable.

If nobody knows who benefits, permission weakens.

If nobody knows who pays, permission weakens.

If nobody can audit what happened, permission weakens.

If the local person only sees the cost while the distant company captures the upside, permission weakens.

The builder who ignores this may still have capital. They may still have models. They may even have demand.

But they do not yet have capacity.

## This Also Applies to Personal AI

It would be easy to keep this article at the level of data centers and policy.

But the same pattern appears in ordinary AI use.

Many people want an agent that "handles everything." It sounds great. Let it read the files, check the calendar, send the emails, search the web, move the documents, update the spreadsheet, summarize the call, and make the next plan.

Then reality appears.

Which files is it allowed to read?

Which messages are private?

Can it send anything without review?

What happens when it is wrong?

Who notices a silent failure?

Which task needs speed, and which task needs consent?

Where is the log?

Where is the off switch?

A personal AI system also needs permission. Not because machines have rights, but because humans have responsibilities.

If you do not define permission, the agent either becomes too weak to matter or too dangerous to trust. Too weak means it can only chat. Too dangerous means it can act where it should have asked.

The useful middle is designed access.

Read this folder, but not that folder.

Draft emails, but do not send them.

Use my public notes, but strip private details before publishing.

Summarize the meeting, but mark uncertainty.

Run the script, but preserve the log.

Make the result easy to inspect.

This is not bureaucracy. This is how trust becomes operational.

The same is true in learning. A tutor, human or artificial, needs permission to challenge you. If you only allow comfort, the tutor cannot make you stronger. If you only allow attack, the tutor may make you defensive. The relationship has to define what kind of pressure is allowed, when, and for what purpose.

Permission is not merely a gate.

It is a design surface.

## The Zeroth Class Builds Permission Systems

I keep returning to the idea of the zeroth class: people who use AI to become stronger, not softer.

Here is another definition:

the zeroth class knows that power without permission does not compound.

They do not only ask, "What can this model do?"

They ask:

Where may it act?

What evidence must it preserve?

What cost does it create?

Who is affected but not represented?

What should remain human?

What must be explained before it is scaled?

These questions sound slower than a demo.

They are.

But slowness at the right layer can make the whole system faster later. A clear permission structure reduces fear, prevents hidden damage, and lets stronger automation run without turning every action into a leap of faith.

This is true for companies building AI infrastructure.

It is true for communities deciding what kind of development they want.

It is true for one-person businesses turning a tool stack into a service.

It is true for learners deciding whether AI is helping them practice or helping them avoid practice.

The next serious advantage will not belong only to people with the largest model or the cheapest token.

It will belong to people who can make intelligence legible to the systems it must enter.

## Ask What Has Not Given Permission Yet

So here is the question I want to keep from today's notes:

What has not given permission yet?

Maybe the grid has not given permission.

Maybe the community has not given permission.

Maybe the user's workflow has not given permission.

Maybe your own standards have not given permission.

Maybe the facts have not given permission.

Maybe your future self has not given permission because the work leaves no log, no source, no reusable structure, and no way to learn from the mistake.

This question is not anti-AI.

It is pro-reality.

AI is becoming powerful enough that reality can no longer be treated as a passive background. Reality has costs, rhythms, limits, histories, communities, and memory. It pushes back. It asks for proof. It asks for respect. It asks to know whether the beautiful machine will leave people stronger or merely surrounded by infrastructure they did not choose.

That pushback is not the end of AI.

It is the beginning of AI growing up.

The model can think.

The chip can compute.

The agent can loop.

The capital can rush.

But the world still has to say yes.

And the builders who understand that yes, who earn it instead of assuming it, will build the systems that last.
