# Proving You're Human Is the Wrong Bet

> 发布日期 · Published: 2026-06-01 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-01-证明你是人是一场押错的注.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-01-proving-youre-human-is-the-wrong-bet.md)

---

In 2023, a designer in Philadelphia named Allen Hsu started a website called Not By AI.[^1] The idea was simple: if at least 90% of your work was made by a human, you could put a small badge on it that said so. Free for personal use; a paid subscription if you wanted to stamp it on a business.[^2] Three years later the badge is on a long tail of websites, and Hsu is not the only one selling the feeling. There are at least half a dozen competing "human-made" marks now, plus a whole industry of AI detectors that schools and platforms pay for, plus a gray market of services that strip the AI fingerprints back off so your work can pass as human again.[^3]

Sit with that last one for a second. We have built tools to prove content is human, tools to detect that it isn't, and tools to defeat the detectors. An entire economy has grown up around a single anxious question: *how do I prove I'm not a machine?*

It goes past content. The Light Phone III ships for $799 — more than a base iPhone — and its whole pitch is everything it refuses to do: no apps, no browser, no social media, no email.[^4] The clothing brand Aerie pledged to never use AI-generated bodies in its ads, and its sales jumped 23% year over year in the quarter after.[^5] "What it can't do" has become a price tag. "Made by a human" has become a luxury good.

I understand the impulse completely. And I think almost everyone buying it is making the wrong bet.

---

## A badge is a wall, and walls don't make value

Here is the quiet problem with every one of these moves. They are all *defensive*. The badge, the dumbphone, the no-AI pledge — each one draws a line and says: *the machine stops here, and on this side, I am safe.*

But a line is not a moat. A wall doesn't create anything; it just marks the spot where you decided to stop. It can keep something out for a while. It has never, in the history of anything, generated value on its own. The newsstand, the guild, the union card — every wall that people mistook for a fortress eventually got walked around, because the thing on the other side kept getting better and the people behind the wall stopped moving.

When you pay to certify that you're human, you're not building anything. You're standing very still and asking to be inspected. That's a fine thing to do once. It is a terrible thing to organize your life around.

## We have run this exact experiment before

In March 1811, in a village near Nottingham, a group of men broke into a workshop and smashed the knitting frames.[^6] They were called Luddites, after a man named Ned Ludd — who, it turns out, never existed.[^7] He was a story, an invented general the workers signed their threatening letters with. (Notice that detail. A defensive movement reaching for a savior who isn't there is one of the oldest shapes in history.)

We remember the Luddites as people who hated technology. They didn't. Most of them were elite craftsmen — croppers, stockingers — skilled workers who had spent years earning high wages with their hands.[^8] What they were actually fighting was *deskilling*: factory owners using new frames so that an untrained child could do in an afternoon what a master had trained ten years to do. The Luddites weren't anti-machine. They were anti–being-made-irrelevant.[^9] Which is exactly what the badge buyers are too.

And here is the part that matters. The machines won. Not because the machines were better than the craftsmen — at first they were worse, the cloth was shoddier. The machines won because the craftsmen chose to fight the tool instead of climbing on top of it. They put their backs to the future and threw rocks at it. History rewards the people who learn to drive the new thing, never the people who guard the old one. Every single time.

The Luddites lost the argument so completely that we now use their name as an insult. That is what losing a defensive war looks like in the long run: not just defeat, but your name becoming the word for "person who was on the wrong side."

## The opposite of defense

So what's the offensive move? Not "prove you're human." Use the machine to become a human the machine can't build.

There's a line I keep coming back to, and it's brutal in the best way: **if AI is making your work easier, you're using it wrong.** Read it twice, because it inverts everything the productivity crowd tells you. Most people reach for AI to feel lighter — to offload the thinking, ship faster, sweat less. That feels like winning. It is quietly the opposite. Every judgment you hand to the tool is a capacity you stop exercising, and capacities you stop exercising atrophy. Lean on it to think, and your own ability to think erodes — slowly, comfortably, invisibly. That's not productivity. That's *capacity offloading*, and at the end of it you are exactly the deskilled worker the Luddites were afraid of becoming. You just did it to yourself, voluntarily, and called it efficiency.

The other way feels worse and is better. Used right, AI doesn't make you do less — it lets you take on more than you could have carried alone. You don't offload the hard thinking; you offload the rote work *around* it, and you pour the freed energy straight back into harder thinking, bigger systems, things you couldn't have attempted last year. You finish the day more tired, not less, because you did more. That tiredness is the receipt. It means you were *loading* capacity instead of shedding it.

That's the fork. Two people open the same model. One asks it to do the work so they can relax. The other asks it to clear the runway so they can attempt something that used to be impossible. A year later they are not slightly different. They are different *kinds* of people. One has been quietly hollowed out — fast at asking, helpless at deciding, an operator with no one home. The other has compounded into something genuinely rare. And no badge can tell them apart, because the badge measures the wrong thing — it measures whether AI touched the work, when the only question that matters is whether the work made *you* larger or smaller.

## Build the thing that builds you

There's a deeper reason the offensive move wins, and it's almost mathematical. Back in the late 1940s, John von Neumann worked out the logic of a machine that could build a copy of itself — a self-reproducing automaton — and showed that the engine of real growth is *recursion*: a system that improves the system that improves the system.[^10]

That's the whole game now. The rare people aren't using AI to produce more essays. They're using it to build a system that makes *them* better at building — and then turning that system on itself. Recursion, applied to a single human life. You don't get there by certifying that you stayed the same. You get there by changing on purpose, over and over, using the most powerful change-amplifier ever invented as your lever instead of your replacement.

So the next time you open a model, run one question before anything else: *am I about to load capacity, or shed it? Will I come out of this larger, or smaller?* That single question, asked honestly every time, is worth more than every human-made badge ever printed.

The badge says: *I am still human.* It's a wall, and the machine will walk around it like every machine has walked around every wall.

The better sentence — the offensive one, the one no certificate can sell you — is this: *I am becoming someone the machine could never build.*

Stop paying to prove you're a person. Start using the machine to become a rarer one.
