# Learn to Predict Before You Automate

> 发布日期:2026-06-19 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-19-先学会预测再让AI代劳.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-19-learn-to-predict-before-you-automate.md)

---

One of the easiest mistakes in the AI era is to confuse output with intelligence.

The model writes. The page appears. The summary is clean. The code runs once. The presentation looks finished. The workflow feels magical because a thing that used to take an afternoon now takes a few minutes.

But a finished-looking output is not the same as understanding.

The deeper question is: can the system predict what will happen next?

That question sounds technical, but it may be one of the most useful questions for ordinary people using AI. It applies to robots, chemistry labs, writing, learning, business, and personal growth. A system that cannot predict consequences can still produce impressive text. It can still imitate patterns. It can still pass as useful for a while.

But when reality pushes back, imitation starts to crack.

This is why I keep coming back to a simple distinction:

AI should not only help us produce faster. It should help us build better prediction loops.

If it only helps us produce, we become faster at making artifacts.

If it helps us predict, test, correct, and remember, we become stronger.

## Language Is Not the Whole World

Large language models are astonishing because language is a powerful compression layer for human experience.

A recipe compresses cooking. A manual compresses machinery. A law compresses social conflict. A textbook compresses centuries of trial and error. A good explanation compresses someone's hard-earned understanding into a few paragraphs.

So it makes sense that language models feel so intelligent. They are trained on the traces of human intelligence.

But traces are not the thing itself.

The word "hot" is not heat. A driving manual is not traffic. A market report is not a customer handing over money. A learning plan is not the discomfort of being tested. A beautiful AI-generated strategy is not the consequence of running it for three months.

This is why recent work on so-called world models is interesting beyond robotics. Meta's V-JEPA 2, for example, is described as a self-supervised video world model built to understand, predict, and plan in the physical world. The point is not that it writes better sentences. The point is that it learns from video and can be used for planning actions in new environments.[^1]

That is a different ambition.

Instead of only asking, "What text should come next?", the system asks something closer to, "If this action happens in this situation, what state is likely to follow?"

That is closer to how we survive as humans.

We do not cross a street by generating a paragraph about traffic. We predict motion. We do not learn a skill only by collecting definitions. We try, fail, update, and try again. We do not build trust by sounding confident. We build trust by repeatedly matching words with outcomes.

Language matters. But intelligence that cannot meet reality eventually becomes decorative.

## Science Is a Loop, Not a Speech

The same pattern is showing up in AI for science.

The most interesting part is not that an AI can read papers or propose hypotheses. That is useful, but it is still mostly language. The more important shift is connecting the model to a feedback loop where proposals meet experiments.

Periodic Labs frames this directly: science works by proposing how the world might be, running experiments, and learning from the results. The company is building AI scientists and autonomous laboratories, not merely chatbots that summarize papers.[^2]

That sentence matters because it puts reality back in the center.

A model can generate a thousand plausible ideas. Reality accepts only some of them.

In chemistry, the same direction is becoming visible. OpenAI and Molecule.one recently reported work on improving Chan-Lam coupling, a reaction relevant to medicinal chemistry. Their paper describes a high-throughput screen of 10,080 microscale reactions involving primary sulfonamides, with AI helping propose and refine the experimental direction while humans still handled oversight and validation.[^3]

The lesson for most of us is not chemistry.

The lesson is the loop:

idea -> experiment -> result -> correction -> next idea

This is what turns intelligence from performance into progress.

A model that only generates ideas can make you feel rich in possibilities. A system that tests ideas can make you less wrong.

That distinction is everything.

## The Personal Version of a World Model

We do not need robot arms or chemistry equipment to use this idea.

Every person already has a world model.

It is the private system of expectations we carry around:

- If I sleep late, tomorrow's focus will be worse.
- If I only collect notes, I will feel productive but remember little.
- If I start with a vague goal, the AI will produce vague work.
- If I avoid hard feedback, the project will look alive while quietly dying.
- If I turn a recurring task into a checklist, the next attempt will be easier.

The problem is that most people's world models stay implicit.

We feel them as intuition, mood, confidence, anxiety, taste, resistance, or "I just know." Sometimes that intuition is earned. Sometimes it is old fear wearing a useful mask. Sometimes it is laziness pretending to be strategy.

AI can make this better, but only if we use it correctly.

The weak use of AI is asking it to replace the uncomfortable part:

"Write the plan."

"Summarize the book."

"Make the decision."

"Tell me what to do."

The stronger use is asking AI to expose the prediction:

"What do I expect to happen if I follow this plan for 30 days?"

"What evidence would prove this idea wrong?"

"Where does this workflow fail on the second repetition?"

"What part of this article is merely sounding smart?"

"What result should I check tomorrow morning?"

This turns AI from a content machine into a thinking mirror.

Not a mirror for feelings. A mirror for predictions.

## A Prediction Is a Moral Act

This sounds abstract, so let me make it practical.

When you make a prediction, you put your thinking at risk.

That is why people avoid it.

It is much safer to say, "AI will change education." Almost nobody can falsify that today.

It is riskier to say, "If I use AI to generate quiz questions after every lesson for the next 14 days, I will remember more than if I only reread my notes." Now we can test it.

It is safe to say, "This product has potential."

It is riskier to say, "If I show this demo to ten target users this week, at least three will ask for a follow-up call." Now reality has a vote.

It is safe to say, "I should write more."

It is riskier to say, "If I publish one clear essay every day for 30 days, I will discover which ideas actually have tension and which ones were only interesting in my notebook." Now the world can answer.

Prediction creates accountability.

And accountability is what turns learning into compounding.

Without prediction, feedback becomes noise. You do something, something happens, and you explain it afterward.

With prediction, feedback becomes information. You expected one thing, observed another, and now your model of the world has a chance to improve.

This is why the "zero class" in the AI era will not be defined by who owns the best tools.

It will be defined by who keeps improving their internal model.

## Automation Without Prediction Makes People Weaker

There is a dangerous version of AI convenience.

It lets people skip the very friction that would have made them stronger.

If a student lets AI solve every problem before struggling, the output improves while the student's prediction ability weakens.

If a founder lets AI write every strategy without talking to users, the deck improves while the market model stays fake.

If a creator lets AI produce endlessly without checking what readers actually remember, the publishing rhythm improves while the taste model decays.

If a knowledge worker automates a workflow before understanding its failure modes, the system becomes faster at producing hidden errors.

This is not anti-AI. It is the opposite.

I want AI to be used more deeply.

But deep use means we should not ask AI only to remove effort. We should ask it to move effort to a higher level.

Instead of spending an hour formatting notes, spend that hour testing recall.

Instead of spending three hours drafting from zero, spend those hours sharpening the claim.

Instead of manually repeating a process, automate it, then inspect where it breaks.

Instead of asking AI for the answer, ask it to help you design the feedback loop that will make the answer better next time.

That is the difference between laziness and leverage.

Laziness says: "Do this for me."

Leverage says: "Help me build a system that makes my judgment better."

## The Smallest Useful Loop

A good prediction loop does not need to be complicated.

For most personal work, it can be as small as five lines:

- What am I trying to change?
- What do I predict will happen?
- What will I measure or observe?
- When will I check?
- What will I change if I am wrong?

That is enough.

Before using AI to write an article, predict what the article should make the reader feel, question, or do.

Before using AI to build a tool, predict who will use it, in what moment, and what they will do if it fails.

Before using AI to learn a subject, predict what you should be able to explain without notes after one week.

Before using AI to optimize a workflow, predict which step is actually the bottleneck.

Then run the loop.

Let AI help you generate options, build the checklist, create the test, analyze the result, and remember the lesson.

But do not let AI remove the prediction.

The prediction is where your mind touches reality.

## The Future Belongs to People Who Can Be Corrected

The more powerful AI becomes, the easier it will be to live inside fluent illusions.

Everything will sound reasonable. Every weak idea will have better packaging. Every unfinished thought will find an elegant sentence. Every shallow plan will arrive with a clean table and a confident conclusion.

So the rare skill will not be prompting.

The rare skill will be correction.

Can you notice when the output is beautiful but the model of reality is weak?

Can you design a test before you fall in love with the answer?

Can you let tomorrow's evidence embarrass today's confidence?

Can you update without losing momentum?

This is what lifelong learning looks like in the AI age. Not endless consumption. Not endless generation. Not building a personality around tools.

It is the willingness to keep bringing your ideas into contact with reality, faster and more honestly than before.

AI can help with that.

It can make the loop shorter. It can make the experiment cheaper. It can make reflection clearer. It can remember what you learned last time. It can force you to write down the prediction you were about to hide from yourself.

But it cannot care on your behalf.

It cannot decide that becoming stronger matters more than appearing smart.

That part is still ours.

So before asking AI to automate the task, ask one better question:

What should I learn to predict first?
