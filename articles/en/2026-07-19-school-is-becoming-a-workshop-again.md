# School Is Becoming a Workshop Again

> 发布日期:2026-07-19 · [中文](../zh/2026-07-19-学校正在重新变成工坊.md) | [English](../en/2026-07-19-school-is-becoming-a-workshop-again.md)

---

For a long time, school was built around a quiet assumption: knowledge was scarce.

The teacher had it. The textbook organized it. The classroom delivered it. The exam measured whether the student had absorbed enough of it. This system was not stupid. It fit the world that produced it. When books were expensive, teachers were rare, information traveled slowly, and large organizations needed people who could follow shared procedures, a standardized school made sense.

But every technology eventually exposes the hidden assumptions inside an institution.

AI is doing that to school.

The new argument is often presented in the shallowest possible form: if AI can explain algebra, summarize a book, solve a practice test, or draft an essay, then children no longer need to learn so much. That is the wrong conclusion. It treats learning as if the main purpose were to move information from an external source into a student's head, then stop.

The more interesting conclusion is almost the opposite.

If AI can make access to explanation cheap, school should become more demanding, not less. It should ask students to do what cannot be faked by quick access to an answer: make something, defend a choice, revise after feedback, collaborate with other people, recover from confusion, and connect knowledge to consequences in the real world.

In other words, school is becoming a workshop again.

This is why a new wave of AI-native schools is worth paying attention to, even if we should not worship it. Forge Prep, for example, describes itself as a school for grades 5 to 12, with a first campus opening in Livingston, New Jersey in Fall 2026 for grades 5 to 8. Its public message is blunt: a school built for 2040, not 1940. Students are expected to learn by building businesses, conducting original research, and designing products people actually use. The school also says that graduates who continue down an entrepreneurial path can receive a $200,000 investment.[^1][^2]

Alpha School represents a different but related experiment. Its "2 Hour Learning" model says students complete core academics in the morning through AI-powered personalized learning, then use the rest of the day for workshops and real-world skills.[^3]

We should be careful here. These schools are expensive. They serve a narrow slice of families. Their public claims are not the same as long-term proof. Education is full of beautiful experiments that worked for early adopters and then broke when scaled. A school can have a strong narrative and still fail ordinary students. A model can be inspiring and still be unequal.

But we should not dismiss the signal just because the first versions are imperfect.

The signal is this: once knowledge delivery can be personalized and accelerated, the scarce part of education moves somewhere else.

It moves to attention.

It moves to feedback.

It moves to judgment.

It moves to the student's ability to turn tools into agency.

A 1984 educational research paper made a famous claim that one-to-one tutoring combined with mastery learning could produce performance far beyond conventional classroom instruction. The practical problem was scale: society could not give every learner a good personal tutor all day.[^4] AI does not magically solve education, but it changes the cost structure of personalized explanation, practice, and feedback. That matters.

Still, a tutor is not a school.

This distinction is important. A tutor can help a learner understand a concept. A school must help a person become the kind of human who can use concepts under pressure. Those are different jobs.

If AI becomes the tutor, the school cannot merely become a room where children sit with screens. That would be the laziest version of the future: private tutoring at scale, but with less human presence, less social practice, and fewer opportunities to build courage.

The better version is harder.

AI handles part of the knowledge loop: diagnosis, explanation, practice, review, translation, examples, reminders, adaptive difficulty. Human teachers, mentors, and peers handle the formation loop: taste, ethics, responsibility, collaboration, public expression, emotional resilience, and the discipline of finishing.

The first loop asks: do you understand?

The second loop asks: what can you do with what you understand?

Traditional school often pretended these were the same question. They are not. A student can understand a formula and still be unable to design an experiment. A student can know vocabulary and still be unable to persuade another person. A student can pass a test on entrepreneurship and still be unable to sell a small thing to a real customer. A student can ask AI for a beautiful plan and still collapse when the plan meets friction.

This is where "AI makes people stronger" has to become more than a slogan.

AI makes a student stronger only if the surrounding system asks for stronger behavior.

If the system rewards copying, AI will make copying easier.

If the system rewards shallow completion, AI will make shallow completion faster.

If the system rewards obedient answer production, AI will produce more obedient answers.

But if the system rewards proof, revision, initiative, and responsibility, AI becomes a force multiplier for those traits.

This is the part many education debates miss. The tool is not the curriculum. The incentive system is the curriculum.

Tell a student, "Use AI to finish the worksheet," and AI becomes a shortcut.

Tell a student, "Use AI to build something a real person can test," and AI becomes a workshop assistant.

Tell a student, "Use AI to avoid confusion," and AI becomes a sedative.

Tell a student, "Use AI to name your confusion, generate three explanations, test which one works, and write down what changed in your thinking," and AI becomes a mirror for learning.

The same model can weaken or strengthen a person depending on the loop around it.

This is not only about children. Adults have the same problem. Many adults use AI to generate more content, more plans, more outlines, more polished sentences. The screen fills up, so it feels like progress. But nothing in the person's life has necessarily changed. No decision was tested. No habit was rebuilt. No skill became embodied. No feedback was taken from the real world.

That is why the workshop metaphor matters.

A workshop is not a place where you merely receive information. It is a place where materials resist you. Wood splits. Code fails. Customers ignore you. A prototype breaks. A sentence sounds wrong when spoken aloud. A teammate misunderstands the plan. Reality pushes back.

Good education needs that pushback.

AI can remove pointless friction. It can explain a concept ten ways, translate a text, generate practice problems, simulate a debate, help debug code, and remind a learner what to review. That is valuable. We should welcome it.

But it should not remove all friction. Some friction is the training itself.

The student still needs to choose. The student still needs to persist. The student still needs to make taste visible. The student still needs to hear another person's objection without falling apart. The student still needs to publish, present, ship, repair, apologize, and try again.

If a school uses AI to delete those experiences, it is not preparing students for the future. It is wrapping them in a more intelligent bubble.

The strongest AI-native education will probably have three layers.

The first layer is personalized knowledge acquisition. Each student gets explanation, practice, and review at the right level and speed. This is where AI is naturally useful.

The second layer is project-based proof. Students must create artifacts outside the answer sheet: essays, tools, experiments, presentations, businesses, research notes, films, community projects, repaired machines, public arguments. The point is not that every child must become a founder. The point is that every learner needs proof of contact with reality.

The third layer is human formation. Students need adults and peers who can ask better questions than a grading rubric: Why did you choose this? Who does it help? What did you miss? What did you change after feedback? Where did you hide behind the tool? What will you do differently next time?

That third layer is the hardest to automate because it is not only cognitive. It is moral and social. It asks the learner to become visible.

This also changes what "teacher" means.

The teacher as lecturer will not disappear, but that role becomes less central. The teacher as coach, editor, critic, project designer, culture builder, and judge of seriousness becomes more central. A good teacher in the AI era may spend less time saying the first explanation and more time designing the conditions under which students cannot remain passive.

That is a much higher bar, not a lower one.

It is also a more human bar.

There is a temptation to think the future of education will be decided by better AI tutors alone. I do not think so. Better tutors will matter, but the decisive question is whether we can redesign the environment around the tutor.

If the old school was a factory for standardized answers, the new school cannot simply be a more efficient factory.

It has to become a place where a young person learns how to convert abundant intelligence into personal agency.

That phrase sounds abstract, so make it concrete.

Can you notice a real problem?

Can you ask AI for help without surrendering your judgment?

Can you learn enough background to stop being fooled by fluent nonsense?

Can you build a small version?

Can you show it to someone?

Can you survive the embarrassment of weak first attempts?

Can you improve the work after feedback?

Can you explain what you learned in your own words?

Can you do it again, in a different domain?

That is education for the AI age.

Not less learning.

More contact with reality.

Not fewer standards.

Better standards.

Not children protected from difficulty by intelligent tools.

Children strengthened by intelligent tools, human feedback, and real work.

The old classroom trained people to answer questions when the teacher already knew the answer.

The workshop trains people to move when nobody has handed them the answer yet.

AI makes the second kind of education more urgent, not less. Because when answers become cheap, the ability to turn answers into judgment, work, and character becomes expensive.

That is the quiet shift underneath the AI school story.

The future is not a child sitting alone with a perfect tutor.

The future is a child surrounded by tools, mentors, peers, materials, constraints, and real consequences, learning how to become someone who can make useful things in the world.

School is not ending.

It is being asked to remember what it was for.
