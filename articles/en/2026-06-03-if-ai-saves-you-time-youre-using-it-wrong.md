# If AI Is Saving You Time, You're Using It Wrong

> 发布日期:2026-06-03 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-03-如果AI让你更省事你大概率用错了.md) | English

---

My Claude account has a weekly limit. I pay for the highest tier, and most weeks I run out of budget around the sixth day. The tool that was supposed to hand me back my evenings leaves me rationing tokens by Friday. For months I filed this under billing — a number to optimize, a plan to upgrade. It is not a billing problem. It is the entire argument, compressed into one line of an invoice: when a tool that makes work cheaper is used by someone who actually works, the bill goes up, not down.

That sentence runs against everything AI was sold on. The promise was subtraction — fewer hours, lighter load, your life back. The receipt says addition. If your AI is genuinely saving you time, the most likely explanation is not that you have become efficient. It is that you have stopped doing the thing efficiency was supposed to amplify.

## The pitch and the receipt

The pitch is rest. Automate the boring parts, reclaim the weekend, let the machine carry the weight. It is a good pitch. It sells.

The receipt tells a different story, and you only get to read it if you talk to people who are actually shipping. None of them are working less. They are working more, learning more, and they are tired in a specific way — the way you are tired after a day that went somewhere, not the way you are tired after a day that drained out. The ones who got their evenings back, in my experience, are mostly the ones who used AI to avoid the work rather than to do more of it. They feel lighter. They are also, quietly, learning nothing.

This is not a paradox of AI. It is a very old law wearing new clothes.

## Jevons already ran this experiment

In 1865, the English economist William Stanley Jevons published a book with an unpromising title, *The Coal Question*. Britain was anxious about running out of coal, and the comforting theory of the day was that James Watt's vastly more efficient steam engine would slow the burn — same work, less fuel, crisis postponed.

Jevons looked at the data and saw the opposite. As engines got more efficient, coal did not get conserved. It got consumed — far more of it. Cheaper power per unit of work made steam economical for industries that could never have afforded it before, so factories multiplied, and total coal consumption soared. By 1900 it had tripled.[1] Jevons stated the mechanism without hedging: "It is a confusion of ideas to suppose that the economical use of fuel is equivalent to diminished consumption. The very contrary is the truth."[2]

Make a resource cheaper to use and people do not bank the savings. They use more of it. The efficiency does not reduce the load. It removes the ceiling.

## What AI makes cheap is the act of doing

Substitute one word and Jevons is describing your week. AI did not make leisure cheaper. It made *doing* cheaper — writing the function, drafting the argument, building the prototype, reading the paper you would have skipped. The cost per unit of producing something dropped through the floor.

By the old logic, that should mean you produce the same amount in less time. By Jevons's logic — the correct one — it means you produce far more, because things that were previously too expensive to attempt are suddenly within reach. The backlog of "I'd build that if I had a month" collapses into an afternoon, so you build it, and then you see the next thing, and that is now affordable too. The list does not get shorter. It gets longer, faster than you can clear it. That is not a failure of the tool. That is the tool working exactly as a cheaper engine works.

## Learning is production. Production is learning.

There is a second collapse happening underneath the first, and it is the one that matters most.

For most of history, learning and producing were separate phases. You studied, then you applied. You read the manual, then you built the thing. AI dissolves the seam. I did not learn to be an engineer by reading a textbook and then writing code. I learned by building an editor — a real one, north of three hundred thousand lines — with AI as the bridge between not-knowing and shipping. Every line I could not have written alone taught me something the moment it ran or broke.

This was not studying. It was building. And the building was the studying.

That is what the line "learning is production, production is learning" actually means once a tool exists that can hold the gap open while you cross it. The reason real users end up busier is not discipline. It is structural. When doing teaches and teaching requires doing, there is no idle phase left to save time in. The time you "save" on production gets reinvested, automatically, into more production, which is more learning. The loop has no exit marked rest.

## The fork in the surplus

Every efficiency gain hands you a surplus, and there are only two things to do with it.

You can spend it on doing less. Let the model write the thing you don't understand, paste the output, move on, feel light. Do this long enough and the muscle that used to do the work quietly wastes away. You become dependent on a tool you cannot evaluate — fluent at prompting, helpless at judging. I have a private word for where that road ends, and I do not want to arrive there: outsourcing your thinking until you can no longer tell when the machine is wrong.

Or you can spend the surplus on doing more — taking on the project that was out of reach, reading the source you used to skip, building the second thing now that the first one was cheap. This road is exhausting and it compounds. AI does not flatten the difference between these two people. It widens it. The same tool that lets one person coast lets the other person climb, and a year later they are not in the same place at all. The technology makes the capable more capable and quietly retires everyone who was getting by on bluff.

Same surplus. Opposite lives.

## The tiredness is the receipt

I owe an apology, and it belongs in this argument rather than off to the side. For years I underestimated how much work systems engineering actually is — I thought a lot of it was simple. Building something real with my own hands, even with AI carrying half the weight, showed me how wrong that was. The respect arrived through the fatigue, not despite it.

So I will not pretend the busier path feels good in the moment. It doesn't. My account runs dry by day six. The work expands to fill every tool I point at it. But the exhaustion is not a sign the tool is broken. It is the sign that real learning happened — that the surplus got spent on climbing instead of coasting. Tired-because-empty and tired-because-full look identical from the outside and are opposite on the inside. One is the cost of avoidance. The other is the receipt for growth.

## What it actually is

AI is not a labor-saving device. We were sold a labor-saving device, and the framing is doing real damage, because it trains people to measure success by how little they now have to do.

It is a leverage device. And leverage has never, in any era, lowered the load on the people who use it well. It raises the ceiling on what they can reach. The lever did not let the worker rest; it let him move the bigger stone. The steam engine did not let Britain burn less coal; it let Britain build an empire on more of it. The pattern is two centuries old and perfectly consistent, and there is no reason this one breaks it.

If the work got lighter, look again at what you stopped doing.

Not less work. More — and more of you in it.

## In Short

AI was sold as a way to do less. Used correctly, it makes you do more — more building, more learning, more tired. This is not a quirk; it is Jevons's 1865 paradox repeating: make a resource cheaper to use and total use goes *up*, not down. AI made *doing* cheap, so the backlog of things worth doing exploded. And because AI collapses learning into producing — you learn by building, not before it — there is no idle phase left to bank the savings. Every efficiency gain is a surplus, and you get two choices: spend it doing less (and slowly lose the ability to think for yourself), or spend it doing more (exhausting, compounding). Same tool, opposite outcomes. The tiredness from the second path is not a bug. It is the receipt that growth happened. AI is not a labor-saving device. It is a leverage device — and leverage never lowers the load. It raises the ceiling.
