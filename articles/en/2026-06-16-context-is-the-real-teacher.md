# Context Is the Real Teacher

> 发布日期:2026-06-16 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-16-真正的老师是语境.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-16-context-is-the-real-teacher.md)

---

A sentence from today's notes stayed with me longer than the rest: practice should return words to real contexts, not leave them alone on a list.

It sounds like a language-learning tip. It is actually a much larger principle.

Most people think learning means collecting correct answers. Learn a word. Save a quote. Highlight a sentence. Ask AI for an explanation. Put the answer somewhere. Repeat.

This feels productive because the surface is full. There are notes, summaries, flashcards, screenshots, and clean paragraphs. But a strange thing happens when life asks for the knowledge back. The answer is "known," yet it does not come out. Or it comes out stiffly, like a phrase copied from a textbook into a conversation where it does not quite belong.

The problem is not always memory. Often, the problem is missing context.

Knowledge learned without context is like a tool bought without knowing which drawer it belongs in, which problem it solves, and what your hand should feel when using it. You own it, but you cannot reach it at the moment of need.

That is why I increasingly think the real teacher is not the person, the book, or even the AI system.

The real teacher is the context that forces knowledge to become usable.

## A Word Is Not a Word Until It Has a Place to Live

Take a simple word in a new language.

On paper, it looks harmless. A spelling, a pronunciation, a meaning. If you are diligent, you may add a sample sentence. If you are more diligent, you may add three.

But in real life the word never appears alone. It comes with a speaker, a relationship, a tone, a location, a purpose, a level of politeness, a history of what was said before, and an expectation of what should happen next.

This is why vocabulary lists are useful but insufficient. They give you labels. They do not give you coordinates.

Coordinates matter. The 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine went to John O'Keefe, May-Britt Moser, and Edvard Moser for discoveries about place cells and grid cells, the neural machinery behind the brain's positioning system.[^1] I do not want to stretch the neuroscience too far, but as a metaphor for learning it is powerful: the brain is not only a storage device; it is also a mapping device.

We do not merely remember things. We remember where things fit.

That is true for physical space, and it is also true for meaning. A phrase has a place. A concept has a place. A method has a place. A decision rule has a place. If you cannot place it, you probably cannot use it.

This is where many learning systems go wrong. They treat knowledge as items. But the mind uses knowledge as relationships.

When a learner cannot use a phrase, the problem may not be that the phrase has not been repeated enough. The phrase may not yet have enough neighbors. It has not been connected to situations, emotions, mistakes, alternatives, and consequences. It is floating.

Floating knowledge looks impressive in a notebook and disappears in action.

## Context Is Not Decoration

People sometimes misunderstand context as "add a story so it is more interesting."

That is too shallow.

Context is not decoration. Context is retrieval infrastructure.

Tulving and Thomson's 1973 work on encoding specificity argued that memory retrieval depends on the relationship between how information is encoded and the cues available when we try to retrieve it.[^2] In plain language: the conditions around learning are not neutral packaging. They can become part of how the memory is found later.

This explains a lot of ordinary failure.

You read a book in a quiet room and understand it. Then you try to explain the idea during a noisy meeting and suddenly it becomes blurry. You memorize a sentence on an app and recognize it immediately. Then a real person says something similar with a different accent and you freeze. You know a framework in theory. Then a messy case appears, and you cannot tell which part of the framework applies.

The knowledge was there, but the retrieval path was weak.

This is why good teaching often feels inefficient from the outside. A good teacher may use examples, analogies, counterexamples, stories, questions, role play, diagrams, and small corrections that seem to wander away from the "point." But those detours are often where the point becomes usable. They build more roads back to the same idea.

AI can help here, but only if we give it the right job.

If we use AI only to produce cleaner explanations, we may make learning smoother without making it stronger. If we use AI to generate varied contexts, test retrieval, expose mistakes, and force transfer, then it becomes something better: not an answer machine, but a context engine.

That distinction matters.

An answer machine makes you feel finished.

A context engine makes you practice being alive with the knowledge.

## The Old Mistake: Confusing Recognition With Ability

There is a specific trap in modern learning: recognition masquerades as mastery.

You see the answer and think, yes, I know that.

You read the summary and think, yes, I understand.

You watch the tutorial and think, yes, I can do this.

Then you close the tab and reality politely disagrees.

This is not a moral weakness. It is a cognitive design issue. Recognition is easier than retrieval. Re-reading is easier than reconstructing. Nodding is easier than using.

Research on retrieval practice has made this point repeatedly. Karpicke and Roediger reported in Science in 2008 that retrieval practice plays a critical role in consolidating learning, and that learners often underestimate its value.[^3] A broader body of work on distributed practice also shows that spacing learning over time improves retention compared with massed practice; a 2006 meta-analysis reviewed 839 assessments across 317 experiments.[^4]

The practical lesson is simple, but uncomfortable:

If you want to learn something, do not only ask whether you can recognize it.

Ask whether you can bring it back when the surface changes.

Can you explain it without looking? Can you use it in a different example? Can you notice when it does not apply? Can you compare it with a nearby idea? Can you teach it to someone who has a different background? Can you recover it tomorrow, next week, and next month?

That is where AI can be either poison or medicine.

Used poorly, AI removes retrieval. It gives you the answer before your memory has to work. It completes the sentence before your thought has to form. It makes the path so smooth that your own muscles never touch the ground.

Used well, AI multiplies retrieval. It can ask you ten variations of the same question. It can create a dialogue, a case, a scenario, a role-play, a comparison, a near miss. It can make you explain, fail, correct, and try again. It can change the surface while preserving the underlying structure.

The question is not "Should I use AI to learn?"

The better question is: "Is this use of AI replacing retrieval, or multiplying it?"

## A Better Learning Loop

The weak learning loop looks like this:

Capture a fact. Ask AI to summarize it. Save the summary. Feel organized.

The stronger loop looks different:

Start with a real problem. Learn the smallest useful piece. Ask AI to place it in several contexts. Try to retrieve it without looking. Use it in a slightly different situation. Let AI critique the attempt. Correct the misunderstanding. Compress the lesson into your own words. Return to it later.

That loop is slower at first. It creates more friction. It may even feel less intelligent because there is more stumbling.

But the stumbling is the point.

Learning is not the removal of difficulty. Learning is the conversion of difficulty into structure.

When AI removes all difficulty, it may also remove the structure-building process. When AI supplies the right difficulty at the right time, it becomes a training partner.

This is where "using AI to become stronger rather than lazier" stops being a slogan and becomes an operating rule.

Do not ask AI only to give you the answer.

Ask it to build the terrain where the answer can be used.

Ask for situations where the idea is natural, situations where it fails, and one case where you must choose between it and a nearby idea. Ask for the vague claim, the missing evidence, the likely mistake, the failure case, the practice question, and the warning sign that tells you when you are pretending.

AI is most dangerous when it becomes a vending machine for finished thoughts.

It is most useful when it becomes an environment for unfinished practice.

## The Counterargument: Context Can Become Noise

There is a fair objection here.

If context is so important, should we add endless stories, examples, metaphors, and generated scenarios to everything?

No.

Context is useful only when it increases transfer. Otherwise it becomes entertainment.

This is another common mistake. People move from dry memorization to endless "richness," and the learning still fails because the richness is not disciplined. A beautiful story can make an idea memorable while hiding the fact that the learner still cannot use it. A vivid analogy can feel profound while carrying the concept in the wrong direction. AI-generated examples can be fluent and plausible while quietly teaching a mistake.

So context needs constraints.

A good context should do at least one of three things:

It should help you retrieve the idea later.

It should help you distinguish this idea from nearby ideas.

It should help you act differently in a real situation.

If it does none of these, it is probably decoration.

This matters especially with AI because AI can generate infinite decoration. Infinite examples are not the same as good practice. A strong learning system does not ask for more material forever. It asks for the next useful variation, the next error signal, the next test of transfer.

The goal is not abundance.

The goal is alignment between knowledge and use.

## The Quiet Upgrade

The more I work with notes, teaching, writing, and AI systems, the more I feel that the future of learning will not belong to people who collect the most information.

It will belong to people who build the best contexts for becoming the kind of person who can use that information.

That sounds abstract, but it is very concrete.

Your environment teaches you what to notice. Your questions teach you what to retrieve. Your tools teach you what to outsource. Your routines teach you what to repeat. Your mistakes teach you where the map is still blank.

AI enters this system as a force multiplier. It can make a weak loop weaker: more summaries, more passive recognition, more polished forgetting. Or it can make a strong loop stronger: more contexts, more retrieval, more feedback, more transfer.

The difference is not in the model.

The difference is in the learning architecture around the model.

So maybe the practical question for the AI age is not "How do I learn faster?"

Maybe it is:

What kind of context am I building around myself?

Does it make me avoid effort, or does it make effort more precise?

Does it give me answers, or does it train me to recover answers when life changes the question?

Does it make my knowledge look organized, or does it make my knowledge become usable?

A person does not become stronger by owning more answers.

A person becomes stronger when answers grow roots in enough situations that they can be called back, tested, revised, and used.

That is why context is the real teacher.

And that is why AI, used well, should not make learning feel like copying from a smarter machine.

It should make learning feel like stepping into a richer world, where every idea has somewhere to live, somewhere to fail, and somewhere to become yours.
