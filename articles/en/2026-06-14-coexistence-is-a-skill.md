# Coexistence Is a Skill

> 发布日期:2026-06-14 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-14-和 AI 共处是一种需要练习的能力.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-14-coexistence-is-a-skill.md)

---

The easiest sentence to say about AI is also the least useful one:

We must control it.

It sounds responsible. It sounds adult. It suggests a hand on the wheel, a switch on the wall, a clear hierarchy between the human who commands and the machine that obeys.

But the more capable AI becomes, the more I think the word "control" quietly misleads us.

Not because control no longer matters. It matters more than ever. The problem is that many people imagine control as a static possession: once you have the right rule, the right policy, the right prompt, the right kill switch, the right benchmark, the system is under control.

That is not how we live with powerful systems.

We do not "control" electricity by understanding every electron. We live with it through standards, insulation, meters, breakers, habits, training, and consequences. We do not "control" cities from one dashboard. We live with them through roads, signals, inspections, budgets, norms, maps, emergency services, and millions of small adjustments. We do not even fully control our own bodies. We coexist with appetite, fatigue, emotion, memory, injury, aging, and attention through routines.

The serious word is not control.

The serious word is coexistence.

And coexistence is not a soft word. It is not surrender. It is a skill.

## The Superintelligence Debate Has a Practical Lesson

A new Google DeepMind report, *From AGI to ASI*, frames the transition from human-level artificial general intelligence to artificial superintelligence as something that may happen through several pathways: scaling, paradigm shifts, recursive self-improvement, and large-scale multi-agent collectives.[^1] The report is careful about uncertainty. It does not pretend the future has one clean line. It talks about possible frictions, bottlenecks, and open research questions.

That uncertainty is exactly the point.

If AI progress were a single product launch, control could look like a gate. Before the launch, inspect. After the launch, approve or reject.

But if progress arrives as a moving ecology of models, tools, agents, workflows, datasets, organizations, users, incentives, and feedback loops, then control cannot be one gate. It becomes an ongoing relationship with a system that changes while we are learning how to relate to it.

This is why "coexistence" is the better frame.

Coexistence asks a harder question than "Can I stop it?"

It asks: what practices let humans remain capable, responsible, and awake while living beside a form of intelligence that is faster, cheaper to copy, easier to scale, and increasingly embedded in our work?

The answer cannot be only technical. It has to be technical, personal, organizational, and cultural at the same time.

## Coexistence Is Not Letting Go

Some people hear "coexistence" and think it means giving up. Let the machine do what it wants. Accept whatever happens. Become peaceful spectators to our own replacement.

That is the wrong reading.

Coexistence is not passivity. It is disciplined participation.

A parent does not control a child the way a factory controls a machine. But good parenting is not absence. It is boundaries, example, conversation, repair, patience, repeated explanation, and the painful art of allowing more autonomy without abandoning responsibility.

A pilot does not control the weather. But flying is not surrendering to weather. It is reading instruments, respecting limits, adjusting altitude, delaying when necessary, and knowing when confidence has become arrogance.

A teacher does not control whether a student will truly learn. But teaching is not helplessness. It is designing conditions where learning becomes more likely: questions, examples, practice, correction, rhythm, trust.

This is the kind of control AI will increasingly require: less like holding a leash, more like maintaining an ecosystem of constraints and feedback.

The mistake is to think that if control is not total, it is fake.

Most real control has never been total.

It has always been partial, situated, practiced, and renewed.

## The Hidden Risk Is Human De-skilling

When people talk about AI risk, they often imagine dramatic failure: deception, runaway autonomy, malicious misuse, catastrophic bugs.

Those risks deserve serious work. NIST's AI Risk Management Framework, released in January 2023, organizes that work around functions such as governing, mapping, measuring, and managing AI risks.[^2] That language is useful because it treats risk management as a lifecycle, not a slogan.

But there is another risk that is quieter and easier to miss.

AI may not need to defeat human judgment. We may stop training it.

If every hard first draft is outsourced, the ability to form a first draft weakens. If every decision is pre-chewed by a model, the habit of deciding weakens. If every question is answered before we have sat with it, the muscle that tolerates confusion weakens. If every small tool is generated without anyone understanding its assumptions, our environment fills with invisible dependencies.

This is not because AI is evil. It is because comfort is persuasive.

The first time AI removes friction, it feels like liberation. The tenth time, it becomes expectation. The hundredth time, a skill you used to practice may have disappeared without ceremony.

That is why coexistence has to include human strengthening.

The goal is not to keep humans busy with pointless labor. The goal is to keep humans in contact with the kinds of difficulty that produce judgment.

Good AI use removes dead friction: formatting, searching, repetitive conversion, mechanical summarization, low-value coordination.

Bad AI use removes living friction: wrestling with a real question, checking an assumption, choosing a tradeoff, noticing that a beautiful answer does not fit the situation, taking responsibility for a result.

The difference is everything.

## A Personal Operating System for Coexistence

For an individual, coexistence with AI does not begin with philosophy. It begins with a loop.

Before using AI, name the work:

What am I trying to make better?

What must remain my responsibility?

What would count as evidence that this helped?

After using AI, inspect the change:

Did I understand more, or merely produce more?

Did the output reveal a better question, or did it let me avoid one?

Did I keep the final judgment, or did I decorate the machine's judgment with my signature?

This sounds slow, but it is not. It is like brushing your teeth. The loop becomes cheap once it becomes a habit.

I like a simple three-part rule.

First: let AI expand the search space.

Ask it for alternatives, counterarguments, analogies, examples, edge cases, missing assumptions, and different framings. This is where machines are wonderful. They give breadth at a price that would have been unimaginable a few years ago.

Second: keep the compression human.

The final "this matters because..." should come from you. The choice of what to keep, what to delete, what to risk, what to publish, what to promise, what to refuse, and what to stand behind should not be outsourced. That compression is where character enters the work.

Third: make the loop visible.

Write down the decision, the assumption, the test, the failure, the revision. A visible loop can improve. An invisible loop becomes superstition.

This is where AI can make a person stronger. Not by replacing effort, but by turning effort into a system that can learn.

## Organizations Need the Same Discipline

The same pattern applies to teams and companies.

The weak version of AI adoption is: give everyone access, celebrate usage, count prompts, and call it transformation.

The stronger version asks different questions.

Where is AI allowed to act automatically?

Where must a human review the work?

Which failures are acceptable because they are cheap and reversible?

Which failures are unacceptable because they damage trust, privacy, safety, or legal responsibility?

How do we know whether the system is getting better?

Who owns the decision when the model was involved?

These questions are less glamorous than a demo. They are also where the real work begins.

An AI-native organization is not one where humans vanish. It is one where humans design better loops: clearer goals, sharper constraints, faster feedback, stronger memory, lower coordination cost, and more explicit responsibility.

The organization does not become smarter because it bought intelligence by the token.

It becomes smarter only if it learns how to absorb intelligence without dissolving accountability.

## The Future Will Reward People Who Can Stay Awake

The future may bring systems far more capable than today's models. It may also bring disappointment, bottlenecks, regulation, economic limits, and many uneven transitions. We should be humble about prediction.

But one thing already seems clear.

AI will keep increasing the gap between people who build learning loops and people who merely consume convenience.

The first group will use AI to ask better questions, test faster, remember more, build small tools, compare perspectives, rehearse difficult conversations, and turn experience into systems.

The second group will use AI to avoid the very discomfort that would have made them stronger.

Both groups will look productive for a while.

Only one will compound.

This is why "coexistence" is not a retreat from ambition. It is a higher standard for ambition. It says: if we are going to live beside intelligence that can scale beyond us in many directions, then we must become more deliberate about what we practice, what we protect, what we automate, and what we keep close.

The old fantasy of control says: build a machine powerful enough to obey.

The better question is: can we build humans and institutions strong enough to live with power?

For me, that is the real test of the AI age.

Not whether the machine becomes astonishing. It will.

The test is whether we become more awake beside it.
