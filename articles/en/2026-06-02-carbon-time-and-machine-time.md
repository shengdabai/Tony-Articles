# Carbon Time Is Capped. Machine Time Isn't.

> 发布日期 · Published: 2026-06-02 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-02-碳基时间有限归机时间无限.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-02-carbon-time-and-machine-time.md)

---

In May 2026 I published two English books on Amazon. I am not a native English writer, and I have never studied political science. The second book is a 440-source study of how organizations arrange things so that, when something goes wrong, no one is to blame. From first outline to the page going live, it took seven days.

I want to be exact about what that sentence claims and what it doesn't. It does not say I got faster at writing. It says I did something I could not have done at all — not in seven days, not in seven years. A book in a language I don't write natively, in a field I never trained in. The interesting part isn't the speed. It's that a boundary moved.

That is the whole shift. And most people are staring at the wrong half of it.

---

## The two clocks

Each of us now runs on two clocks.

The first is **carbon time** — your own physical hours. You get twenty-four of them a day. You cannot buy more, you cannot store them, and you cannot run two at once. Carbon time was capped the day you were born, and no tool in history has raised the cap.

The second is **machine time** — the hours an AI works on your behalf. It runs while you sleep. It never tires. And here is the part that breaks the old intuition: you can run it in parallel. Ten agents, a hundred, a thousand, each on its own thread, all at the same moment. Carbon time is a single candle. Machine time is a sky you can keep adding stars to.

So here is the claim I have come to believe, stated plainly: from here on, ninety-nine percent of what any of us achieves will come from machine time, not carbon time.

If that is even close to true, then the most valuable thing you can build is not a skill. It is the habit of converting your scarce carbon hours into compounding machine hours.

## The wrong way to hold a telescope

The most common mistake is also the most natural one. People use AI to do — a little faster — the exact thing they were already doing. Write the email they'd have written anyway. Summarize the report they'd have read anyway. It feels like progress. It is almost nothing.

In 1609 Galileo heard that someone in Holland had put two lenses in a tube and made far things look near.[^1] Within weeks he had built his own, sharper version and turned it on the night sky. He did not use it to read street signs from a little farther off. On 30 November 1609 he pointed it at the Moon and found mountains and craters where everyone "knew" there was a smooth, perfect sphere; weeks later he found four moons circling Jupiter.[^2] The telescope's gift was never *seeing the same things bigger*. It was seeing what had been, until that night, completely invisible.

AI is a telescope for the mind, and it just walked into your study. If you aim it at the work you can already do, you have aimed a telescope at the page in your own hand. The test of whether you're using it right is brutally simple: *am I doing something I literally could not do before?* When I use it to write a book in a language I don't command, in a field I never studied — that is the lens pointed at the sky.

## A hundred brains

Here is how that second book actually got made, because the method is the point.

I did not write it. I assembled a team — more than a dozen AI agents, each a narrow specialist. One, which I called Nancy, did nothing but check that every quotation complied with U.S. publishing law. Another, The Reader, hunted for places where I'd used a concept without explaining it, or simply repeated myself. A third did nothing but research modern war cases. Change one section, and the relevant agents woke up and re-ran themselves. The total labor was something like a dozen people working without rest for a year and a half. It took me seven days.

Nature has run this exact trick for millions of years. Thomas Seeley, a biologist at Cornell, spent decades watching how a honeybee swarm chooses a new home.[^3] Several hundred scout bees fan out, each inspecting a candidate site, and each returns to perform a waggle dance whose vigor is honest — about a hundred dance-circuits for an excellent site, a dozen for a mediocre one.[^4] No single bee compares all the options. No bee needs to. The swarm, as a whole, reliably lands on the best site. Each small head holds one slice; the colony holds the decision.

That is exactly what machine time buys you. Not a faster you. A *distributed* you. The honest definition: machine time is not AI doing your work for you. It is you having a hundred brains at once, each chewing on a different piece of the same problem.

## You stop writing the code

In August 2025 I made a decision that sounded extreme even to me: I would never again write a single line of code by hand. Not one. I would build software only by instructing AI in plain language from the command line.

This is older than it sounds. In 1952 Grace Hopper built the A-0, widely credited as the first compiler.[^5] Before it, programmers fed machines raw binary, or punched holes in paper tape. Hopper's idea — write in something a human can read, and let the machine translate it down — was met with three years of "computers can only do arithmetic."[^6] She was right. And every layer since has repeated her move: machine code, then high-level languages, then — now — natural language. Each rung freed the human from a layer of detail and pushed them one step up.

Refusing to write code is not laziness. It is standing on the next rung. The detail of *how* a function gets written is something the machine already does better than I do, and will only do better. What it frees me to spend my carbon hours on is the part it cannot reach: deciding what to build, shaping the argument, checking whether the logic actually holds.

## The gap that compounds

Nine months in, the change I did not expect is not in my output. It is in my head.

Things that used to look flat now look networked. I can see how a 5th-century ritual, a 17th-century lens, and a 1952 compiler are the same shape. That did not happen because I got smarter in carbon time. It happened because machine time let me hold dozens of investigations open at once, and the connections between them became visible.

This is why the metaphor that fits machine time is not "tool" but **capital**. Leverage is using resources beyond your own to amplify what you can do. Distributed computing is splitting one large problem across many processors running in parallel. Compound interest is a series of small, smart choices returning far more than their sum. Machine time is all three at once — and like all three, it accumulates. The person who banked machine hours this year begins next year with brains already running.

Which means the gap between people is about to widen without limit. Not along the old axis of raw talent or hours logged. Along a new one: how much machine time you have learned to command. Training your own ability to use AI is, right now, the highest-return investment available to you.

## The fork, and the honest limits

I won't pretend the default path bends toward growth. For most people it bends the other way. AI will quietly make them more productive and less capable at the same time — doing the same old job a little faster, outsourcing each judgment until the muscle that makes judgments has wasted away. Faster, and emptier.

And I have to be honest about machine time's own failure mode, because my second book is literally about it. Its subject is what the philosopher René Girard called the scapegoat mechanism — the ancient human habit of restoring order by pinning the blame on a single expelled victim, while everyone stays blind to what they are doing.[^7] In the age of the agent, that danger wears a new face: when a hundred machines do everything, who is responsible when it goes wrong? "No one did it" becomes the default answer. Machine time with no carbon-time judgment steering it does not produce achievement. It produces fast, confident, unaccountable garbage.

So the two clocks are not rivals. Carbon time is where judgment, taste, and responsibility live — the scarce human signal. Machine time is the amplifier. An amplifier with no signal is just noise, louder.

One more limit, stated flat. This is one person's nine months. The taste I use to steer those hundred brains was built over years of carbon time before any of this began. Hand the same dozen agents to someone with no direction of their own, and you will not get a book in seven days. You will get a thousand pages of nothing, faster. What would change my mind is watching machine time produce real work in the hands of someone who never put in the carbon years first. I have not seen it yet.

## What to carry out of here

Forget the productivity tips. There is one question worth keeping.

*What is your ratio of carbon time to machine time — and which way is it moving?*

If your carbon hours go into reacting, into doing by hand what a machine could do while you sleep, then your ratio is stuck and your ceiling is your own twenty-four hours, forever. If your carbon hours go into pointing the telescope at things you genuinely could not do before — and into building the teams of brains that do them — then you are converting a capped resource into an uncapped one. You are compounding.

The telescope was never about seeing the same things bigger. It was about seeing the sky.

Point it up.

## In Short

You run on two clocks. Carbon time is your own twenty-four hours — finite, un-expandable, capped at birth. Machine time is the work AI does for you — tireless, parallel, effectively unlimited. The biggest mistake is using AI to do your old job a little faster; that is a telescope pointed at the page in your hand. Used right, AI lets you do what you genuinely could not do before, and gives you a hundred specialized "brains" working at once — the way a beehive decides with hundreds of scouts. But machine time is only an amplifier. Carbon time is where judgment, taste, and responsibility live, and an amplifier with no signal is just louder noise. The gap between people will now widen along one axis: how much machine time you have learned to command. So ask yourself one thing — what is your ratio of carbon time to machine time, and which way is it moving?
