# Productize the Judgment

> 发布日期:2026-07-01 · [中文](../zh/2026-07-01-别追流量了把判断做成产品.md) | [English](../en/2026-07-01-productize-the-judgment.md)

---

The most exhausting trap in a crowded market is not competition.

It is the belief that more attention will save you.

When a field becomes crowded, the first instinct is usually to shout louder. Publish more. Stream more. Post more. Buy more traffic. Make the offer sound sharper. Add urgency. Add testimonials. Add another hook. If yesterday's message did not work, write ten more today.

This can work for a while. Attention is real. Distribution matters. Nobody buys from a person they cannot find.

But there is a point where attention stops being leverage and becomes rent. You wake up each day needing to pay again. If the platform slows down, you slow down. If the algorithm changes, your confidence changes. If a competitor copies your angle, you have to run faster just to stay in the same place.

The strange thing is that many people call this entrepreneurship.

Sometimes it is only dependence with better language.

Today's note reminded me of a more interesting move: instead of using AI to produce more content for the same red ocean, use AI to rebuild the product so the customer no longer has to buy your time in the old way.

That sounds simple.

It is not.

It asks a harder question than "How do I get more traffic?"

It asks: "What part of my judgment can become a product?"

## The red ocean is often a delivery problem

We usually describe a red ocean as a market full of competitors. That is true, but incomplete.

Many red oceans are crowded because everyone is selling the same delivery structure.

The visible category may be consulting, education, design, operations, coaching, writing, planning, research, diagnosis, or any other knowledge service. But under the surface, the model is often identical:

The customer has a messy situation.

The expert uses experience to interpret the mess.

The output is delivered through meetings, documents, calls, messages, or custom reports.

The expert's time is the bottleneck.

Because time is scarce, the price must stay high, the number of customers must stay limited, and the seller must keep proving why this particular human is worth choosing over other humans. That is where the traffic treadmill begins. If the product is basically "my personal attention applied to your case", then every new customer requires a new act of trust.

So the market becomes noisy.

Not because everyone is stupid.

Because everyone is trying to sell human judgment through a channel that does not scale well.

The classic idea of blue ocean strategy is not simply "find an empty market". It is the simultaneous pursuit of differentiation and lower cost, creating new demand instead of fighting only over existing demand.[^1] In other words, a blue ocean is not a place you discover like an island. It is often a new value structure you build inside an old market.

AI matters here because it can change the value structure.

Not by replacing judgment.

By making judgment distributable.

## Do not automate the surface. Automate the shape

The lazy version of AI adoption is to automate the surface of the old business.

If the old business needed articles, generate more articles.

If it needed sales scripts, generate more scripts.

If it needed reports, generate longer reports.

If it needed customer replies, generate faster replies.

This can reduce cost, but it rarely changes the game. It may even make the red ocean redder, because everyone else can use the same tools to produce the same surface faster.

The deeper move is to automate the shape of the expert's judgment.

A good expert does not merely "know things". A good expert has a sequence:

What information matters?

Which signals are noise?

What questions expose the real constraint?

Which options are impossible because of hidden trade-offs?

What is the customer probably misunderstanding?

Where does the customer need a recommendation, and where do they need a map?

What should be customized, and what should be standardized?

This sequence is usually invisible. It lives in the expert's head, in habits, in memory, in pattern recognition, in years of small mistakes.

The AI opportunity is to drag that sequence into the open.

Turn it into intake questions. Turn it into a scoring model. Turn it into a decision tree. Turn it into a report structure. Turn it into a set of examples. Turn it into a review loop. Turn it into a product that can produce a useful first answer for people who could never afford the full custom service.

That is not "AI content".

That is judgment packaging.

And judgment packaging is much harder to copy than a post.

## Cheap does not have to mean shallow

There is a fear among experts that productization makes their work cheap.

Sometimes it does. A bad productized service is just a template with a payment link. It removes the human, but it does not preserve the intelligence. It gives the customer a cheaper experience by giving them a thinner experience.

That is not the point.

The better version is different: use AI to lower the cost of access while keeping the core logic alive.

McKinsey estimated in 2023 that generative AI could create $2.6 trillion to $4.4 trillion in annual value, with about three-quarters of that value concentrated in customer operations, marketing and sales, software engineering, and R&D.[^2] The useful part of that estimate is not the giant number. Giant numbers are easy to admire and hard to act on.

The useful part is the pattern.

AI has the most leverage where language, judgment, process, and repeated variation meet.

That is exactly where many small expert businesses live.

The old expert business says: "Pay me a lot, and I will think about your case."

The productized judgment business says: "Here is a structured version of how I think. It will not replace deep custom work, but it will give many more people a real first step."

This can create a new layer of demand.

Some people still need the full expert. Some people never could afford the expert. Some people are not ready for the full service yet, but they are ready for a diagnosis, a map, a draft plan, a simulation, or a decision aid.

When done well, the lower-priced product does not cannibalize the high-touch service. It educates the market, filters better customers, and lets people experience the expert's thinking before buying the expert's time.

That is not discounting.

That is architecture.

## The product is not the PDF

A common mistake is to think the product is the visible output.

The PDF. The dashboard. The report. The checklist. The bot. The portal.

Those things matter, but they are containers. The real product is the customer's movement from confusion to a better next action.

If a report is beautiful but does not change the customer's decision, it is decoration.

If a chatbot answers quickly but does not ask the question the customer was avoiding, it is theater.

If a dashboard has twenty charts but no hierarchy, it is just anxiety with colors.

The product is not information. Information is cheap.

The product is shaped judgment.

This is why the builder has to stay close to real customers. You cannot productize judgment by sitting alone and turning your old knowledge into templates. You have to watch where people misunderstand, where they hesitate, where they ask the wrong question, where they need confidence, where they need friction, where they need a warning.

AI can help generate the object.

It cannot decide what deserves to become the object.

That choice still belongs to the human.

## Traffic is proof of reach, not proof of value

I am not arguing against distribution.

A product nobody sees is a private hobby. A useful thing still needs a route to the people it can help.

But traffic should be treated as proof of reach, not proof of value.

If more attention is the only thing keeping the business alive, the business is not yet engineered. It is exposed.

The healthier question is:

If traffic dropped tomorrow, what asset would remain?

Would there be a diagnostic tool?

A useful dataset?

A repeatable workflow?

A set of customer cases that made the product smarter?

A community that trusts the method?

A product that can deliver value before the founder appears on camera?

A system that learns from every delivery?

If the answer is no, then AI should not be used to make more noise. It should be used to build the missing asset.

This is the difference between using AI as a megaphone and using AI as a machine shop.

A megaphone amplifies the same dependency.

A machine shop changes what you are able to build.

## The zero class builds leverage without leaving the work

This connects to something I keep circling back to: using AI to make people stronger, not lazier.

The lazy path is to ask AI to produce more surface area so we can avoid the uncomfortable work of product thinking.

The stronger path is to ask AI to help expose the hidden structure of our own judgment.

Why do I recommend A instead of B?

What do I check first?

What do beginners always miss?

What can be safely standardized?

What must remain human?

What should the customer learn from the process, even if they do not buy the premium service?

These questions are not glamorous. They are not viral. They do not look like a breakthrough demo.

But they are where durable leverage begins.

The person who can answer them is no longer only a service provider. They are becoming a system designer.

They still care about craft. They still respect the customer. They still do the hard cases by hand when necessary. But they are not trapped in the idea that every unit of value must pass through their calendar.

That is the real promise.

Not "AI will do my work."

"AI will help me turn my work into a system that more people can use."

## A quieter definition of scale

Scale is usually imagined as bigger: more users, more revenue, more traffic, more staff, more output.

There is another kind of scale that matters more for a person building with AI:

Can your best thinking travel farther without becoming stupid?

That is the test.

If your judgment travels farther but becomes vague, you have content.

If it travels farther but becomes rigid, you have bureaucracy.

If it travels farther but still helps a real person make a better decision, you have a product.

The future does not belong only to people who get the most attention. Attention is too expensive, too unstable, too easy to rent and too hard to own.

The future belongs to people who can turn lived judgment into usable systems.

Not because systems are colder than humans.

Because a good system is how a human stops being trapped inside one-to-one delivery forever.

So if you are in a crowded market, maybe the answer is not to post harder.

Maybe the answer is to ask what your customers repeatedly need from your mind, then build a small machine that gives them the first honest version of it.

Do not chase traffic forever.

Productize the judgment.

That is how a red ocean begins to change color.
