# Your Brain Holds Seven Things. Experts Just Pack Them Better.

> 发布日期:2026-06-04 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-04-你的大脑只有7个格子高手赢在会打包.md) | English

---

This morning I spent seventy minutes teaching a beginner in Rome how to say where things are in Chinese. Up and down, left and right, front and back, inside and outside, the four compass points. Twelve little words, and he kept dropping them on the floor — remember four, lose three, reach for the fifth and the first two were already gone.

So we stopped memorizing and started grouping. Up-down is one pair. Left-right is one pair. Front-back, inside-outside, two more pairs. The compass is one set. Suddenly twelve loose items became five small packages, and he could carry all five across the room without spilling a thing.

I didn't teach him Chinese in that moment. I taught him the single trick that separates everyone who is good at anything from everyone who isn't. And the strange part is that the trick exists because of a hard physical limit in the human skull — one that no amount of effort, talent, or willpower has ever moved.

## The integer that persecuted a Harvard psychologist

In 1956, the cognitive psychologist George Miller published a paper with one of the most famous opening lines in all of science. "My problem," he wrote, "is that I have been persecuted by an integer. For seven years this number has followed me around, has intruded in my most private data, and has assaulted me from the pages of our most public journals."

The number was seven. Everywhere Miller looked, the human mind seemed to hit a wall right around there. We can hold about seven things in working memory at once — plus or minus two. Show someone a string of random digits and most people top out around seven before the front of the line falls off the back. It is one of the most stubborn findings in psychology. Seventy years later, the wall is still seven.

But here is the part almost everyone misses, the part Miller spent his career trying to explain. The limit is not on *information*. It is on *chunks*.

Take the letters F-B-I-C-I-A-U-S-A. Read one at a time, that is nine items — already past the wall, already hopeless. But the moment you see FBI, CIA, USA, it collapses into three. Three packages instead of nine pieces. The wall hasn't moved. You just stopped throwing pebbles at it and started throwing three neatly tied bundles. Same seven slots. Wildly different cargo.

Miller's contemporaries proved this was trainable, not innate. One of them, Sidney Smith, taught himself to recode strings of binary digits — ones and zeros — into larger groups, two digits becoming a number, then three, then four. With nothing changed in his biology, he pushed his memory span for raw bits from a handful up into the dozens. Same brain. Bigger chunks.

## Why the chess master forgets

If you want to see how far this goes, watch what happens when a chess master and a beginner stare at the same board.

In a landmark 1973 study, the psychologists William Chase and Herbert Simon showed players a real game position for five seconds, then swept the pieces away and asked them to rebuild it. The masters reconstructed it almost perfectly. The beginners managed a few pieces. The obvious conclusion — masters have better memories — is wrong, and the experiment was designed to prove it wrong.

Because then they did it again with the pieces scattered at random, in arrangements that could never occur in a real game. And the master's advantage simply vanished. On a nonsense board, the grandmaster remembered no more than the novice.

The master was never holding twenty pieces. He was holding five or six familiar patterns — a castled king, a pawn chain, a known opening shape — each one a single chunk that happened to contain four pieces. Strip away the meaning and the patterns dissolve, and he is back to seven slots like the rest of us. Chase and Simon estimated that reaching that level takes somewhere between fifty thousand and a hundred thousand of these stored patterns. That is what a decade of practice actually builds. Not a faster mind. A library of bigger packages.

This reframes everything we lazily call talent. The expert is not someone who broke the seven-slot limit. Nobody breaks it. The expert is someone who spent years quietly compressing their field into chunks so dense that seven of them hold what would crush a beginner. Expertise is a compression system you grow inside your own head, one hard-won pattern at a time.

## What AI compresses, and what it can't

Now hold that idea next to the machine on your desk, because something uncanny rhymes here.

AI compresses too. A language model doesn't store the world as raw text; it crushes it into tokens and embeddings — dense numerical packages where related ideas sit close together. Compression is not a side effect of how these systems work. It is the whole engine. In a real sense, the model and the master are doing the same thing: turning a flood of detail into a small number of meaningful units, then operating on the units instead of the flood.

Which raises the question that should keep you up at night. If the machine can chunk, and chunk far faster and far wider than any human ever will, what exactly is left for the seven slots in your skull?

The answer is the most important distinction of this decade, and it hides in one word: *whose*.

The model's chunks are the model's. They live in its weights, available to anyone who types a prompt, identical for you and the next person and the ten thousand after that. Your chunks are yours. They are the patterns *you* recognize instantly because *you* earned them — the bug you can smell before you read the stack trace, the shape of a deal that is about to go wrong, the wrong note in a sentence three drafts before anyone else hears it. The machine can rent you its compression. It cannot install yours.

I felt this directly not long ago. I'd spent over a decade building a mental model of how version control works — a concept I carry as a single chunk, the way the chess master carries a pawn chain. When AI took over the actual typing, the file edits, the command syntax, the operational layer underneath, I didn't get dumber. I got faster, because all that freed-up capacity flowed straight to the layer where my chunks live. AI handled the operations. I danced on the concepts. The reason a person could one day juggle fifty thousand ideas without exhaustion is that the machine carries the operational weight, and the human spends every slot on the patterns only a human built.

## The choice the machine hands you

Here is the fork, and almost nobody notices they are standing at it.

Use AI to outsource your thinking, and you stop laying down chunks. You ask it the question instead of building the pattern that would have answered it. You feel lighter — and you are lighter, the way an unused muscle is lighter. The compression system in your head quietly stops growing. That is what using AI to become lazier actually costs: not this week's output, but the patterns you will never own.

Use AI to outsource the *operations* — the typing, the boilerplate, the mechanical bottom layer — and you free the seven slots to do what only they can. You spend your scarce attention compressing your field faster than you ever could alone, building bigger and denser chunks, climbing toward the fifty thousand. The machine doesn't replace your library. It clears your desk so you can fill it.

The seven-slot wall was here long before the first computer and it will be here long after the last model is retired. It is the one specification of the human mind that does not get an upgrade. Everything that has ever looked like genius — the master, the prodigy, the person who makes the hard thing look easy — has been someone quietly winning the only game the wall allows: not holding more, but packing it tighter.

That game is still yours to play. It is, in fact, the one game the machine can't play for you. It can hand you its packages all day long. It can never grow yours.

So the real question is no longer how much you can hold. It never was. The question is what you are packing into your seven — and whether, in an age that will gladly think for you, you are still bothering to build chunks of your own.
