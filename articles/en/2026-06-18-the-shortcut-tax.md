# The Shortcut Tax

> 发布日期:2026-06-18 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-18-捷径税.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-18-the-shortcut-tax.md)

---

Today's notes kept pointing to the same uncomfortable pattern.

The tempting option is usually the easy one: the cheap route, the one-click route, the unofficial route, the "let the model handle it" route, the workflow that feels done because the first output looks polished.

But the easy path often does not remove the cost. It only moves the cost to a place where you notice it later.

I want to call that cost the shortcut tax.

It is the interest you pay when you choose the path that feels frictionless now but makes the system harder to trust, debug, repeat, or grow later.

This is not only a software problem. It appears in learning, writing, business, personal workflows, and the way we use AI. AI makes it more visible because it makes the first step so cheap. You can generate the page, draft the proposal, summarize the meeting, analyze the file, and produce the image. The artifact appears before reality has charged you the bill.

That feeling is powerful. It is also dangerous.

The real question is not whether a shortcut works once.

The real question is: what does it make harder the second, third, and tenth time?

## The Wrong Path Now Looks Professional

Before AI, many shortcuts looked ugly.

A rushed document looked rushed. A shallow analysis looked thin. A half-built process exposed its cracks early. The friction was annoying, but it acted like a warning light.

AI changes that.

Now a weak idea can arrive in clean paragraphs. A fragile workflow can produce a polished demo. Missing understanding can be covered by confident language. A bad process can be hidden behind speed.

So the AI era does not simply reward faster people. It rewards people who can tell the difference between removed friction and hidden debt.

Some friction is dead weight: formatting, file conversion, repetitive cleanup, basic search, first-pass summarization, resizing images, extracting tables, writing a rough draft. If AI removes that friction, good. That is what tools are for.

But some friction is load-bearing.

Understanding the source of a claim. Checking whether an output survives a real use case. Knowing what the user actually needs. Protecting privacy. Choosing the slower but stable path. Turning a clever trick into a repeatable workflow.

When AI removes low-value friction, we become stronger.

When AI hides high-value friction, we become fragile.

The hard part is that both feel like speed at the beginning.

## Debt Is Not Evil. Unnamed Debt Is.

Software people already have a name for one version of this problem: technical debt.

Ward Cunningham introduced the debt metaphor in the early 1990s to explain that shipping immature code can be like borrowing against the future. A little debt may help you move faster, but if you do not pay it back, future changes become slower and more expensive.[^1]

That metaphor escaped software because it describes something broader than code.

You can have learning debt: you collect explanations but never test yourself, so the knowledge looks familiar and collapses under use.

You can have workflow debt: you finish a task once by manual patchwork, then repeat the same rescue every week.

You can have trust debt: you use a source, vendor, channel, or method that you cannot inspect, then act surprised when the system fails at the worst moment.

You can have attention debt: you let every task become urgent, then lose the quiet needed to build anything durable.

The debt metaphor matters because it removes fake purity.

Not every shortcut is evil. Sometimes you borrow because the situation is real. You need to ship the prototype, survive the week, answer the client, test the idea, or keep momentum.

The problem is not borrowing.

The problem is pretending borrowing is income.

A shortcut becomes dangerous when you treat it as proof that the underlying cost disappeared.

It did not. It moved.

## Correct Does Not Mean Complicated

One sentence from the notes stayed with me:

Do the correct thing, not the simple thing.

This can be misunderstood. It does not mean we should worship complexity. It does not mean the more painful path is automatically better. It does not mean every long checklist is wise.

Correct does not mean complicated.

Correct means the cost is visible.

A correct workflow makes it clear where the data came from, what changed, how the result can be checked, what assumptions were made, and what should happen when something fails.

A correct learning method makes it clear whether you can actually use the idea, not merely recognize it.

A correct AI setup makes it clear what the model can see, what is private, what is logged, what is repeatable, and what human judgment still owns.

The simple thing often says: "Don't worry. It worked."

The correct thing asks: "Worked under what conditions, at what cost, and can we trust it again?"

That second sentence is less exciting. It is also the beginning of adulthood.

Fred Brooks made a related argument in his 1986 essay "No Silver Bullet." His point was not that software cannot improve. His point was that some complexity is essential to the problem itself, so no single tool or technique can magically erase it.[^2]

AI removes enormous amounts of accidental complexity. That is real. But it does not remove the essential complexity of knowing what should be done, why it should be done, who it affects, and how failure should be handled.

If anything, AI pushes more of that burden back onto the human.

The machine can make more possible. The human must become better at deciding what is worth making possible.

## The Tax Comes Late

The cruel thing about the shortcut tax is timing.

At the beginning, the shortcut usually wins. It is faster, cheaper, easier to show, and emotionally satisfying.

The tax comes later.

It comes when you need to reproduce the result and cannot remember the steps.

It comes when an output looks right but nobody can verify it.

It comes when a tool breaks and you do not know which layer failed.

It comes when a private boundary was ignored because convenience felt harmless.

It comes when you have twenty little hacks, none of which can be taught, delegated, or improved.

It comes when you realize you did not build a system. You built a habit of rescue.

This matters for anyone using AI to become stronger. AI can make you feel like you are gaining ability when you are only gaining finished outputs. The file exists, the page exists, the summary exists. But did your judgment improve? Did your process improve? Did your future self inherit something reusable?

If not, the machine did the task and you paid the shortcut tax in growth.

Bloom's 1984 "2 Sigma Problem" is useful here. One-to-one tutoring was powerful not because the tutor delivered more answers, but because the learner got personalized instruction, feedback, correction, and a path toward mastery.[^3] The expensive part was not information. The expensive part was the loop.

AI gives us a chance to make that loop cheaper.

But only if we use it as a tutor, reviewer, simulator, critic, and workflow builder.

If we use it only as an answer machine, we skip the loop and keep the illusion.

## Make the Correct Path Cheaper

The answer is not to reject shortcuts forever. Tools exist to remove unnecessary labor. A good system should make the right thing easier, not turn every task into a ceremony.

The better question is:

Can we make the correct path cheaper than the lazy path?

If you keep making the same mistake, build a checklist.

If you keep forgetting context, write a short project rule.

If you keep asking AI the same question, turn the good version into a reusable prompt or script.

If you keep doubting outputs, define a verification step.

If you keep losing decisions, keep a decision log.

If a workflow depends on your memory, externalize it.

If a tool is useful but hard for others to adopt, explain it in human language.

If a process creates privacy risk, redesign the boundary before speed becomes addiction.

This is close to the spirit of the PDSA cycle: Plan, Do, Study, Act. The Deming Institute describes it as a systematic process for learning and continual improvement of a product, process, or service.[^4] The important word is not "process." The important word is "learning."

A correct system is not one that never fails.

A correct system is one that learns from failure without depending on panic.

AI can help enormously here. It can turn repeated work into templates, scripts, tests, checklists, reports, diagrams, and routines. It can ask the boring questions you avoid. It can compare today's decision with yesterday's rule. It can make hidden steps visible.

But you must aim it at the system, not only at the output.

Most people ask: "Can AI finish this for me?"

A stronger question is: "Can AI help me make this kind of work easier, safer, and more repeatable next time?"

That is the difference between saving time and compounding time.

## Fast Is Fine. Lying Is Not.

There is one serious objection: if we become too obsessed with correctness, we may kill exploration.

That is true. Many good things begin as hacks. A prototype should not carry the weight of a mature system. A first experiment should be allowed to be ugly.

The point is not to remove roughness. The point is to label it honestly.

A temporary shortcut says: "This is a test. We will throw it away or harden it later."

A hidden dependency says: "This is good enough," while quietly becoming part of the foundation.

Exploration is healthy when it has an exit.

Debt is healthy when it has a repayment plan.

Speed is healthy when it improves contact with reality.

What damages people is not moving fast. What damages people is moving fast while lying to themselves about what has been proven.

AI makes this lie easier because the artifact looks finished.

So every fast output should carry a label in our own mind:

Is this a draft, a test, a reusable asset, a public claim, a private note, a client deliverable, or an operational dependency?

Different labels require different standards.

Many failures come from using draft standards for dependency-level work.

## The Five Questions

When a path feels easy, I want to ask five questions:

What cost did this remove?

What cost did this hide?

If it fails, where will I see the failure?

If it works, can I repeat it without remembering everything?

Did this make me stronger, or did it only make the output appear?

These questions are not glamorous. But they protect compounding.

They help preserve the part of AI that is genuinely liberating: removing dead labor, widening exploration, speeding up feedback, and turning personal experience into reusable systems.

And they help avoid the part that quietly weakens us: accepting finished-looking things before we have earned understanding.

The shortcut tax is paid by future you.

Sometimes future you can afford it.

Often, future you cannot.

So the mature move is not to reject ease. It is to earn ease.

Use AI to make the correct thing easier. Use it to build the checklist, write the test, compare the source, record the decision, package the workflow, expose the assumption, and turn the once-clever trick into something stable enough to trust.

The goal is not to be slow.

The goal is to stop confusing hidden cost with progress.

In the AI era, the strongest people will not be the ones who always choose the hardest road. That is just another form of vanity.

They will be the people who can tell which friction should die and which friction is protecting the future.

They will use machines to remove the first kind.

And they will have the discipline to respect the second.
