# The Work Is in the Middle Layer

> 发布日期:2026-06-30 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-30-真正的工作在中间层.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-30-the-work-is-in-the-middle-layer.md)

---

The easy story about AI is still replacement.

AI will replace journalists. AI will replace junior workers. AI will replace writers, analysts, teachers, designers, programmers, managers, maybe everyone who touches a keyboard. It is a dramatic story, so it travels well. It gives both optimists and pessimists something simple to hold: one side says the machine will do the work for us; the other says the machine will take the work from us.

But today's notes pointed in a different direction.

The more interesting story is not that AI replaces the old endpoint. It is that AI appears in the middle of a process, between the human and the complexity, and changes the shape of the whole interaction.

That sounds less exciting than replacement.

It is also closer to what is actually happening.

In the Reuters Institute's 2026 Digital News Report, weekly use of standalone AI chatbots for news rose globally from 7% to 10%. That is real growth, but it is not a takeover. Only 1% of respondents said AI was their main source of news.[^1] If you only look for replacement, the conclusion is easy: AI news consumption is still small.

But look at what people do after they enter the chatbot.

Across 45 markets, the most common use was not simply "show me the news." It was asking follow-up questions, reported by 42% of users. Others used chatbots to summarise complicated stories, make news easier to understand, compile stories from different outlets, translate news, or assess the reliability of a source.[^1]

That is not the behaviour of a person switching from one newspaper to another.

That is the behaviour of a person putting a thinking layer between themselves and the information world.

The same pattern appears in work. PwC's 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer analysed more than one billion job ads across six continents. The report found that the most AI-exposed companies had faster headcount growth than the least exposed ones, 52% versus 36%, and higher wage growth, 24% versus 17%.[^2] Again, this does not fit the simple replacement story. The companies closest to AI are not merely shrinking into machines. Many are growing faster, while asking humans to bring different skills into the work.

So maybe the real question is not:

"What will AI replace?"

Maybe the better question is:

"Where does AI become the middle layer?"

## The middle layer changes the job

A middle layer is not a passive tool.

A dictionary is a tool. A calculator is a tool. A search box is a tool. You put something in, you get something out, and the task remains mostly the same.

AI is different because it changes the distance between intention and action.

Before, if you wanted to understand a complicated topic, you needed to search, open links, read several sources, compare claims, translate unfamiliar terms, write notes, and hold the whole picture in your head. Many people gave up somewhere in the middle, not because they lacked intelligence, but because the workflow was too heavy.

Now the interaction can become a loop:

Ask. Clarify. Challenge. Compare. Translate. Summarise. Ask again. Check the source. Change the frame. Turn the mess into a structure.

This does not remove thinking.

It relocates thinking.

The human is no longer only consuming information. The human is steering the information. The work moves from "Can I find something?" to "Can I interrogate it well?" From "Can I read everything?" to "Can I ask the next better question?" From "Can I produce an answer?" to "Can I build a process that makes weak answers visible?"

That is why the replacement frame misses so much.

When a new middle layer appears, the old visible task may look smaller, but the total system becomes more demanding. A person who uses AI well does not merely receive a summary. They learn to specify what kind of summary matters, which assumptions should be exposed, which sources should be distrusted, where the answer is too smooth, and when a missing detail changes the conclusion.

In other words, AI removes some labour and creates a higher-order labour above it.

That higher-order labour is not optional.

It is the work.

## The danger is not laziness. It is shallow command

People often say AI will make humans lazy. I think that is true in a small way, but false in the more important way.

The larger danger is not that people stop doing work.

The larger danger is that people keep giving commands without building judgment.

This is a quiet failure. It does not look like laziness. It can look very productive. The person is generating documents, reading summaries, creating plans, producing dashboards, drafting emails, building small apps, and posting more than before.

But if the middle layer is doing the interpretation and the human is only accepting the output, then the person is not becoming stronger. They are becoming a supervisor of fluent fog.

The line is subtle.

Using AI to summarise a complex article can make you stronger if you ask what was omitted, what evidence carries the claim, which terms are doing too much work, and what would change the conclusion.

The same summary can make you weaker if you treat it as a substitute for contact with reality.

Using AI to draft a plan can make you stronger if you force the plan to expose dependencies, risks, owners, verification steps, and failure paths.

The same plan can make you weaker if you mistake completeness of format for completeness of thinking.

Using AI to learn a new field can make you stronger if you build a map, test yourself, return to original sources, and turn concepts into action.

The same learning can make you weaker if you collect explanations like souvenirs.

The machine is not the deciding variable.

The operating posture is.

## The "zero class" is not anti-machine

I keep coming back to an idea I care about: the "zero class."

It does not mean people who reject AI. That would be nostalgia disguised as virtue. It also does not mean people who use the newest tools first. Tool access is cheap, and getting cheaper.

The zero class is made of people who use AI without surrendering their agency.

They do not compete with the machine at tasks the machine can already do better. They also do not hand over the whole process and call the result leverage. They stand in the middle layer and keep asking: What is the actual problem? What is the right unit of work? What should be automated? What should stay manual? What has to be verified? What kind of human taste, care, courage, or patience is still required?

This is why the Reuters and PwC data belong in the same conversation.

In news, people are not only using AI to get headlines. They are using it to ask, simplify, compare, translate, and evaluate. In work, companies closest to AI are not simply removing humans. They are changing what they need from humans.

Both point to the same shift:

Value is moving from isolated execution to orchestration.

Not orchestration in the vague corporate sense. I mean the concrete ability to connect sources, tools, constraints, people, and goals into a working loop. The person who can do this is not less active because AI is present. They are more active at a higher level.

The old internet rewarded people who could find information.

The AI internet will reward people who can structure interaction.

## A good middle layer has friction

Here is the uncomfortable part: a good middle layer should not feel perfectly smooth.

If AI only makes the world feel easier, it may be hiding the places where you should be thinking. A useful middle layer asks back. It shows uncertainty. It points to sources. It separates fact from inference. It says, "This part is probably stale." It says, "This number needs verification." It says, "Your question assumes the wrong frame." It says, "You are asking for output, but the real bottleneck is selection."

This is why engineering matters.

Not engineering only in the software sense, but engineering as a way of living with complexity: define the input, constrain the output, preserve provenance, create checkpoints, test the result, make failure visible, and improve the loop.

Without that, AI becomes a beautiful blur.

With it, AI becomes a personal and organizational exoskeleton.

That word matters. An exoskeleton does not replace the body. It changes what the body can carry. It also requires posture. If you wear it badly, you can injure yourself faster than before. If you use it well, the boundary of your ability moves.

This is the difference between using AI to avoid thinking and using AI to make thinking heavier, clearer, and more consequential.

## The future is not less human

The simplest AI advice is still: learn to prompt better.

That is fine, but too small.

The deeper skill is learning to design the relationship between yourself, the machine, and the world. Where should the machine search? Where should it summarise? Where should it argue? Where should it stop? Where must you return to the source? Where must your own experience overrule the clean answer? Where are you using AI as a shortcut, and where are you using it as a training partner?

The people who answer these questions well will not necessarily look like the loudest AI users.

They may be quieter. More skeptical. More precise. Less impressed by demos. More interested in workflows than screenshots. More willing to make the machine slow down and show its work.

That is not resistance to the future.

That is how you stay awake inside it.

AI will replace some tasks. It will create some tasks. It will damage some institutions and strengthen others. The details will differ by field, country, company, and person.

But across the noise, one pattern is already visible:

The middle layer is becoming the battlefield.

Whoever owns that layer owns the question, the frame, the verification path, and eventually the action.

So do not only ask whether AI will do your work.

Ask whether AI is teaching you to command the middle layer.

If it is, you are not becoming lazy. You are becoming stronger.

If it is not, the machine may still make you faster.

But faster is not the same as more alive.
