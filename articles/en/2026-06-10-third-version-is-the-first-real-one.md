# The Third Version Is the First Real One

> 发布日期:2026-06-10 · [中文](../zh/2026-06-10-第三版才是真正的第一版.md) | [English](../en/2026-06-10-third-version-is-the-first-real-one.md)

---

The most dangerous thing AI gives us is not laziness. It is the illusion that the first version counts.

Before AI, the first version was expensive. You had to think, write, design, code, revise, ask for help, get stuck, and pay for the privilege of seeing your own confusion. The cost was annoying, but it had one hidden benefit: it forced you to notice that making something real is different from imagining it.

Now the first version is cheap.

A draft appears in seconds. A prototype appears in minutes. A landing page, a script, a lesson plan, a slide deck, a product mockup, a small app, a marketing outline: all can be summoned before the old version of you would have finished making tea.

That is powerful. I use it every day.

But it also changes the psychological danger. When creation becomes fast, completion starts to look like creation. We see a thing on the screen and feel the old satisfaction of having built it. The artifact exists, so the mind relaxes. We forget that the first version is often not a product, not a system, and not even a real opinion.

It is only a question made visible.

## Why Version One Lies

Tony Fadell recently described a simple rule on Lenny's Podcast: every new product needs three generations. The first generation makes the product. The second fixes the product. The third fixes the business. That line stayed with me because it is not only about hardware. It is about learning.

Apple's iPod is a useful example because its early history looks obvious only in hindsight. Apple introduced the original iPod on October 23, 2001, as a music player that could hold 1,000 songs in your pocket. But that first version was tightly tied to the Mac. In July 2002, Apple announced new iPods and said a Windows version would arrive in late August. In April 2003, Apple launched the iTunes Music Store with more than 200,000 songs. A week later, Apple said the store had sold more than one million songs.

If you compress the story, you get a myth: brilliant company invents perfect device, world changes.

If you slow the story down, you see something more useful: the idea had to become a system. The device was not enough. The compatible market mattered. The music supply mattered. The purchasing experience mattered. The business model mattered. The cultural story mattered.

The first iPod was not wrong. It was incomplete.

This is the trap with most first versions. They are not useless, but they are dishonest if treated as final evidence. A first version mostly proves that something can be made. It does not prove that people want it, that you understand the problem, that the workflow survives contact with reality, or that the economics make sense.

Version one answers the weakest question: "Can this exist?"

The stronger questions arrive later.

Can it be used repeatedly? Can it survive edge cases? Can it create value without your constant explanation? Can the user understand it without borrowing your brain? Can it become a habit, a business, a practice, a system?

That is why the third version matters. By then, the work has absorbed resistance from the world.

## AI Makes Version One Too Easy

AI does not remove this pattern. It intensifies it.

When the cost of version one collapses, we produce more version ones. That sounds like progress, and often it is. More attempts mean more exploration. More prototypes mean more chances to stumble into something real.

But there is a second-order effect: we may become addicted to beginnings.

Beginnings are emotionally generous. The idea is still clean. Nobody has misunderstood it yet. No user has complained. No bug has embarrassed us. No distribution problem has exposed the weakness of our assumptions. No long-term maintenance has shown the shape of the debt.

AI makes beginnings feel even better because it decorates them quickly. A weak idea can now wear a polished interface. A shallow argument can be dressed in fluent paragraphs. A fragile script can look like a working tool because it passed one happy-path demo.

This is what I call the version-one trap.

The version-one trap is not that AI gives bad output. Sometimes it gives excellent output. The trap is that the output arrives before the learning has happened.

Learning needs friction. Not pointless friction, but real feedback: the uncomfortable moment when your plan meets a person, a file, a market, a classroom, a workflow, a deadline, or your own future self. The first version is allowed to be fast. The second version must listen. The third version must remember.

If version one is expression, version three is digestion.

## Building Is Not the Same as Becoming

This matters beyond products.

A person's learning process also has three versions.

Version one is exposure. You read the book, watch the lecture, try the tool, copy the method, follow the tutorial. This stage is necessary, but it is also the easiest to fake. You can collect terms and still have no usable ability.

Version two is correction. You try to apply the thing and discover that your understanding was decorative. The method breaks in your hands. You notice missing steps. You ask better questions. You stop worshiping the surface and begin to understand the structure.

Version three is integration. The thing becomes part of your system. You no longer need to perform "learning" as a special activity. It changes how you see, decide, write, teach, build, or practice. It has entered your judgment.

Most people quit between version one and version two because version two feels worse. Version one gives identity: "I am learning AI," "I am building a product," "I am writing," "I am studying." Version two takes identity away and replaces it with evidence: this part does not work; you skipped this assumption; your example is too convenient; your audience did not understand; your workflow depends on memory; your system is not a system yet.

That is why version two feels like failure.

But version two is usually the first honest stage.

The goal is not to worship difficulty. Some difficulty is waste. Bad tools, unclear instructions, broken environments, and artificial gatekeeping do not make us noble. They merely consume life.

The point is different: when AI removes low-value friction, we must not also remove high-value feedback. The time saved from producing a first draft should be reinvested into testing, revising, connecting, and turning the work into a system.

If AI saves you five hours, the question is not how to avoid work for five hours. The question is what kind of work previously had no room.

## The Human Job Moves Downstream

This is why "human in the loop" is too weak as a slogan.

Being in the loop cannot mean occasionally clicking approve. It cannot mean asking AI for output and then giving it a quick feeling-based pass. That is not judgment. That is a rubber stamp with anxiety.

The more powerful position is human across the loop.

At the beginning, the human frames the real problem. In the middle, the human notices where the machine is fluent but shallow. At the end, the human decides what the work should become after it has met reality.

AI can accelerate expression, but it cannot automatically complete the moral and practical work of commitment. It does not know what you are willing to maintain. It does not know which tradeoffs fit your life. It does not know which audience you want to serve for years. It does not know which pain is worth returning to after the excitement is gone.

Those are not prompt problems. They are ownership problems.

A first version can be generated. Ownership cannot.

Google's 2014 acquisition of Nest for $3.2 billion is often remembered as a smart-home milestone. But the deeper lesson is that hardware, software, data, distribution, trust, and domestic habits all had to meet in one place. A thermostat is not impressive because it has an algorithm. It is impressive only if it fits the home, the wall, the energy bill, the family routine, the installer, the retailer, and the support system.

Real products are not features. They are negotiated relationships with reality.

So are real skills.

## The Third Version Mindset

The practical question is simple: how do we avoid being fooled by version one?

I think we need a third-version mindset.

When you build something with AI, do not ask only, "Did it finish?" Ask three questions.

First: what did version one reveal? Not what did it prove, but what did it reveal. A first draft reveals vocabulary. A prototype reveals missing decisions. A lesson plan reveals what you do not understand about the learner. A script reveals which assumptions were hidden in your manual process.

Second: what did version two correct? Did you show it to a real person? Did you run it on messy data? Did you test the edge case? Did you remove the part that only made sense in your head? Did you rewrite the argument after finding the strongest objection?

Third: what did version three integrate? Did the work become easier to repeat? Did it leave behind a checklist, a template, a tool, a habit, a clearer concept, a better taste? Did it make the next attempt stronger?

If not, you may have produced output without producing progress.

This distinction matters because AI will make output abundant. The world will not lack drafts, demos, posts, apps, lesson plans, summaries, or strategies. The scarce thing will be people who can stay with something long enough for it to become real.

Version one is speed.

Version two is humility.

Version three is compounding.

The first version asks the world, "Is this anything?"

The second version listens when the world says, "Not yet."

The third version answers, "Now I understand what I was actually building."

That is the part AI cannot skip for us.

And maybe it should not. Because the third version is where the work stops being a trick of production and starts becoming a trace of who we are becoming.
