# You Do Not Upgrade All at Once

> 发布日期:2026-07-13 · [中文](../zh/2026-07-13-你不是一次性升级的.md) | [English](../en/2026-07-13-you-do-not-upgrade-all-at-once.md)

---

Today's notes circled around a tempting idea:

people can restart.

That sentence is useful, but also dangerous. It is useful because many people are trapped by the story that the current self is the final self. It is dangerous because "restart" sounds too clean. It makes change feel like closing one app and opening another. Old identity out. New identity in. A bright line between before and after.

Real change is rarely that elegant.

The body gives us a better metaphor. A 2021 paper in *Nature Medicine* estimated that the human body turns over about 330 billion cells per day, with a total cellular mass turnover of about 80 grams per day. Most of that cell-number turnover comes from blood cells, with gut epithelial cells also contributing heavily.[^1]

That sounds like a biological miracle. It also corrects the popular myth that the whole person simply becomes "new" every seven years.

The body does not upgrade all at once.

Some parts change quickly. Some parts persist for a long time. Some systems renew constantly so the whole organism can keep functioning. The continuity is not a failure of renewal. It is the condition that makes renewal possible.

That is exactly how a person changes in the AI age.

## Identity Is a Live System

The shallow version of AI transformation says:

I used to be a consumer. Now I am a builder.

I used to be non-technical. Now I can make software.

I used to ask for answers. Now I command agents.

There is truth in that. AI has lowered the cost of crossing old boundaries. A person who could not code can now build a small internal tool. A person who could not design can now produce a usable interface. A person who used to wait for experts can now create the first draft of a workflow, a lesson, a script, a dashboard, or a service.

But a new tool does not instantly create a new person.

The old loops remain.

You may have a model that can write code, but still think like someone waiting for permission. You may have an agent that can research, but still avoid publishing. You may have a powerful writing assistant, but still outsource your first uncertain thought. You may have automation everywhere, but still use it to protect the same old fear: the fear of being seen before you are ready.

That is why identity cannot be treated like a label.

Identity is a live system. It includes what you notice, what you avoid, what you repeat, what you measure, what you keep private, what you share, what you call "too hard," and what kind of evidence you allow to change you.

If the system stays the same, the label becomes theater.

AI-native. Builder. Creator. Founder. Lifelong learner. These words are useful only if they point to changed behavior. Otherwise they become costumes for the old self.

## The Old Self Is Not the Enemy

The mistake is to imagine that transformation requires destroying the old self.

That makes for dramatic writing. It makes for bad engineering.

When a real system is migrated, you do not simply burn down the old one while traffic is still flowing. You find the parts that must keep running. You isolate the risky pieces. You move one service, one table, one workflow, one integration, one contract at a time. You test. You roll back when needed. You preserve the state that matters.

A person also has to migrate under load.

You still have obligations. You still have habits. You still have emotional defaults. You still have old strengths that were built before AI arrived: patience, taste, memory, language, empathy, domain experience, attention to detail, a sense of what people actually need. Those are not obsolete. They are the substrate.

The old self is not garbage.

It is the data center you are migrating from.

The question is not how to erase it. The question is which parts should be preserved, which parts should be retired, and which parts should be wrapped in a better interface.

This matters because many people use AI to imitate a new identity before installing the practices that would sustain it. They generate impressive outputs, but the old operating system still decides what happens next.

They build a demo, then do not show it.

They draft a plan, then do not test it.

They save a note, then never retrieve it.

They ask for advice, then avoid the small uncomfortable action that would make the advice real.

Nothing is wrong with the model in that story. The migration simply did not happen.

## Practice Rewrites More Than Belief

There is a comforting belief that if we think differently, we will act differently.

Sometimes that is true. But often the direction runs the other way: repeated action gives belief something to attach to.

Research on training-induced neuroplasticity has shown that adult brains can change structurally after learning. In one well-known line of work, people who learned to juggle showed temporary gray-matter changes in areas involved in processing complex visual motion.[^2] The lesson is not that juggling is magic. The lesson is that practice is not merely a performance of change. Practice is one of the ways change becomes physical.

Behavior and identity also reinforce one another. A review in *Frontiers in Psychology* argues that linking habits to identity may help sustain newly formed behaviors, especially when those behaviors connect to central values and goals.[^3]

In plain language:

you do not become a builder by announcing that you are a builder.

You become a builder when building becomes one of the ways you recognize yourself.

That recognition needs evidence.

The evidence can be small. A tool that saves ten minutes. A published note. A fixed bug. A conversation turned into a clearer offer. A repeated workflow turned into a checklist. A private confusion turned into a public explanation. A rough prototype placed in front of one real person.

The size is less important than the loop.

Identity changes when the loop repeats:

notice a problem;
use AI to lower the cost of acting;
ship a small artifact;
let reality respond;
record what changed;
make the next action slightly less imaginary.

That is not motivational language. It is migration work.

## AI Can Make the Migration Easier or Fake It

AI helps most when it makes the next unit of practice possible.

It can explain the missing concept, draft the first ugly version, generate tests, produce variants, summarize feedback, compare options, inspect assumptions, and help turn one success into a reusable process.

Used that way, AI becomes scaffolding.

It does not replace your growth. It lets growth reach places that used to be blocked by lack of time, lack of technical access, lack of examples, or lack of confidence.

But AI can also fake the migration.

It can give you the language of the new identity before the behavior exists. It can produce a polished article before you know what you think. It can make a product demo before you know the customer's pain. It can generate a learning plan before you have survived one boring repetition. It can simulate confidence so well that you forget confidence is supposed to be backed by contact with reality.

This is the new danger:

AI can make a person look updated while the core system remains untouched.

That is worse than slow change, because it creates false evidence. You feel transformed because the output changed. But the output may have changed only on the surface. The decision loop, feedback loop, courage loop, memory loop, and responsibility loop may still be the old ones.

The test is simple:

What is easier for your future self because you used AI today?

If the answer is only "I have a finished output," the value may still be real, but it probably did not compound.

If the answer is "I now have a reusable prompt, a clearer checklist, a working script, a corrected assumption, a smaller next test, a visible standard, or a habit I can repeat," then something migrated.

## The Zeroth Class Migrates Loops

I keep returning to the phrase "the zeroth class."

It names people who use AI to become stronger rather than more dependent.

Here is another definition:

the zeroth class does not chase a new identity. It migrates loops.

A consumer loop becomes a production loop.

Instead of only reading about a tool, you use it to make one small thing.

A private-thinking loop becomes a publishing loop.

Instead of polishing thought forever, you let one clear idea meet readers.

A vague-learning loop becomes a practice loop.

Instead of collecting explanations, you create retrieval, application, correction, and spacing.

A service loop becomes a product loop.

Instead of repeating the same judgment manually, you turn part of it into a template, a diagnostic, a script, or a workflow.

That is the real meaning of "AI makes people stronger." Not that the person becomes magical overnight. Not that the model replaces years of taste. Not that every old limit disappears.

The stronger claim is more practical:

AI lets a person migrate one important loop earlier than before.

Earlier matters.

If you can migrate one loop this week, another next week, another next month, then change stops depending on a grand reinvention narrative. It becomes operational. It becomes inspectable. It becomes boring enough to repeat.

And boring enough to repeat is often where transformation finally becomes real.

## Do Not Rush the Whole Person

There is a kindness hidden in this view.

You do not have to become a completely new person by Tuesday.

You do not have to despise the older version of yourself who learned slowly, worked manually, needed permission, avoided publishing, or did not know how to build.

That version carried you here.

Now the work is to give that version better instruments.

Some parts of you should change quickly. Some parts should change slowly. Some parts should not change at all, because they are the reason the new tools will have direction.

The body renews itself without breaking continuity.

A person can do the same.

The question is not, "Who can I pretend to be now that AI exists?"

The better question is, "Which loop can I migrate today?"

Migrate one loop, and the old self gains a new movement.

Migrate enough loops, and one day the label catches up.

You did not upgrade all at once.

You stayed alive while becoming harder to recognize.
